The days passed and slowly her body became stronger.

She had moved beyond therapy and into routine: free weights in the morning, jogging the bunker corridors in the afternoon, four miles measured in fluorescent laps and the echo of her own breathing. Most of it was done underground. Rarely did she venture topside, and never alone.

The moments to herself were few. But when they came, they were full of the memories of her former self, or the woman whose self she carried. The distinction blurred more with each passing day, like a photograph left in the sun. The edges softened. The colors bled.

ROGER had been helping with her physical therapy, training her to recognize the muscle groups that interfaced with the device. The neural pathways from her brain still reached for fingers that no longer existed, and it was this phantom architecture that the weapon had been built upon.

"Imagine making a fist," he had told her, his lavender eyes steady and clinical. "Close your hand. Feel the tendons pull, the knuckles lock. That impulse will ignite the charging belt. And when you are ready to fire, imagine opening your hand."

A fist to charge. An open palm to release.

She practiced the motion a hundred times a day, her brain sending signals down nerves that terminated in alien circuitry instead of bone. Some days it felt natural. Most days it felt like screaming into a language she hadn't learned yet.

Sarah had just finished breakfast in her room.

She had remained sequestered from the rest of the bunker's population since her surgery, confined to a private wing accessible only to those with special clearance. An inner sanctum. Po had decided it was necessary, for her protection, and for theirs. The people in the bunker had accepted many strange things in the years since the world ended. An alien weapon fused to a woman's arm might be one strange thing too many.

She headed to her training facility: a section of the shelter that had been converted into something between a gymnasium and an armory. Weights. Boxing bags. Mock enemies fashioned from sandbags and scrap metal, their silhouettes vaguely humanoid. A secure space where she could train without an audience, where the sounds of charged particles and crackling discharge wouldn't send a thousand frightened people scrambling for cover.

She paused at the door.

Inside, ROGER and Wax Griffin stood over one of the salvaged robots, the primitive automated units that had been found in the bunker when they first discovered it years ago. Wax had been trying to restore them for months. Sarah stood quietly and listened.

"The nanoparticles that make up my epidermis contain biomimetic regenerative polymers that are also photoreactive," ROGER was explaining, his voice carrying the precise cadence of a textbook read aloud by someone who didn't know it was boring. "They perform dual functions: recharging and regenerating my body's systems and core processing units on a molecular level. This means my body can both heal itself and charge itself when light energizes the chemical reactions of the polymers."

He placed a hand on the dormant robot's chassis the way a surgeon might touch a patient who was beyond saving.

"I will do my best to help you with these primitive versions. But absent some form of sustained electrical energy, they will not last long on battery power alone. They will need constant recharging. In the field, that makes them virtually useless." He paused. "I'm sorry to be the bearer of bad news."

He placed his hand on Wax's shoulder. The gesture was consoling, and almost human. Almost.

"The alien technology that is now a part of Sarah's body is similar to mine, in that it also recharges without the need of a battery. From what I can determine, the conducting sphere and belt create an electrostatic charge which is magnetically propelled through the barrel and exits through the muzzle." He turned his hand over, palm up, as if holding something invisible. "Similar to a bolt of lightning. Albeit a directed bolt of lightning, and one that can be controlled."

"Eavesdropping?"

Sarah spun. Jayk stood behind her in the corridor, his braided hair hanging over both shoulders, that crooked half-smile pulling at his lips.

"Oh! You startled me! Jayk, don't do that; I could have blasted you to smithereens!" She held up the weapon arm, its surface catching the fluorescent light.

"I highly doubt that. You don't even know how to use that thing." He leaned against the doorframe. "I figured I'd find you here, though."

She narrowed her eyes. Waited.

"Speaking of not knowing how to use that thing," he continued, "how about a little weapons training? We could go get some fresh air."

The words hit her like cold water. Fresh air. Outside. The real sky, not the bunker ceiling she had been staring at for two months.

She brushed the hair from her eyes and smirked. "Only if you think you're up for it. I'll get my coat."

Jayk watched her disappear down the corridor and felt something shift in his chest.

She moved differently now. Two months of recovery had changed her. When they had first brought her in from the slave camp she had been a wreck, broken, feverish, barely conscious. The surgery had nearly killed her twice. But the woman who returned from all of that was not the woman who had gone in.

Her long hair fell in tawny ringlets. Her skin was pale, almost luminous in the bunker light. But she still had the unseasoned look of a newborn colt: all legs and restless energy, not quite sure of the body she was in. She was stronger now, harder, and there was something behind her eyes that hadn't been there before. A sharpness. A resolve. Or maybe just the particular stubbornness of someone who had died on the table and decided she wasn't finished.

She came back wearing the bulky overcoat that concealed her left arm. The coat was too large for her, hanging past her hips, and she held it closed with her right hand. Beneath it, the weapon hummed at a frequency just below hearing, a vibration Jayk could feel in his teeth when she stood close.

They maneuvered through the bunker's corridors and stairwells, past the checkpoints, past the soldiers who saluted and looked away, and emerged topside.

The desert opened before them.

The air was dry and thin and tasted of dust and distance. The sky stretched from horizon to horizon without a single cloud, a pale dome bleached by years of atmospheric damage. Somewhere to the south, the alien gardens pressed their boundary closer, but here there was nothing. Just sand and scrub and the bones of a world that had forgotten how to grow.

Sarah ran her hand across the cold metallic surface of the device. Her fingers, her real fingers, the ones on her right hand, traced the seams where alien metal met human tissue. Her arm tingled. It felt like power. She could feel the energy pooling in the conducting sphere as she imagined clenching her phantom fist, her brain sending signals down nerve endings that now terminated in circuitry instead of bone.

Jayk had placed targets on a small foothill ahead of their trip: crude dummies fashioned from scrap wood and sandbag cloth, their shapes barely human. They stood on the ridge like scarecrows waiting for a field that would never be planted.

"This should be a good place to practice," Jayk said. "Away from the risk of damaging the shelter, and high enough to see anything approaching. Now, remember what ROGER taught you, just..."

The air split.

A sound like the atmosphere tearing itself in half, a crack so loud it seemed to come from inside her skull. The charge erupted from the muzzle in a blinding arc of white-blue light, ionizing the molecules in its path, turning the air itself into a conductor.

The recoil threw her backward. She hit the ground hard, the breath punched from her lungs, sand spraying in a fan around her.

Silence.

Then Jayk was beside her, his hand on her back, supporting her as she sat up. "Oh, wow, are you okay?"

She wiped a drop of blood from her lip with her good hand. My real arm, she thought. And then, correcting herself: My other arm. Because in this world, the weapon is becoming the good one.

Jayk stifled a laugh. She beamed.

"That was incredible!" The words poured out of her. "It was like... it was like I was plugged into the sun! Like lightning coursed through the veins in my arms and exploded from the tips of my fingers! Wow! Wow! Wow!"

Her eyes were glowing. Not metaphorically; there was a faint residual discharge in her irises, a flicker of electricity that hadn't fully dissipated. She stared at the weapon with the wild, half-terrified, half-ecstatic look of someone who had just discovered they could do something impossible.

There was the slightest hint of burning in the air.

She squared her shoulders for a second attempt.

This time she was ready.

She angled toward the target. Knowing the recoil, she extended her left arm forward and balanced herself with her right arm behind her, planting her feet the way Ideo had taught the recruits: shoulder width apart, knees bent, weight low. She looked down the barrel. Lined up the shot as best she could.

A deep breath. Exhale. Hold.

She imagined her phantom hand making a small fist. The conducting sphere hummed. The belt charged. The air around the muzzle began to shimmer, molecules vibrating at frequencies that made Jayk's hair stand on end.

She opened her hand.

The bolt screamed toward the target, and missed by three feet, scorching a line across the hillside that turned sand to glass.

"Whooo hoo! Ha ha ha!" She giggled like a child. Like someone who had forgotten what joy felt like and had just been reminded.

The electricity had rocketed through her teeth. She cradled her left arm in her right and felt the warmth emanating from the device, a deep, satisfying heat like holding a mug of something hot on a cold morning.

She turned and pointed the weapon at the next dummy. Fired. Missed again, but closer. Fired again. The bolt clipped the target's shoulder, sending splinters spinning into the air.

She was laughing now. Actually laughing. The sound of it surprised them both.

She fired sideways. She fired from the hip. She spun and dropped to her knees and fired from the ground. She was experimenting. Playing. Finding the edges of something she didn't yet understand but could feel in her bones, or whatever passed for bones where the weapon met her body.

She fired over and over and over again.

The experiencing self was overriding the remembering self. The woman with two arms and wrought iron chairs and organic tea was fading. The woman with a cannon for an arm and sand in her teeth and the taste of copper on her tongue was taking over.

For two months she had been caught between two selves: the Sarah who remembered a life that wasn't hers, and the Sarah who was living a life she hadn't chosen. The first Sarah lived in nostalgia, in soft pillows and lazy afternoons. The second Sarah lived in the bunker, in fluorescent light and recovery and the phantom ache of missing fingers.

But out here, firing her weapon into the wasteland, something shifted. A third self was emerging. One who belonged to neither the past nor the bunker. One who was purely, terrifyingly, present.

Eventually she collapsed into the sand, chest heaving, heart hammering, a thin layer of smoke hanging low around them like a veil. Her eyes still flickered with residual discharge. The air smelled of ozone and scorched earth.

She lay there and stared at the sky.

As the sun fell below the mountains they sat atop the small hillside and stared at the arid landscape. The desert stretched in every direction, an expanse of nothing punctuated by the occasional skeleton of a building or the rusted husk of a vehicle half-buried in drifting sand. It was beautiful in the way that emptiness is beautiful: vast, indifferent, without opinion.

After a long silence, she spoke.

"Do you ever wonder what your purpose is?"

Jayk considered the question carefully.

"I never really gave it much thought," he said. "Since I can remember I've been training to fight. My mom and dad raised me in an old museum, one of the few remaining structures that hadn't been destroyed. It was solid and sturdy and had plenty of entertainment. There was a whole community of us living together. Many families shared the building. We were a tight-knit group."

He picked up a handful of sand and let it fall between his fingers.

"I honestly don't know what we thought. Maybe we were hoping the governments would save us. Or the military. But nobody was coming. We eked out an existence hunting and trapping animals until none were left. There were days I went hungry. But I always had my friends. We pretended to fight, with swords and knives and bats, whatever weapons we could make or steal."

His voice dropped.

"I guess, if I had to claim a purpose, it would have been after they killed my parents. And my friends. Somehow I escaped. I don't even remember how." He paused. Stared at the horizon. "Isn't that weird? The most important moment of my life, and I can't remember it."

Another pause. Longer.

"Anyway. Ever since then I just ... I just keep going. I don't know what it is. Something won't let me stop. I just want to make them pay for what they took from me. I guess that's my purpose."

Sarah absorbed his words. He had described a world of pain and misery, of difficulty she could only imagine and nothing she would want to endure. Her mind drifted to the other Sarah's childhood, how different life had been before the world changed.

She stared across the expanse of desert and spoke, not quite to Jayk, not quite to herself.

"The world is very different from the one she grew up in. The roads were green and lush. The trees hadn't been choked by poisonous gasses. The water was safe to drink and the animals roamed freely."

Her voice softened. Her eyes went somewhere Jayk couldn't follow.

"I can remember sitting in wrought iron chairs, rusty and uncomfortable, sipping organic black tea with orange peel. Watching the clouds hide from the sun on lazy days. Hearing the rhythm drift down from the upstairs apartment. Smooth drum and bass beats. Cold soft pillows stuffed with goose feathers pressing against my warm skin."

She pulled her coat tighter.

"And dreaming. I remember dreaming. Not what they were about, but the fact that it happened. I don't dream anymore."

She turned to look at him.

"I hate this new world, Jayk. I don't want to do this anymore." Her voice cracked on the last word. She looked out at the barren landscape, the poisoned sky, the dead earth, the absence of everything green and growing. "There's nothing here but death."

The anger came without warning. She felt the charged particles in the air responding to her emotion, coalescing in the barrel of the device. Her muscles tightened. Her phantom hand clenched.

She released.

The bolt of lightning erupted upward, not at a target, not at an enemy, but straight into the sky. It crackled heavenward in a blinding column of white-blue light, splitting the darkness, exiting the atmosphere itself. The sound was deafening. The ground shook.

It was the most powerful thing Jayk had ever seen.

Sarah dropped to her knees. Folded over. The afterglow flickered and pulsed, wrapping the horizon in a warm coronal discharge.

"I miss my life," she said quietly. "But it was never my life in the first place. They're just memories. The memories of her life." She pressed her palm against the sand. "But I miss it nonetheless. I miss dressing up and going out on Friday nights. Dancing and singing. Shopping in department stores, buying nice things."

She looked down at herself. The overcoat. The mismatched layers beneath it.

"And these clothes, a mishmash of overcoats and sweaters, heavy cottons, leather and tweed. Patchwork trousers sewn together with fishing line and cord. Scarves and fingerless gloves, hoods and goggles. There's no style to any of it. It's so..." She searched for the word. "Utilitarian. I'll never get used to it."

Jayk touched her shoulder.

"We'd better head back." His voice was gentle but urgent. "You just lit up the sky like I've never seen before. I've been fighting these things for years and I've never seen a blast like that."

He helped her to her feet.

"Perhaps you are our secret weapon, Sarah." He said it with a crooked smile. "That sort of gives me hope."

She followed him down from the hillside. He contemplated her world as they walked, how beautifully she had described it. The only green Jayk could remember was among the sagebrush and wildflowers that had adapted to the warm climates and dry air, the hardy survivors of a planet that was mostly desert now. He tried to imagine the world she carried in her borrowed memories: vehicles like the ones left to rust in the wastes, but moving, hovering above the ground. People rushing through busy lives, unbothered by the sky.

He couldn't imagine what it was like for her. Having to accept that she was a copy of another human being. A counterfeit life built on someone else's memories. What did that do to a person? How did you build a self on a foundation that belonged to someone else?

But she was strong. Resilient. Already so different from the broken woman they had pulled from the slave camp. There was a self-reliant air about her now, an otherworldly quality that separated her from everyone else in the bunker, as if she had lived every one of her two hundred and twenty years in a few short months.

"I'm really glad you came out here with me, Jayk." She fell into step beside him. "It's good to have someone to talk to. Sometimes I feel like I've known you my whole life."

Jayk didn't know what to say. So he just stood there, silently, wanting to touch her, wanting to fold her into his arms and hold her, but just standing there. Dumbly. His hands at his sides like they'd forgotten their purpose.

She looked at him and smiled, awkward, a little crooked, unsure.

Then she reached up with her right arm, slung it around his neck, and pulled him close.

Jayk threw one arm around her and they held each other in a tight embrace. It lasted a long time, and not long enough. She smelled like sweat and burning hair. It was sickly sweet, and he loved it.

She ran her fingers through her filthy hair and grinned sheepishly.

Something was shifting. She could feel it, the balance tipping between her two selves. The remembering self was loosening its grip. The experiencing self was reaching for the controls.

I'm not my memories. I'm me.

The thought was small and new and fragile, like the first green shoot pushing through scorched earth.

I'm a part of everything happening around me right now. I shouldn't mourn a life that was never mine. I should join this one. Live in the moment. Only then will I truly be free instead of a slave to the past.

Her lips curled into a sly grin. Her eyes darted back and forth in brief saccades as she weighed the thought, tested it, decided whether it could bear her weight.

It could.

The moon was rising behind the mountains as they walked toward the shelter. They were a few meters from the entrance when Jayk stopped. Beneath the wind, almost too low to be a sound at all, a faint droning hum.

"Do you hear that?"

Sarah froze.

She heard it. A low hum. A vibration in the air that was not wind, not an echo, not anything that belonged to this desert. Jayk's body had changed; his posture rigid, his eyes scanning the darkening sky with the predatory focus of someone who had spent his whole life listening for exactly this sound.

"Get down. We need to find cover." His voice was flat. Controlled. All the warmth gone from it. "There, under those bushes. Hurry. It's getting closer."

They crawled beneath a crop of sagebrush, pressing their bodies flat against the earth, faces in the sand. Sarah could feel the ground vibrating beneath her.

The airship crested the ridge in virtual silence.

It did not roar. It did not rumble. It simply appeared, gliding over the landscape with a terrible, effortless grace, as though the air parted for it willingly. It hovered above them, close, impossibly close, and the underside of the vessel filled their entire field of vision. Dark metal. Monolithic construction. No rivets, no seams, no visible engine. Just a smooth, organic hull that seemed less built than grown.

They lay in its shadow and did not breathe.

Inside the vessel, two of the creatures spoke to a third through a communication device. Their language was not sound as humans understood it; it was vibration, frequency, a modulation of electromagnetic pulses that registered somewhere between hearing and feeling:

Those are the correct coordinates.

There is nothing here. All we observe is empty desert. Confirm the coordinates.

Confirmed. Those are the exact coordinates of the device. It has not moved.

Then there is a discrepancy. Because there is nothing at this location.

A pause. Something shifted in the hum of the ship.

Wait. Detecting biological signatures.

Sarah's blood went cold. She was certain, absolutely certain, that her stunt with the weapon, the bolt she had fired into the sky, had brought them here. The guilt hit her like a physical blow. She had lit up the atmosphere like a flare and now the creatures had come looking.

They began to crawl. Slowly. Inches at a time. Toward the hidden entrance to the bunker.

The signatures are moving.

Moving? What are they?

Difficult to determine. Possibly fauna of some kind.

They reached the entrance. Slipped inside. The darkness of the corridor swallowed them.

The heat signatures have disappeared.

The device coordinates have also vanished. The signal is gone.

A long silence.

Return to base.

The airship hung in the air for one more moment, a predator scanning the ground where its prey had been, and then it sailed into the distance, and the desert was quiet again.

They ran. Flat out, full sprint, the bunker walls blurring past them in the dark. Down the corridors. Through the checkpoints. Deeper. Faster. Neither of them spoke. Neither of them needed to. They ran until the bunker swallowed them whole.

Sarah's mind was racing. They had tracked her weapon. The device had coordinates; it was broadcasting a signal, a homing beacon embedded in the alien technology now fused to her body. Every time she fired it, she was telling them where she was.

She filed that thought away. It would need to be discussed with ROGER. Tomorrow.

"How did your first trials with the alien device turn out, Sarah?" ROGER asked when they entered the common room. He was seated at a table, his posture as always rigidly perfect, his diagnostic gaze already registering her elevated heart rate and dilated pupils with clinical precision.

"They were exhilarating, doctor." She composed herself. Stood straighter. "At first I was thrown back by the power of the weapon. But I regained my footing, stood firmly like you suggested, and everything went perfectly."

She kept talking. Jayk heard her voice but not her words. He had drifted somewhere else, somewhere warm and electric, somewhere that smelled like sweat and burning hair. He watched the others sitting around talking and laughing, their voices muted by the sound of his own thoughts, and it almost seemed as if the world would be fine. Or at least, he hoped it would be.

Before, he had been fueled by revenge. A purpose built on anger, on the memory of his parents' faces, on the sound of his friends dying in a place he couldn't remember leaving. That purpose had carried him for years. It had kept him sharp. Kept him moving. Kept him alive.

But now he had found something else. Not a replacement for the anger; the anger was still there, coiled and waiting. But alongside it, growing through it like a vine through cracked stone, was something new.

A reason to live. A reason to build. A reason to imagine a world worth fighting for, not just fighting against.

And that put a smile on his face.
