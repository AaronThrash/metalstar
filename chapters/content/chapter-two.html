John D'Arby hit the floor hard and came up gasping.

He began taking giant panic breaths. His lungs burned. He screamed and the sound bounced back at him from the concrete walls, distorted and strange. He was blind. Completely blind. He stayed on all fours for what felt like hours, trying to force his body to obey commands his brain could barely form.

His training as a Marine had prepared him for sensory deprivation. He knew how to function without sight, how to navigate by sound and touch, how to remain calm when every instinct screamed. But right now his senses weren't just deprived; they were haywire. Sounds arrived without direction. Input without orientation. He couldn't triangulate, couldn't parse, couldn't make his brain do the work it had always done automatically.

*Improvise. Adapt. Overcome.*

He told himself the words like a mantra.

The floor beneath his palms was polished and slick, covered in some viscous solution that smelled of copper and chemicals. Somewhere nearby, something had shattered. Alarms wailed, overlapping, dissonant, and beneath them, impossibly, he could hear symphony music. Strings. Horns. A composition he couldn't name, playing to an audience of no one.

He tried to remember how he'd gotten here. There had been an experiment. Military. Classified. Something called the Proto... the proto-platypus... protometheus...

*No.* The noise was splintering his thoughts. He couldn't hold a coherent idea for more than a few seconds.

He began to crawl, searching for walls, for edges, for anything that might orient him. The darkness was absolute, he couldn't see his hand in front of his face. *There has to be a switch*, he thought. *A door. Something.*

"Hello?!" His voice rang out and died in the emptiness.

No answer.

"*Hello?!*"

No response. Just the alarms, the music, the sizzle and pop of something burning.

After several minutes of crawling, his hand found a wall. He stood, pressing his palm flat against the surface, and began to follow it. The tactile feedback steadied him. Procedure. Protocol. One step at a time.

Then he realized he was naked.

He covered himself reflexively, one hand on the wall, the other cupped between his legs. *I must look like a complete fool*, he thought. *Then again, who could see me in this darkness?* A small, absurd comfort.

He moved along the wall, counting steps, listening for changes in the acoustic signature of the space around him. Little by little, his other senses began to sharpen. The burning-wire smell separated from the chemical tang. The sizzling distinguished itself from the alarms. His brain was recalibrating, slowly, painfully, learning to process a world it had never actually experienced before.

*Have I been drugged?*

He tried to remember the purpose of the experiment, but the details slipped away like water through his fingers. So he fell back on what he knew. What he could verify.

*My name is John D'Arby. Gunnery Sergeant. United States Marine Corps.*

He repeated it again. And again. An anchor in the static.

*Everything will be fine. This is part of the experiment. A test of my abilities. I just need to find the door, and the others will be waiting for me.*

The wall led him to an opening: a corridor. He followed it, and the alarms began to fade behind him. Without sight, the facility was a labyrinth. Every turn could be wrong. Every step could lead deeper into darkness. But he'd been through readiness exercises before. He'd trained for this. He would get through.

*Someone could be standing right next to me and I'd have no idea.*

The thought made his skin prickle. He reached out, waved his hand through the empty air. Nothing.

He kept moving. Counting. Measuring.

Then the hunger hit.

It came without warning: an intense, hollow ache that seized his entire body. He'd been hungry before, field-rations hungry, three-days-without-food hungry. This was different. This was primal. His body screaming for something it had never been given.

He heard footsteps approaching. Fast.

John stopped. Stood at attention, habit and instinct, the training taking over. He faced the direction of the sound and waited for whoever it was to identify themselves. Rescue team, maybe. Someone who could explain what the hell was going on.

The person reached him. Hands touched his face, gentle, precise, cold.

A voice whispered: "Don't worry. Your sight will come with time. Wait for me here."

Then the hands withdrew, and the footsteps faded, and John D'Arby stood alone in the dark, waiting for orders that might never come.

When Sarah revived, her head felt as if it had been split open end to end.

There was a thumping noise, rhythmic, insistent, that increased in intensity every time she tried to move. It took her a long moment to realize the sound was her own heartbeat, amplified by trauma into something monstrous. She could hear a constant dripping somewhere nearby, and beneath it, the murmuring of small voices crying out in the darkness. Whimpers. Prayers. The discord of the dying.

She still had trouble focusing, but found it easier to hear and smell. The air was thick, fetid, carrying notes of rust and rot and something else, something organic and wrong. She was terrified, but didn't say a word. Whatever was out there would hear her movements, so she remained still and waited, counting the drips like a metronome. *One. Two. Three. Four.*

As she lay there, her visual acuity began to sharpen. She could see that she was in some sort of dark enclosure: a room, large and damp, its walls slick with moisture that caught what little light filtered through cracks in the ceiling. The place reeked of mold and feces and fear. She put her hand to her head and felt a sticky substance. Her hair had become matted with blood, the strands clinging to her fingers like wet rope.

Then she heard it.

A roaring of some kind, distant at first, like the rumble of thunder before a storm. But it grew closer. Heavier. The footfalls shook dust from the ceiling, each impact a percussion she felt in her chest. A large, heavy beast.

The door burst open and it let out a terrible howl, a sound that existed beyond language, beyond reason, a vibration that rattled her teeth and turned her blood to ice. Her immediate reaction was to scream, and so she did.

She began shuffling backward on her feet and hands until she hit the wall behind her. Cold stone against her bare skin. The creature towered over her, its body enormous, blocking the light from the doorway like an eclipse. She could not process what she was seeing, only that it was *wrong*, fundamentally wrong, a shape that should not exist.

It reached toward her with a taloned hand that she scrambled to avoid. Her senses were still unrefined, but her movements were automatic. Instinctive. The ancient mathematics of prey fleeing predator.

She was quickly subdued.

Heavy chain around her neck. The weight of it like a collar of iron, cold against her collarbone. Then she was tossed, *tossed*, like something without value, into another room filled with people who were also bound with chains and shackles.

There she lay, completely nude, while she watched in silence as the others around her drifted in and out of consciousness. Their skin was filthy and leathery, covered with sores that wept in the dim light. They were marked by exhaustion. Their bodies bore wounds that would never heal, not from lack of medicine, but from lack of hope. These were people who had stopped believing rescue would come.

She began to sob uncontrollably.

The sound escaped her in waves, ugly and raw, a melody of grief with no resolution. She curled into a ball on the cold floor and remained there for what felt like hours, her body shaking, her mind refusing to process the reality of her situation.

*This is no kind of life.*

The thought arrived unbidden, sharp as a blade.

Eventually, a withered-looking man shuffled over and knelt beside her. His movements were slow, deliberate, conserving energy, she realized. Everything here was rationed. Even motion. He reached out with bony hands and gently placed a jacket around her shoulders as she shivered.

She uttered a small "Thank you" through damp eyes.

He said nothing. Just nodded once and moved back away, returning to his own corner of the darkness. But that small kindness, the warmth of fabric against her skin, the acknowledgment that she was still human, still worthy of dignity, it broke something open in her chest.

Who were these people? Why were they here? What had happened to the world?
