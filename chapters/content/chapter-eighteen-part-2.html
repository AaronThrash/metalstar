"I've known a lot of charlatans in my life," he said. His voice was quiet, stripped of the bravado and the wit and the performative irreverence that he wore like armor. Without those things, his voice was surprisingly gentle. "People who claim to have special abilities but're just pretending. I've known psychics, fortune tellers and spiritual guides... but they were all tricksters and frauds. They just did it for the money and the fame. They were performers."

He paused. He stared at the floor, at the worn stone beneath his boots, and Rensa could see the muscles in his jaw working, the effort it took to keep the next words from coming out wrong.

"I decided a long time ago dere ain't no such ting as... any o' dat. Visions, prophecies, none of it. If I can't see it or prove it, it don't exist." He looked up at her, and what Rensa saw in his eyes made her breath catch. She had seen Dade angry. She had seen him drunk, afraid, wounded, reckless, and wildly inappropriate. She had never seen him like this. His eyes were pouring out everything he had: all the anguish, all the loss, all the grief he had been carrying in a box labeled do not open since before she had known him.

"But Ren, there's no way she could've known 'bout my sis. I don't care how long she'd been followin' us. It's not possible."

"So what does that mean?" Rensa asked.

Dade swallowed. "The thing is, I'd heard of her. The 'blind prophet' they call her. And I just chalked her up to anotha faker." His hands clenched between his knees. "But Ren, when she mentioned my sis, it was like she spoke directly into me. Like my sis was talkin' to me, tellin' me everything was gonna be ok." His voice broke on the last word. Not dramatically, not with a sob, but with a hairline fracture that let the grief seep out. "There's no way she could've known 'bout my sis, Ren. Because my sister's dead. She's been dead for years. Longer than I've known any of you."

The room was quiet. The lamplight on the walls. The distant hum of the ventilation shafts.

"And it was more than just her bringin' her up," Dade said, and now his voice was steadier, anchored by a conviction that was forming in real time, hardening like metal cooling in a mold. "It was the feelin' in my body. I can't explain it. But she's real. She's the realest thing I've ever felt." He looked at Rensa, and his eyes were clear. "And if she says we're a part of some prophecy, then I say we believe her."

Rensa did not speak. She crossed the room and sat down beside him on the bed and put her hand on his, and let the silence be enough.

Later, when the house had settled into the particular quiet of a place where people are resting but not yet asleep, Po offered his bedroom to Trinidad and Riley. He explained that he would sleep on the couch, and the offer was made with the simple generosity of a man who understood that comfort, in this world, was not a luxury but a gift.

Trinidad accepted graciously. Riley was already half asleep, his small body curled in the chair, his brown eyes heavy-lidded and fighting a battle they were about to lose. She guided him to the bedroom, her hand on his shoulder, navigating by the map of his fading vision and the sound of Po's voice directing them down the short corridor.

When they were alone and Riley had been laid down and covered with a blanket that smelled of cedar and age, Trinidad sat on the edge of the bed and listened. She listened to the boy's breathing as it slowed and deepened. She listened to the muffled sounds of the underground city beyond the walls: the distant clang of metal, the murmur of voices, the low drone of ventilation pushing air through tunnels carved from stone. And beneath all of it, the vast, mineral silence of the earth itself.

She had nearly been killed in Sid Mantelis. Had almost been swept away by a sandstorm in the wastes. Had come close to starving in the long days between the cathedral city and this place. Her body ached with the memory of all it had endured, and her mind ached with the knowledge of all that was yet to come.

Yet none of that compared to what she was about to face. What they would all face.

She reached out through the bond and felt Riley's consciousness, warm, dreaming, far away. Through his closed eyes she saw nothing, and the nothing was a comfort. For a few hours, at least, she could rest in her own darkness.

Trinidad lay back and closed her eyes. The prophecy waited, patient and inexorable, a wheel that had been turning since before she was born and would continue turning long after she was dust. She was just another cog in its interlocking design. They all were: the warrior and the green-eyed woman with the blade and the Marine and the man with the braid and the woman with the weapon arm, and the girl who needs healing and the man who sat in the other room staring at the ceiling thinking about his sister and the little boy who slept beside her who only spoke in pictures.

All of them, turning. All of them, converging.

The fate of the planet was sealed. And somewhere out in the storm, four figures ran through the rain toward the same underground city where a blind woman lay in a borrowed bed, dreaming of a future she could see but could not stop.

The wheel turned.

And the silence held.
