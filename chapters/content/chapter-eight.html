They came in like the tide comes in. Slow. Inevitable. Rank with salt and ruin.

The rescued poured through the bunker entrance in waves, and the stench arrived before they did. A living thing, that smell. Sweat and festering wounds and the sour reek of bodies left too long without water or light. It filled the corridors and settled into the walls and would not leave for days.

John stood at the end of the main hall and watched them file past.

Hollow faces. Sunken eyes. Ribs pressing through skin like the bars of cages they had only just escaped. Some limped. Some were carried. A woman near the back clutched a bundle of rags to her chest that might have been a child, or might have been all she had left in the world. She did not look up.

 

Most had been eating scraps for months, the nutrient-poor slurry the creatures fed their workers to keep them alive and nothing more. When the bunker cooks offered them real food, their stomachs revolted. They retched into buckets, into corners, into their own hands. The medics moved among them with what little they had, but there was no medicine for what starving did to the gut. Only time.

John counted them as they passed. Old habit. A shepherd's habit, or a sergeant's. The number climbed past eight hundred. Past nine. Past a thousand.

Over a thousand souls.

And not one of them ready for war.

He found Wax Griffin in the corridor outside the command room, poring over a hand-drawn map that had been pinned to the wall with salvaged nails. The bunker administrator looked like he hadn't slept in days, which was likely the truth.

"How long?" John asked.

Wax didn't look up. "How long what?"

"Before the gardens swallow what's left. Before there's no earth left to fight for."

Now Wax looked at him. The map behind him showed the known boundaries of alien growth, sketched in red charcoal, and the boundaries were close. Closer than John had expected.

"From the scouting we've done, from the maps we've been able to make?" Wax exhaled. "Two years. Maybe two."

"Two years."

"That's a projection. There's no way to know for sure. We have no idea what the rest of the world looks like." He rubbed his eyes with the heels of his hands. "But if they're as thorough in other places as they are here, we're looking at two years. Tops."

John looked back down the corridor, toward the sound of retching and low moans.

"We have to train an army," Wax said. "And there's not much time to do it."

"How long have we got?"

"A few weeks. Maybe longer. But we can't wait, John. Those gardens are getting closer. Pretty soon there won't be any land left."

John studied the rabble of men and women being ushered toward the showers. Thin as kindling. Weak as wet paper.

"If we had a year, maybe. Hell, I'd take six months."

"We don't have six months." Wax held his gaze. "We need you."

John was quiet for a long time.

Then he nodded once, the way his grandfather used to nod when he accepted something that could not be changed. Not with joy. Not with defeat. With the understanding that the work would be done because it must be done.

"I'll need four of your best," he said. "Po. Ideo. Rensa. Dade. And a room big enough to hold two hundred."

He would chisel two hundred soldiers out of this raw stone.

Of those, twenty would become his personal guard, his tip of the spear, the ones who would go where no one else could follow. They would run missions designed to target and weaken enemy resolve. The battles to come would be fought and won by people like these.

But first, they had to be made.

John gathered the squad leaders in his ready room. They came as they were: Po, broad and still as a man carved from oak; Ideo, compact and watchful, her father's blade riding her hip like it had always been there; Dade, leaning against the doorframe with a half-grin that never quite reached his eyes; Rensa, pacing, always pacing, her mop of twisted locks falling across her face.

John spoke to them standing, because a man does not sit when he asks others to go to war.

"We'll start by dividing everyone into squads. Within that squad they'll do everything together. Eat, shower, piss, fight. They'll learn about each other. Most of all they'll learn to trust each other."

He paused. Let the silence do the work.

"This is what makes an army strong. Not its ability to fight, although that's important. Its ability to fight for one another. As a single unit. A common cause gives an army its strength. If a person has something to fight for, they'll fight harder."

He looked at each of them in turn. Po met his eyes. Ideo met his eyes. Dade tilted his head. Rensa stopped pacing.

"This is what we'll teach them."

They broke the recruits into groups of two hundred and brought them to the recreation room, a gymnasium buried deep in the bunker's guts that they had fitted with jump ropes, free weights, and sandbags fashioned from old canvas and sand hauled in from the surface. The fluorescent lights hummed overhead, casting everything in a flat, pitiless white.

The four squad leaders would demonstrate their methods, one at a time. Each style different. Each brutal in its own way. The recruits would watch, and then be sorted into the training group best suited to their bodies, their temperaments, their capacity for violence.

This was the beginning. This was where the blade met the whetstone.

Po was the first to address them.

He walked to the front of the room with the unhurried stride of a man who has no use for urgency. He did not raise his voice. He did not posture. He simply stood there, hands loose at his sides, and waited until the murmuring died of its own accord.

Many mornings, before the others woke, Po could be found cross-legged in the center of this same room, eyes closed, breathing in a rhythm that seemed to slow time itself. The Hindu mystic. The centered man. His style of fighting was not about fighting at all. It was about being present. About mastering one's chi so completely that combat became a conversation your body already knew the words to.

"First," Po said, "you must learn how to breathe."

He began to inhale, deep and slow, his massive chest expanding.

"How to breathe?" A recruit near the middle of the group interrupted, drawing scattered laughter. "I've been doing that since I was a child. You should be teaching us how to kill!"

Po stopped.

He walked over, calm as still water. "What's your name?"

Before the man could answer, Po punched him in the throat.

The recruit crumpled, choking, hands clawing at his neck. Po stepped over him and continued, his voice unchanged.

"If you cannot breathe, you cannot fight. Let this be your first lesson."

He paused. Looked at the room. Two hundred faces stared back, very still, very quiet.

"Now. Let's begin." He closed his eyes. "Breathe in through the nose. Deep inhale." His chest rose. "And exhale through the mouth."

He breathed.

And slowly, one by one, they breathed with him.

Ideo stepped forward after Po had finished.

She was shorter than most of the recruits and outweighed by all of them, and it did not matter in the slightest. She had learned to wield a blade at her father's knee, and her training began not with the weapon but with what held it up.

"You must learn how to stand."

She scanned the rows, found a young man whose posture told her everything she needed to know, and pointed at him. "You. Come here. Show me how you stand."

He shuffled to the front, uncertain. Stood the way he always stood: feet splayed in different directions, weight shifted to one leg, hips tilted.

"Your stance is off balance. See?"

Ideo shoved him at the hips. He stumbled backward two full steps and nearly fell.

"You see?" She didn't wait for an answer. "Now, stand with both feet pointing toward your opponent. Shoulder width apart."

He adjusted.

"Don't stand with your feet together." She pushed him at the shoulder. He rocked but held. Better.

"Feet shoulder width apart. This distributes your weight and gives you a lower center of gravity. Keep your hips loose. Knees bent. Nimble." She watched his body find the shape she was asking for. "That's better."

She drew her blade. The sound it made leaving the sheath was like a single note plucked from a string, clean and bright.

"Everything begins here." She tapped her foot against the floor. "The blade follows the feet. If the feet are wrong, the blade is useless."

She held the weapon loosely, almost carelessly. But her stance was perfect. And every person in that room understood, without being told, that she could have killed any of them before they knew it was happening.

Dade came next, and the room's energy shifted.

Where Po was stillness and Ideo was precision, Dade was smoke. He moved like a man who had spent his whole life making sure no one could predict where he'd be next.

"Make your enemy look at sometin' else," he said, grinning, "den attack where he ain't lookin'. De greatest weapon you have at your disposal is misdirection. Your enemy is goin' to be watchin' what you're doin', so you have to do de opposite of what he expects."

He grabbed a recruit by the arm and pulled him to the front. "Now. You're me enemy. Fight me."

The recruit tried a punch. Dade blocked it with the kind of casual ease that made the attempt look foolish. Then he rushed forward as if to counter, leaning in with his shoulder, his arm rising just enough to suggest the blow was coming.

The recruit raised his hands to block.

Dade dropped low and swept his legs.

The recruit hit the floor with a sound like a sack of grain dropped from a height. Dade extended his hand and pulled him up.

"What'd he do wrong, den?"

"He got knocked down," someone called from the back.

"Dat's right! But why?"

"He wasn't paying attention." Another voice.

"Exactly. He was watchin' me shoulder. Normally dat'd mean I was gonna punch him. But he failed to see me hips, which showed I was gonna kick. When misdirectin', always get your opponent to look where you want him to look. Not where you're actually goin'."

He clapped the recruit on the back. "Let's try anudder."

Rensa was last.

She did not introduce herself. She did not explain her philosophy. She walked to the front of the room, selected a recruit, and began.

"Use everything you've got at your disposal. If you lose your weapon, use your hands, your feet, your teeth, whatever you've got."

She poked her student in the eyes.

He staggered backward, blind, reaching for his face. "If you can't see, you can't fight." She grabbed his shoulders and drove her knee into his stomach. He doubled over. Dropped. "Once they're down, don't give up. Continue kicking them until they stop moving."

She kicked him. Once. Twice. Three times. Her boot connecting with ribs, with thigh, with the arms he raised to protect himself. She kept going, methodical, relentless, until John crossed the floor in four long strides and pulled her away.

The room was silent.

Rensa brushed a mop of twisted locks from her eyes and smiled at John. "That went well, don't ya think?"

John turned to the recruits. His face betrayed nothing.

"I believe that concludes our training demonstrations for the day. Get some rest. Tomorrow you will be divided into your individual squads, and your formal training will begin."

A long day. Over a thousand souls. The raw material of an army, and the work was only beginning.

The next morning, they were sorted.

The squad leaders broke them into pairs and stood back to watch. What followed would have been comical if the stakes weren't measured in lives. The recruits punched and kicked at each other like children mimicking something they had seen once and half-remembered. Clunky. Mechanical. No flow, no instinct, no rhythm in their bodies at all.

They were boards stood on end, John thought. Planks with the rough shapes of soldiers carved onto them. Props. Scarecrows.

They would have to be beaten into shape. And they could show no restraint, because the enemy would offer none.

The squad leaders moved among the pairs. Correcting stances. Adjusting grips. Repositioning hips and shoulders and feet with the patience of sculptors working stone.

"Wait. Don't reach! Move in line. Catch and push!" John's voice carried through the room as he walked the rows. "There are four aspects of combat that must be adhered to: speed, accuracy, force, and direction."

Slow is smooth. Smooth is fast.

The words of his first drill instructor, spoken in a voice like gravel over glass, still lived in John's bones. He passed them on now, the way all useful things are passed on: through repetition, through correction, through the ache of muscles learning new shapes.

They learned to break bones. To shatter ribs. To fracture clavicles and target the soft places where minimal effort produced maximum damage. The recruits absorbed it the way dry earth absorbs rain: desperately, imperfectly, but with a thirst that could not be denied.

After two weeks, they were still behaving like wild animals.

Fights broke out. Tempers flared like brush fires in dry grass. The stress of confinement, the fear of what waited above, the memory of captivity still fresh and festering.

Jayk saw two men brawling near the mess hall one morning and moved to intervene. Po caught his arm.

"Let them."

"They'll hurt each other."

"Let them blow off some steam. This is how we settle things." Po's voice was calm. Certain. "Right now, you are still an outsider to this group. Your interference would be taken as a threat. Let this run its course. They'll fight, shake hands, and everything will be fine. You will see."

Jayk watched. The two men swung at each other with more fury than skill. They grappled. Fell. Got up. Fell again. Finally one of them put his hands on his knees, gasping, and the other wiped blood from his lip and offered his hand.

They shook.

That evening, Jayk found them sitting together in the mess hall, laughing over bowls of reconstituted protein, trading insults like brothers.

"You see?" Po said, appearing beside him. "This is what makes men courageous. They need to be reminded that they are strong. Both men are winners because neither backed down. These are the types of men we need. Fearless."

John was confronted on the third week.

A large man, thick through the chest, one of the rescued who had recovered faster than most. He stepped into John's path in the corridor and did not move. His eyes were hard. His fists were closed.

"You think you're better than us." It was not a question.

John looked at him. Said nothing. Stepped around him and kept walking.

Later, Po found him in the ready room.

"You could have taken him. Easily. You know more about warfare than anyone in this bunker."

"I know."

"Now he thinks he can come at you whenever he wants. He thinks you won't fight him. Next time it could be worse."

John was quiet for a moment. Then he said, "I used to know someone who taught me a valuable lesson. She told me that not every battle must be fought." His mouth curved, not quite a smile. A memory. "She would say: 'Is this the hill you want to die on?'"

"Who told you that?"

"My mother."

Po considered this.

"It's not enough to know how to fight," John said. "It's also important to know when not to."

"These people are tired," Po acknowledged.

"They're tired. They're hungry. They're scared. They feel helpless and they're tired of feeling that way. Stress is high and tempers flare. They're just letting off some steam." He looked at the map on the wall. The red lines of the gardens, spreading like veins. "There's no reason to make things worse."

Days became weeks.

The rhythm found them.

Wake before dawn. Form up. Sound off. Run the halls in pairs, boots striking concrete in time, the corridors so narrow their shoulders brushed the walls. The sound of it traveled through the bunker like a heartbeat: thump-thump, thump-thump, thump-thump.

They trained in the recreation room until sweat pooled on the floor and the air grew thick enough to taste. Sparring. Grappling. Weapons work with blades fashioned from scrap metal, their edges honed with whetstones that John taught them to use the way his grandfather had taught him to use a skinning knife. Slow circles. Steady pressure. Patient as prayer.

The sandbags took punishment until they burst. They made new ones.

The recruits who had arrived as scarecrows began to fill out. Muscle wrapped bone. Eyes that had been hollow grew sharp. Hands that had shaken learned to hold steady.

They were still rough. Still raw. But something was changing.

A woman in Ideo's squad who could barely lift a blade on the first day began to move with a fluidity that made even Po look twice. A boy of sixteen, thin as wire, turned out to have reflexes that belonged to a man twice his age. One of Rensa's recruits, a squat man with scarred hands and a face like a clenched fist, took to the brutality of her method as if he had been born for it.

The rhythm accelerated.

Punches landed cleaner. Blocks came faster. Footwork tightened. The clumsy brawling of the first weeks gave way to something sharper, something that had purpose and direction, like a song finding its key.

John heard it happening before he saw it. The sound of training changed. The wild grunts and frustrated curses became shorter. Clipped. Controlled. The percussion of fists on sandbags found a tempo. The shuffle of feet on concrete kept time.

They were learning the rhythm of combat.

"Ahhh-ten-SHUN! Form a line."

His soldiers had learned this meant to form straight lines: ten rows, twenty in each column. Each man and woman stood with arms down, hands at their sides, chest out, chin up, back straight, feet together.

"Sound off!"

The count started at the front. Rolled back like thunder. Each voice landing on the next without hesitation.

"Stand at ease."

Hands brought together in front. Shoulders back and square. Eyes forward.

These motions seemed small. Ceremonial. But John knew better. Discipline is a language, and these were its first words. A body that can obey a command without thought is a body that can act without hesitation when hesitation means death.

"Chow time!"

They jogged the corridors to the mess hall in pairs, shoulder to shoulder. The close quarters were cramped, but they made no complaint. Complaints were a luxury they had trained out of themselves.

The showers were worse. Cold water, limited time, twenty bodies cycling through in shifts. But they endured. They endured because they had learned that endurance was not the absence of suffering but the refusal to let suffering win.

After chow, the squad leaders gathered.

John, Po, Ideo, Rensa, Dade. Five around a table in the ready room, going over the day's exercises, measuring progress, identifying the ones who burned the brightest.

"The woman in my squad. Kael." Ideo set her blade on the table. "She's ready."

"The scarred one." Rensa leaned back. "Don't know his name. Don't need to. He fights like he's already dead and doesn't care."

"That's not a soldier," Po said quietly. "That's a weapon. Weapons break."

"Maybe." Rensa smiled. "But not before they do damage."

John listened. Took notes on a scrap of paper with a stub of pencil, one of twenty-three pencils they had found in a desk drawer, their erasers dried to stone. Strange, the things that survived the end of the world. Office supplies. Filing cabinets. A ceramic mug in the mess hall that read World's Best Grandpa in faded blue letters. Someone's grandchild had given them that, once. Someone had thought it was funny, or sweet, and spent money on it in a store that no longer existed, in a world that no longer existed, for a person who was almost certainly dead.

John drank his water from it every morning.

In this time they had learned the five basic priorities of survival: water purification, shelter construction, fire building, emergency signaling, and food procurement. This foundation would keep them alive in the wastelands, the dead zones between the gardens where the alien plant life choked the earth and very little that was human could grow.

"Usually military-style training involves firearms." John addressed the full assembly, pacing the front of the room. "However, since you won't have any, you will be trained to subdue and overcome your enemy with weapons of opportunity and unarmed hand-to-hand combat."

They had to remain within the compound. Going topside was too dangerous and might alert the creatures to their presence. So they trained indoors. Ran the halls. Fought in the gymnasium. Made do.

John taught them to fashion blades from metal scraps, to keep the edges honed, to kill without hesitation. They only had a few hundred weapons to share, and all of them were improvised: lengths of pipe, sharpened rebar, clubs wrapped in wire. No rifles. No grenades. Nothing that would have earned the word "tactical" in any military he had served in.

But a weapon is what you make of it.

When he was young on the reservation, his uncle had taught him to bring down a rabbit with a stone and a leather sling. "The weapon doesn't make the warrior," his uncle had said, fitting the stone into the pouch. "The warrior makes the weapon."

John had been seven years old. He killed his first rabbit on the third throw.

Those who could not fight, or would not, served in other ways. They prepared food and purified water. Cooked. Cleaned. Maintained sleeping quarters. Made weapons. Ran logistics. Scouted the corridors and kept the maps updated.

At times John felt these tasks were harder than training for battle. A soldier who fights has a clear purpose. A cook who feeds a soldier must find purpose in the feeding, and that is a quieter kind of courage.

He said nothing about this. But he saluted the kitchen workers every time he passed, and they noticed.

ROGER appeared on the sixth week.

He entered the recreation room carrying a rolled sheet of paper and the room fell quiet, because ROGER's presence did that to a room. He was too still. Too smooth. Too precise in his movements, as if each step had been calculated to within a millimeter before his foot left the ground.

He unrolled the diagram against the wall and held it there. A Sky Reaper, drawn in expert detail by one of the survivors who had worked close enough to the creatures to know their anatomy by sight. Beside it, for scale, the small figure of a human being.

The difference was obscene.

"The Sky Reaper physiology is completely different from the human body," John said, stepping aside to let ROGER take the floor. "Most of the techniques I could teach you will be useless against them."

The recruits groaned.

ROGER's lavender eyes swept the room. When he spoke, his voice carried the flat precision of a signal transmitted without static.

"From what we have gathered, the creature's lower legs are made of dense, heavy bones covered with thick connective tissues. The columnar shape suggests they are similar to those of elephants, meaning that, instead of being a weak spot as they are in humans, the legs are stronger and less vulnerable to attack."

He pointed to the diagram's midsection.

"The ribs are covered with a mass of compact fibrous muscular tissue, making them nearly impervious to fracture. The neck and reproductive organs are virtually absent in their anatomy."

He let that settle.

"The typical vulnerabilities of a human are not found in these creatures. Because of this, you will need different methods of assault to render them incapacitated."

The silence that followed was the silence of people recalculating their chances of survival and arriving at a smaller number.

John stepped forward. "Let's make no bones about it. We are up against impossible odds. The chance of your survival will be small. It will take all of your cunning and all of your wits. You will need more than strength to overcome these creatures."

He let them sit with that. Then:

"But rather than teaching you hundreds of techniques that may prove useless, you will learn eight basic maneuvers that will increase your odds of winning dramatically."

He chose one of the larger men in the group. "Now. Use what I've taught you. Attack me."

The recruit lunged with a punch. John parried, disrupted the flow of the man's energy, and used the momentum of the next strike to grapple his wrist and fling him to the ground. He jumped on the man's back and mimicked driving a blade into the base of his skull.

Then he helped him to his feet and thanked him.

"Weight distribution," John said. "Used properly, these techniques can overcome opponents far larger than you. The eyes are a weak spot. Go for them. Likewise the hands and wrists. Grabbing exposed limbs, or horns, and using your body weight to pull them where you want them to go."

He scanned the room.

"Disorienting your opponent is key. Knocking them off balance will be the simplest method of attack. Once unbalanced, your enemy will be confused, and that confusion buys you time to strike."

He held up a fist.

"When I first went to war, I couldn't think of my enemy as men." His voice dropped, and the room leaned in to hear it. "I had to think of them as deer. I had been hunting since I was young, on the reservation. I knew how to track a deer, how to read the way it moved, how to anticipate where it would go. When I stopped seeing soldiers and started seeing deer, I could do what needed to be done."

He opened his fist. Looked at his palm.

"I still regret that I had to take a man's life to protect my own, or my brothers in arms. But that is the cost. And you will pay it, or your enemy will collect it from you."

Po was training a recruit near the back of the room. A young man, barely old enough to be called one. The fear was in his eyes, plain as a wound.

"Are you scared?" Po asked.

The young man nodded. Tried to square his shoulders. Failed.

Po said nothing for a long moment. Then he placed his hand on the recruit's chest, directly over the heart, and held it there.

"Good. Fear means you understand what's coming." He removed his hand. "Now breathe. In through the nose. Out through the mouth. Fear lives in the breath. Control one, you control the other."

The young man breathed. His shoulders steadied. His eyes cleared.

Po nodded once and stepped back into fighting stance. "Again."

Walking the halls one afternoon, John was stopped by a young soldier who had been proving himself a quick study. Sharp eyes. Quiet discipline. The kind of man who saluted because he meant it, not because he had been told to.

"Sir." He stopped. Saluted.

John returned it. "At ease, soldier."

"Sir, I'm having a bit of trouble with this weapon, sir." He held it out as if presenting a gift.

John took it and turned it over in his hands. A long notched base with a cup at one end. The wood was old, dark with age, but the craftsmanship was sound.

"What you have here is called an Atlatl. It's used to hurl projectiles. A spear, a stone." He looked at the soldier. The notion that the man had been using it as a hammer surfaced in his mind. "The reason you can't figure the contraption out is because you don't have a missile. Let's find one."

They walked the halls together, searching. John's mind drifted as they went. A person cannot make someone go to war. That is improper. But someone can prepare a person for war with the right training and the right tools.

They found a long thin tube of metal that came to a point, rusted but solid, and John fitted it to the notched base.

"The proper stance is to hold the left arm out as a guide. Take the right arm back. Hold the Atlatl with the forefinger and thumb. When you are ready, extend the right arm with a quick, fluid motion and release."

They found a target: a painting on the wall, old enough to be dust, featuring a bearded man in a style that belonged to another century. They chose a spot between the eyes.

The soldier did as John instructed. On his first attempt, the missile struck the target dead center.

"Very good, soldier."

The soldier nodded silently. They exchanged salutes. John left him to practice, the sound of the missile striking the wall following him down the corridor like a metronome. Thunk. Thunk. Thunk.

There was a man with long braided hair who moved through the bunker like he belonged and didn't belong in equal measure. Jayk.

John had been watching him. They all had.

His brown eyes and dark complexion were a sign of the altered times. Having had all the nationalities, creeds, and cultures blended together by a common struggle, all of humankind had blurred the lines between the races. The blending of genetics had produced a single race: the human race. Gone were the days when humans distinguished themselves by the color of their skin. They were all now simply a shade of brown.

Jayk's braids hung over his shoulders, two thick ropes of dark hair, his most defining feature. Heavily hooded chocolate brown eyes. Thick lips that curved into a clever smile when he let them, which was not often enough.

He observed the training but did not participate. He watched the fighters with the quiet attention of a man cataloguing what he saw, storing it behind those careful eyes. There was something priestly about him, John thought. Something watchful and separate, as though he were bearing witness rather than preparing for war.

John had seen chaplains carry themselves that way. Men who served in the thick of combat without firing a shot, whose duty was to the spirit rather than the sword.

He filed the observation away.

There was another figure who came and went. A woman who worked hard at keeping her body hidden beneath a thick coat, despite the bunker's warmth. She moved with a purpose that had nothing to do with the training, slipping through corridors, appearing and disappearing on a schedule only she understood.

It was rumored she was carrying a weapon.

Not a blade. Not a club. Something else. Something that hummed.

John caught a glimpse of her once, at the far end of a corridor, her coat hanging open for just a moment as she turned a corner. What he saw beneath it made him stop walking.

He said nothing. But he filed that away, too.

The weeks turned. The soldiers sharpened.

Sentences got shorter in the training room. Commands became single words. Again. Faster. Again. The recruits stopped flinching at contact. Stopped hesitating before the strike. Their bodies began to move before their minds gave permission, which is the only way a body survives what the mind cannot accept.

John watched it happen the way a hunter watches a fire catch. First the smoke. Then the crackle. Then the roar.

They were becoming something.

Not perfect. You couldn't make perfect in eight weeks with salvaged blades and sandbag dummies and corridors for running tracks. But you could make dangerous. You could make willing. You could make the kind of soldier who looks death in the face and spits, because they have trained so long and so hard that their body knows what to do when their brain goes quiet with fear.

John had asked Po and Ideo each to assemble fifty of their best for survival training. He did the same. One hundred and fifty of the sharpest, toughest, most capable fighters the bunker had produced.

He met them in the recreation room.

"Until now, you have been training in combat and physical fitness."

He paced as he spoke. Po and Ideo stood at attention behind him.

"However, we are about to embark on the most important task required of us."

The soldiers, men and women both, kept their eyes forward. If they were nervous, they did not show it. This was good.

"You have been handpicked for this duty because of your strength and ability. We are going to be drawing field maps of the creature camps and gardens, as well as acquiring more gear: clothing, tools, essentials for our training and survival. There are many things you will need to know before we begin."

He let a beat pass.

"This will be a perilous journey. Some of you may not make it back."

He watched their faces. They showed nothing. Eight weeks ago, those same faces would have crumbled.

"You will use basic survival procedures as well as advanced techniques that I will teach you. You will learn the essentials of Marine field survival, which can be recalled through a simple acronym."

He turned to the wall behind him, where he had painted the letters in careful block script, each one the size of a man's hand.

"S" — Size up the situation.

"U" — Use all your senses.

"R" — Remember where you are.

"V" — Vanquish fear and panic.

"I" — Improvise.

"V" — Value living.

"A" — Act like the natives.

"L" — Live by your wits.

"Many people with no survival skills whatsoever have lived through circumstances where the odds were against them. While others who were trained in survival have died." He tapped the wall beside the word SURVIVAL. "Knowledge is only one ingredient. It is vital that you put these skills to use. There is a basic instinct in all of us to survive. This is the psychology of survival. Having the skills to survive is important."

His voice dropped.

"Having the will to survive is essential."

The room held still.

He went through each letter, explaining how it related to their situation, to the terrain, to the enemy. Then he moved on to the gear they'd be searching for and how to spot it in the field.

"Tomorrow, we will be taking our first excursion into the wastes as trained soldiers. Each and every one of you is prepared for this. We will be making maps and gathering gear along the way to fashion weapons that will help us in combat." He paused. "The greatest advantage we have is that most of what we need is already out there. We can procure it rather than manufacture it. Other items will need to be constructed from parts we find."

The men and women stood at attention. Unmoving. Unwavering.

"We are embarking on a great adventure," John said, "and one that will be spoken of for generations to come. Our actions today and in the coming months will be written about, studied, examined, and argued over by our children's children."

He felt the weight of it. The weight of history gathering around them like a storm.

"Let's make them proud."

"HU-RAHHH!"

The sound hit him like a fist to the chest. One hundred and fifty voices, unified, ferocious, alive.

He turned to Po.

"The soldiers are ready. Tomorrow we head out into the wastes."

He walked away to the salute of every soldier in the room. He had won their confidence and their admiration.

And, in no small way, they had earned his.

It had been two months.

Two months of sweat and blood and transformation. Of bodies rebuilt and minds reforged. Of strangers becoming squads and squads becoming soldiers and soldiers becoming something that might, against all odds, have a chance.

They had learned everything about one another. They had bled together and eaten together and slept shoulder to shoulder in cramped bunks in the belly of the earth. John's presence was met with a reverence that embarrassed him, because he knew that reverence belonged not to him but to the work they had done to earn it.

Now they were ready.

The first excursion would test everything. The maps. The training. The will.

Beyond the bunker doors, the wastelands waited. The alien gardens pressed inward like a closing fist. Somewhere out there, the creatures worked their slow and terrible transformation of the earth.

And here, in the dark below, one hundred and fifty soldiers stood ready to push back.

Jayk watched from the corridor as they filed toward the exit, boots striking the floor in unison. He saw the woman in the thick coat slip past, heading in the same direction, her collar turned up, her left arm held close to her body. He saw John at the head of the column, straight-backed, steady-eyed, a man who had been born for this moment or made for it, and the difference no longer mattered.

The tide was going out.

And these were the ones who would meet it.
