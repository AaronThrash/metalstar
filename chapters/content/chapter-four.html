The young man hit the ground hard, dust rising around him like a small explosion. Jayk extended his hand.

"Again."

Not a question. Not a request. The word carried a weight he hadn't known he possessed, something that had grown in him during these weeks of training, of watching these people look to him for answers he wasn't sure he had. The young man clasped his forearm and pulled himself up without complaint.

Jayk knew only one thing with certainty: practice. Repetition until muscle remembered what the mind forgot under pressure. And somehow, when he spoke, they listened. He would teach them everything he knew in a few weeks' time. The rest they would have to discover for themselves, the way he had, alone in a world that no longer made sense.

He had learned their names. Ideo. Zara. Anya. Oso. Iri. Ur. And others he was only beginning to know. But three had become something closer to comrades.

Po was the one who'd nearly killed him in the wastes, a mountain of a man with skin like polished obsidian and a head he kept shaved with his own blade. He rarely smiled, and when he spoke, his voice carried the low resonance of a war drum.

Rensa moved through the bunker like a song you couldn't quite remember; all wild hair tangled in coarse ropes and a laugh that came too quick, too sharp. She'd found a crowbar somewhere and carried it everywhere now, twirling it between her fingers when she was bored. Once, Jayk had caught her sneaking rations to the youngest survivors, the ones too proud to ask. She'd met his eyes and dared him to say something. He hadn't.

Then there was Dade, whose mouth never stopped running: commentary on everything, jokes that landed wrong, complaints about the food, the training, the smell of the bunker. But when they sparred, his lean body became something else entirely. He moved like water finding cracks in stone, and his fists hit like hammers on an anvil.

The four of them trained together daily, preparing for what was coming. What had to come.

Jayk sat with the others around the makeshift table, maps spread before them like the bones of a gutted animal. Surveys of gardens. Slave encampments. Enemy patrol routes. All of it assembled from weeks of dangerous reconnaissance.

"The gardens move," Po said, his finger tracing a path across the worn paper. "These maps are already outdated. But this one..." he tapped a location, "...this one's been stable. We hit it first. Extract as many humans as we can. Bring them back here."

Jayk listened with his eyes closed, hands folded before him. Not praying, though it might have looked like it. Just listening. Absorbing. Letting the rhythm of their planning become something he could feel in his chest.

"Night assault," Po continued. "Ground cover, mountain ridges for concealment. Nothing like this has been attempted before."

Wax's voice cut through like a blade on stone. "The airships thicken every day. The gardens spread closer. Their patrols push deeper into our territory." He paused, letting the silence fill with dread. "It's only a matter of time before they're right on top of us. Then there's nowhere left to hide."

The words hung in the air, a funeral chord waiting to resolve.

"The time has come," Wax finished, "to stop running. To take back what's ours."

They gathered in the main chamber: scavengers, rebels, survivors. People who had lost everything and found each other in the ruins. They clutched their weapons: axes, hammers, blades beaten from scrap metal. Rensa's crowbar gleamed dully on her shoulder.

Po paced before them, and when he spoke, his voice rose and fell like a hymn.

"I have known all of you for years. Some of you I raised from teenagers. Others came to me full grown, hardened by loss. But I have learned from each of you as much as I have taught." He paused, letting the silence breathe. "We didn't bring this war to them. They brought it to us. To our homes. To our families. To the bones of everyone we've buried."

He moved through them as he spoke, meeting eyes, gripping shoulders.

"They think we're scattered. Broken. They think survival is all we have left." His voice dropped low, then surged. "They're wrong. Every single one of you is a force they have never faced. You are tested. You are ready. You are fire."

The crowd stirred, weapons shifting, breath quickening.

"It's okay to be scared." Po's voice softened, almost tender. "Fear keeps you sharp. They don't bury the scared; they bury the dead."

A murmur of assent rippled through them.

"Simply surviving is no longer enough!" His voice cracked like thunder now. "Our planet has become a graveyard, and we will fill it with the bodies of our enemies!"

Metal struck metal. Hammers against blades, crowbars against pipes. The rhythm built, chaotic at first, then finding a pulse. A war-beat rising from the crowd like the first notes of a battle hymn that had no words yet. Just hunger. Just fury. Just the music of people who had finally decided to stop being prey.

Jayk watched them, feeling the vibration in his chest. Po was the voice they needed, grand, electric, a conductor pulling sound from silence. Jayk was still the outsider. The stranger who had stumbled into their midst and somehow found himself at the front.

But standing there, watching the firelight dance across their faces, he understood something. Leadership wasn't about belonging. It was about being willing to walk into the dark first.

They divided into five troops. Po and Jayk at the vanguard. Dade, Rensa, and Ideo holding flanks and rear.

"We move in separate groups to mask our numbers," Jayk explained, walking through the ranks. "We assess when we arrive. The element of surprise is the only advantage we have."

He knew the math. The enemy's army was larger. Their technology superior. They had airships while the resistance had feet and determination. Every metric favored the invaders.

But this was home. And that meant something no scale of theirs could weigh.

"This is our land," Jayk said, and his voice surprised him with its steadiness. "They've taken our families. Enslaved our people. Turned our world into a harvesting ground." He looked at them, really looked, at the fire burning in their eyes. "No more."

They raised their weapons. Fists in the air. A salute to something none of them could name but all of them felt.

The war had begun.

Miles away, in the hollow cathedral of a dead city, John talked to himself again.

It had become habit now, narrating his days to no one, just to hear a human voice. 'Found a sewing kit today. That's useful. Binoculars yesterday. Most of it's garbage, but you take what you can get."

Six weeks since he'd crawled from the cloning chamber. Six weeks of rats and snakes and rainwater collected in plastic jugs. Six weeks without another human face.

He'd made a home in the lobby of an old hotel, a magnificent twenty-four story structure with a hollow core spiraling up through the center. Each floor wrapped around the void, balconies stacked like ribs, reaching toward a penthouse he could see from the marble floor below. The architecture was dizzying, beautiful, frozen in time. It had taken him a week just to clear enough rubble from the entrance to crawl inside.

But once he had... a jewel. Handcrafted furniture from an era that no longer existed. A wardrobe of sturdy clothing, barely touched by decay. And height enough to build an indoor fire without choking on smoke.

He was safe here. Hidden.

And completely alone.

The creatures moved through the city sometimes. He watched them from the upper floors, hunched shapes communicating in grunts and wheezes, sounds like cattle or elephants. He'd avoided them so far. Stayed invisible. Stayed alive.

But survival wasn't enough anymore. If he ever wanted to understand what had happened, where the rest of humanity had gone, he would need to get closer.

He had spent the last week preparing. Mapping their patrol routes. Timing their movements. Planning a reconnaissance mission for the next time he found a few of them alone.

Now, he watched. And waited.

And tried not to think about how long a person could go without hearing another voice before they went completely insane.

John was thirty-three years old, built like something the desert had tried to break and failed. Broad shoulders, a torso that commanded attention, and a way of moving, light on his feet, fluid, that revealed his Native American heritage. His Marine-regulation cut had grown wild out here, a thick mane of raven hair that framed wide-set eyes and skin bronzed dark by weeks under the wasteland sun.

He'd always been an outcast. Growing up, that had forced him to be made of sturdier stuff, the kind of emotional armor that let a man be content alone. When he joined the Corps, he finally found a place that fit. The Marines were the misfits of the armed services, and his brothers in arms had become the family he'd never had.

His mother had been a medicine woman, deeply spiritual, one of the few who still carried the healing knowledge passed down through her lineage. She'd taught him to respect nature, to see the animals as kin. His father had loved her desperately, loved John too, but a lung infection had taken him when John was still small. After that, there was nothing holding him back. He shipped off to Parris Island and never looked back.

But he'd kept what she gave him. Even as a Marine, he held onto that reverence for the living world. He just needed something to protect. A purpose. A cause.

At his core, John was a rebel. And a rebel without a cause is just a man waiting to die.

The days passed quickly despite the silence.

Orange skies turned the sun into a furnace by day, but the desert nights cut to the bone. By the angle of the light and the shortening days, John figured it was fall sliding into winter. Time moved differently when no one was counting it with you.

He was on the roof when he saw them, sitting with a book in one hand and a can of peaches in the other, sauce dripping lazily into his beard. A flicker of movement on the horizon. He set the can down slowly, raised the binoculars.

A band of people. A few hundred, maybe more. All dressed in rugged coats and heavy pants, moving together in tight formations that broke into smaller groups as they advanced. Military discipline, even without uniforms. Even without guns.

John flattened himself against the rooftop, pulse drumming in his ears.

"Well," he said to no one, "this is an interesting development."

They entered the city carefully, spreading out while protecting their numbers. He tracked a few through the scope as they passed, young, old, hard-faced survivors carrying weapons fashioned from tools. Crowbars. Hammers. Blades made from scrap. Not a single rifle or handgun among them.

They were on a mission. That much was clear. Moving with purpose, watching their corners, communicating in gestures. Whatever they were after, it wasn't him.

He wanted to call out. To wave his arms and shout *I'm here, I survived too, take me with you.*

But he didn't.

These people knew where they were going. They had a purpose, a cause, something he hadn't found yet. If he revealed himself now, he'd just be a stranger with questions they didn't have time to answer.

They would have to come back through. And when they did, maybe he'd know enough to be useful. Maybe he'd have earned the right to join them.

Until then, he had his own mission.

He picked up the can of peaches, wiped his chin, and watched them disappear into the city.

They crossed the wastelands like ghosts, using abandoned buildings and hollow car frames for cover, pressing forward in silence. At night they slept in camouflaged tents, always with eyes to the sky, watching for enemy ships. The days blurred together, a rhythm of movement and stillness, advance and hide.

Soon the garden's edge came into view.

The journey had worn on Jayk more than he'd expected. Each night as he lay in the dark, anxiety coiled tighter in his chest. The further they traveled from the compound, the stronger it grew. Were they capable of this? He'd fought these creatures before, a handful at a time, on familiar ground. But this was different. A full assault on enemy territory. Unfamiliar terrain. Unknown numbers.

Something twisted in his gut.

He wanted it over. Wanted to return to the bunker. Home. The word felt strange in his mind. He'd never had one, not really. Home had always meant wherever they weren't. After watching his parents die, after losing everyone he'd ever loved, he'd spent years running. Avoiding. Surviving.

Now he was taking a stand. And he'd convinced others to stand with him.

The weight of that sat heavy on his shoulders.

He found a flat stone overlooking the slave encampment and sat with his broadsword across his lap, running a whetstone along the blade in slow, meditative strokes. The sound was almost musical, metal singing against stone, a hymn of preparation.

A touch on his shoulder startled him from the rhythm.

He turned to find Ideo looking down at him, her dark eyes cool and searching. There was something aristocratic in the way she held herself, every movement precise, deliberate. Her long black hair hung like spun silk, catching the fading light as if it weighed nothing at all.

She was beautiful. And she knew it.

"Jayk." Her voice was soft, almost a purr, as she settled onto the stone beside him. "So. We're finally here." She gazed out toward the garden, then back at him. "Do you think your men are ready?"

"I hope so." He kept his eyes on the blade. "We'll find out soon enough."

She flashed a knowing smile, her eyes glancing skyward before darting away. "Can anyone truly be ready for war? You prepare for it, you train for it, but it's just an idea until you become a warrior." She let the words settle. "You gave them everything you could. Now it's up to them."

Her voice was soothing. He hadn't expected that.

She reached into one of her cargo pockets, the pants dark and stained, worn from hard use, and produced two small bottles that looked like eye droppers. She held them out.

Jayk took them carefully. "What are these?"

"Something I made." That mischievous light returned to her eyes. "Two chemicals. Inert on their own. But when you mix them..." she mimed an explosion with her hands, fingers spreading wide, "...boom."

He raised an eyebrow.

"I call it impact gel." She was clearly proud. "You combine them, rub the mixture in your palm, it heats up, forms an unstable compound. Harmless until you apply force. A few pounds of pressure is all it takes. Throw it against something solid and..." She grinned. "A few drops will turn a concrete wall to dust."

Jayk held the bottles like they might detonate in his hands. "Where do you find chemicals like this?"

"My home. The Free City of Solos." Something softened in her voice. "I should take you there sometime. You can get almost anything in Solos." A pause. "Almost."

"I've never heard of it."

"Good. That means we're doing our job." She smiled. "The people who need to find it, find it. It's not on any map."

He turned the bottles over in his hand, then offered them back. "Thanks. But this is the only weapon I need." He gripped his sword hilt and gave her a wink.

She shrugged, tucking the bottles away. "Suit yourself. But impact gel has gotten me out of more jams than I can count." Her eyes traveled to his blade. "I saw you the other day. In the exercise room. You're good."

"I've had practice."

"My father loves the sword as much as you do." She tilted her head, studying him. "He calls sword fighting a dance between two men." A sly smile. "It sounds better in Japanese. *Futari no otoko no odori.*"

Jayk smirked. "No, it doesn't."

They both laughed, the sound unexpected, brief, human. It felt strange out here on the edge of battle.

She looked away, her voice dropping. "Maybe you could meet him someday."

He studied her then. The burn scars tracing pale rivers along her arms. The dimples that appeared when she smiled. She was hard and soft all at once, strength wrapped in silk, sharpness hidden beneath grace. He didn't know her story. But he recognized something in her. The same displacement he carried. The same sense of being unmoored.

She stared toward the garden, and her voice turned cold.

"The day will come when humans rule this planet again."

He heard the anger beneath the words. The certainty.

"You really believe that, don't you?"

She didn't look at him. "Of course I do. And so should you."

He picked up a handful of debris, let it sift through his fingers. Dust and ash; everything reduced to the same gray nothing. "This is still our planet," he said quietly.

She watched him. Nodded once. Then rose and left him there, alone with the memory of her words.

*Am I being too negative?*

The thought echoed in the silence she'd left behind.

*Maybe being alone this long has made me cynical.*

But something stirred in him now. Something she'd sparked. He felt it kindling in his chest, not hope, exactly. Something harder. More dangerous.

This could be the end of humanity. The last stand of a dying species on a planet that had already become a graveyard.

But Jayk refused to go quietly.

He let the hatred rise. The hatred he'd carried since he was a child, since he'd watched his parents fall, since the world ended and left him alive in the ruins. He'd buried it for years. Tamped it down. Survived.

Now he let it burn.

Revenge.

The word moved through him like a prayer. He let it fill his bones, reignite the fire that had smoldered for so long. They murdered his family. They would pay for that.

He rose, quick, decisive. For a moment he stood at the edge of the rock face, staring out at the desert that had shaped him. A child of the wastes. An orphan who'd learned to survive alone.

He would draw on those skills now.

The men had rested long enough. If fear was taking root in their hearts, it would only spread. Hesitation was a poison. The time to act was now.

He walked to Po's camp.

Po sat inside his tent in the lotus position, hands on his knees, eyes closed. His breathing was steady, measured, a deep rhythm that seemed to slow the world around him. Even now, on the eve of battle, the man radiated stillness.

"Po." Jayk's voice was low but firm. "It's time. The sun is setting. We have surprise on our side; we should mobilize before we lose it."

Po opened his eyes slowly. He must have seen the tension in Jayk's face, because he asked: "You're certain?"

"It's now or never. If we wait, we risk the men losing their nerve. Or worse... deserting."

Po considered this. Then nodded solemnly.

He rose and stepped out of the tent, his voice carrying across the camp like a drumbeat.

"Jayk has decided. It's time to attack."

As day bled into night, John heard it, a low rumbling in the sky.

He launched from his resting place and ran to the hole in the wall that served as his window to the world. The building groaned around him, walls pocked with age and damage. These structures had seen their share of war long before he arrived.

He stepped outside and looked up.

The ship moved like nothing he'd ever seen. Aerodyne, clearly, but it hovered with the weightless ease of a balloon, no visible propulsion, nothing that should have kept it aloft. And yet it hung there, drifting across the darkening sky.

The sound it made was wrong. Not a whoosh or a whirr, but something deeper, a low, plangent drone that resonated in his chest. Heavy. Reverberating. Like the wow and flutter of a phonograph, stretched out and amplified into something almost alive.

The craft itself was silver-white, organic in shape. A bulbous frontend sloped back into something like an airfoil, all smooth curves and alien geometry. It had an ethereal quality that made his skin prickle.

*Alien.* There was no other word for it.

He tracked it with the binoculars as it moved toward the mountains, the same direction the marching band had gone. But this wasn't an intercept course. The ship drifted slowly, deliberately. Carrying something. Supplies, maybe.

He watched until it disappeared beyond the ridgeline.

Something new was happening over there. Something he'd never seen before.

His curiosity sharpened into need.

The army gathered their weapons and moved out on foot toward the garden.

Nightfall crept over them as they descended the craggy rock face, picking their way down into the plains basin. A twisting path led them in circuitous fashion toward a small river that fed the encampment, and toward the massive sewage and waste reclamation pipes that jutted from the compound like open wounds.

They stopped in the shadow of the pipes. The air was foul, thick with the smell of things Jayk didn't want to name.

Beyond, he heard something moving toward them in the darkness.

No one spoke. No one breathed. Po watched from his position at the foot of the surrounding mountains, waiting for the signal.

Then, overhead, that sound. The distinctive drone of one of their vessels, returning to dock somewhere beyond the garden. Jayk looked up as the landing gear opened, huge chunks of dried earth breaking loose and cascading to the ground around them like a plague of stones.

Something snapped inside him.

He couldn't wait any longer.

Jayk broke from cover and ran toward the ship, toward the encampment, sword already in his hand. He didn't think. Didn't plan. The rage that had been building since his parents died, since the world ended, since he'd sworn revenge on a rooftop overlooking this very garden, it all came roaring out of him at once.

He rushed the compound and found himself surrounded.

Twenty of the beasts. Towering above him, their great slabs of legs planted firm. He'd fought three before, when they were ready for him. But this time he was the ambush.

He drew his dagger in his off-hand, sword raised, and threw himself at the closest one.

Blind rage. Pure instinct. Completely alone... unaware his comrades were nowhere near.
