They struck out on their way, leaving the cottages behind them, and Trinidad felt a small pang of sadness in her heart. As the dome of the city began to disappear beneath the horizon she started to feel slightly safer from the influence of Leo's holy guard, who she knew had already been activated in search of her and Riley. It was a long and tedious trek across the wastes with only the barest essentials they could bring, compounded by the weight of carrying the trade goods.

A woman spoke to Trinidad from behind, a slight female wearing multiple thick-woven scarves. "Is this your first time on the road?"

"Yes. Myself and Riley." She patted Riley on the back, who looked up and stared inquisitively at the woman with sparkling green and brown eyes that danced when she spoke.

"Well, it is not as bad as people make it out to be. It can be rough, but if you follow the person in front of you and pay attention to the spotters, you should be fine. Most of the trip is mental exhaustion." She said, walking up beside Trinidad.

"Let us hope. I doubt my body could take much more physical exhaustion." Trinidad said with a laugh.

"Do not worry. Just put your back into it. It helps to have someone to talk to, takes your mind off your feet." The woman said. "I am Anjari."

"Pleased to meet you, Anjari. My name is Trinidad." She extended her hand to the woman, who extended hers in response. Her slender fingers were marked by years of painful service, burnt and wrinkled. Compared to Trinidad's they were the hands of a woman many years her senior. Riley took notice of every line and scar. "You must have traveled this road many times. You seem to have an understanding of the terrain, and what it takes to survive." Trinidad said.

"I have been making these treks for two years now. It has helped me take my mind off the loss of my husband." Anjari said. "I have seen just about everything there is to see. I have even been hunted down by the ajnabi. Once, we got separated by a sandstorm and twelve of us nearly lost our lives. We wandered the desert wastes for three days before finally being found by another caravan. It is easy to get lost out there without any landmarks." She paused. "There are many ways to die in the wastelands, and they grow larger and more oppressive every month. I wonder how long it will be before all the land is consumed by them."

Trinidad thought about that for a moment. Here was a woman who seemed adept at negotiating the severe environment, even went so far as to encourage her to be unafraid of the monumental task ahead. And yet even she herself had doubts about the future of humankind. "You used the word ajnabi. What does it mean?"

"It means foreigner." Anjari said. "We never gave them a proper name. We did not think they would be here that long. Probably thinking that eventually the monarch or the military would save us. The monarch is dead and the military has disbanded. Now, we must save ourselves."

Trinidad deliberated for a moment on telling her about the prophecy she had been given and about her visions. She was not sure if it would comfort her, or scare her away. "I think there is still time for humankind to reverse the damage caused by the ajnabi. Is that how you say it?"

"Yes, very good." Anjari complimented her.

They continued walking for many kilometers as Anjari regaled her with stories of her family and life. "My son died in my arms." She said quietly.

"I am so sorry. Is that how you received the burn scars on your hand?" Trinidad asked, recognizing that the scars were not from hard labor, but had been created by the electrical discharges from one of the alien weapons.

"Yes." Anjari replied. "Sadly." She thought for a moment and then continued. "To have lost my child is bad enough, but to bear the marks of the experience as a constant reminder is almost too much." She looked ahead toward the caravan slowing in the distance. "It looks like we are reaching the first trade post."

Riley and Trinidad had been so caught up in her tale that he had not been paying attention, and as a result neither had she. They continued on until they reached a hillside. "We will rest here for a bit while the buyers come out to look over our goods." Anjari said, removing her pack from her shoulders and laying everything out neatly on a rug in front of her. She sat on her knees arranging all the containers of foodstuffs and the inedibles she carried, awaiting the buyers.

The wastes they had traveled across had not been as bad as Trinidad expected. It was nighttime after all, the best time to travel. The stars were easy to track as they moved across the sky, and the darkness helped shelter them from the creatures who patrolled as much of the Earth as they could. They had stopped next to a hill that did not look like anything at all, and Riley watched as Karesh went in through an opening in the rock face. When he returned he was followed by dozens of men and women and children of all sizes. The flames from their lamps brightened the otherwise moonlit ground and helped provide a light for the traders' wares. They bartered back and forth, haggling over a finger of this or a fist of that. Items were packaged in quantities no larger than what could be carried in the palm of the hand, and sold in the same increments. Trinidad sat with Riley, their packs belonging to another trader, and watched the exchange of goods from one hand to the next, and then saw the children running back and forth filling up canteens and bottles with water from an underground pool. This went on for an hour or so before Karesh informed them it was time to leave. He went back inside the cave, and when he emerged everyone had rolled up their packs and loaded everything back up. With the spotters on the qui vive for alien airships, they were prepared to cover the next stretch of the road.

"Did you remember to refill your water bottle?" Anjari asked.

"Yes, a young boy came up to me and asked for it." Trinidad said.

"Good. I forgot once and had to go for hours without anything to drink. I was too ashamed to ask anyone for some of theirs. Now I make sure to refill them." She said, smiling. They continued on for several more kilometers and Anjari, seeing Riley's eyes locked on the heavens, began pointing out the stars.

"That one there, the bright one, that is Sirius. It is known as the Dog Star." Anjari explained.

Riley was completely absorbed by the astronomy lesson. *The Dog.* *Yes, Riley, the dog in the sky.* Trinidad quietly informed him.

"And that one is Procyon. They make up the constellations of Canis Major and Canis Minor, and just to the west of them is Orion, the hunter. See the three stars that make up his sword belt?" Anjari pointed up at them.

"You know a great deal about the stars. You are like a wizard." Trinidad said.

"When you spend as much time out here as I have, you tend to learn about them. I have not had a home to go back to for so long, the road has become my home, and the stars my family." Anjari said.

The sun began to rise, scattering the heavens with a mottled canopy of crimson and magenta. The caravan reached a stopping point and the travelers removed their packs and threw out their rugs and blankets to sleep on, covering themselves completely with their ghillie suits in case the enemy came by overhead as they slept.

Even as the sun reached its highest point, Trinidad could not sleep. Riley was knocked out beside her, sleeping deeply. She could feel the heat of the sun beating down on the coverings and hear the wind blowing across the sand. Something did not seem right. Something in the air felt wrong, a pressure building where there should have been none, and Trinidad decided to wake Riley so she could see what was happening. "Riley. Riley." She shook his body but he just lay there, so she slipped into his dream and spoke to him. *Riley, will you wake up and look at something for me?* Riley woke and poked his tiny head out from beneath the ghillie suit. *A question mark.* "Something does not feel right. Will you look around so I can see?" *A pair of binoculars.* "Yes, Riley, just scan the horizon for me, please." Riley did as he was asked, his big brown eyes searching. "There. Go back. Look, do you see it?" Riley looked until he finally saw what appeared to be a giant yellow and brown wall coming toward them. "Sandstorm!" Trinidad jumped up and began calling out to the others. "Sandstorm! Sandstorm!"

The entire caravan awoke, looking around until they saw it too. There was nowhere to go, so everyone hunkered down, holding on to everything they had as the mighty force of sand and wind blew across them, turning them topsy-turvy. They covered their eyes and mouths and noses as best they could, but the sand attacked them in every direction, getting into every orifice and penetrating every crevice. A giant yellow wall of debris covering them completely with gale force winds. The travelers held on to their packs and rugs as best they could. Some got away, taking off like kites, but the people stood their ground, holding on to one another tightly as the winds passed over them. And as quickly as it came, it was gone. The travelers took stock of their goods, and finding them in fair condition counted themselves fortunate. Now that everyone was awake Karesh decided to get a jump on the journey. The sun was almost setting on their right side as they started making their way south.

"It is good you caught that storm early, Trinidad. Thanks to you we are safe." Anjari congratulated her.

"It is nothing. It just felt like something was off. Like the air was not behaving right." Trinidad said.

"Well, whatever it was, I am sure everyone here is grateful." Anjari said.

The sun had dropped beyond the horizon and cool winds began to blow across the valley. Anjari finally asked Trinidad about where she and Riley came from. "What was the city like?"

"It was pleasant. Very calm and peaceful. Plenty of good food, plenty of people to interact with. Social gatherings, potlatches and the like." Trinidad continued walking in silence for several minutes. "I will miss it. But I will be back soon enough."

"You say that with certainty. If there is something I have learned about this world, it is that nothing is certain." Anjari responded.

Trinidad was still unsure about how much she could share with Anjari about her life, but decided that if she was going to do it, now would be as good a time as any. "Anjari, do you believe in fate?"

"Fate? I believe in divine intervention. But I do not know if I believe in fate. Do you mean like destiny?" Anjari asked.

"In a way, yes."

"I do not know if I believe in that or not. If I did, I would have to believe that my son and my husband died because something in the stars willed it to be that way, and for some sadistic reason decided to force me to live alone." Anjari contemplated.

"What if, for the sake of discussion, we say that there was an order to everything, and you could step outside of yourself and see that order, just a glimpse. What if I were to tell you that you could watch the future or the past in much the same way as you watch things happening now? What would you call that?"

"You mean fortune telling?"

Trinidad let out a small laugh. "In a way, I suppose it is like that, yes. But in another more specific way, it is like dreaming. Like having a waking dream that you cannot control. You can watch it take place as it unfolds before your eyes, but you have no power over what happens, or what direction it takes. And then, for the sake of discussion, what if we were to say that the events from your dream came true. Would you believe in that?"

Anjari stopped dead in her tracks. "Oh my ... you are her! You are the witch, I mean, the heretic. Which do you prefer, witch or heretic?" She shook her head at such a ridiculous thing to have asked. "Never mind that. You are the blind prophet! You were Sovran's most trusted advisor! You foretold his death! Did you know Vacilly was going to kill him? It is you, is it not? You are her!"

Trinidad smiled. "Yes. I am me. And that was a long time ago. I was probably your age then."

"I am sorry, I did not recognize your name. I feel so foolish. Everyone has heard of you! Can you do it? Can you see the future?" Anjari asked, suddenly interested in prophecy.

"I can indeed. And the past. And the present." Trinidad said with a knowing grin.

The two of them continued talking all through the night and into the morning. Anjari was so utterly consumed by the accounts of Trinidad's exploits, the tales of her gifts, when she discovered them, how she escaped certain death from the clutches of the Chief Cleric, that she barely noticed the growing commotion up ahead as the caravan came to a complete halt.

"I wonder what is going on." Anjari said.

"I have no idea." Trinidad responded.

Anjari turned to the man traveling behind her and asked, but he had not been paying attention either. She walked up toward the front of the caravan where the trade master was. A few people had crowded together in a knot. "What is going on? Why are we stopped?" she asked one of them. Everyone looked on in stunned confusion. Anjari elbowed her way through, her tiny frame carving a path between them. When she reached Karesh it was apparent something was terribly wrong.

"Are you all right? What is wrong?" She begged him.

"We're going where the camel leads as all great ones do." Karesh was not making sense. He stumbled over his words and became frantically upset at his own voice. "Wezert vinhindy garbush. Nup! Sandly, nup sandly! Fluvin." His eyes rolled back and he dropped like a stone to the ground.

Anjari stood helpless, looking down at his body. His face had gone slack on one side. His left hand curled inward like a wilting leaf. He was still breathing, but whatever was happening inside his head was taking him somewhere no one could follow. Unless they could find help soon, he would die. And because he had been leading them in the wrong direction for who knew how long, and because he was the only person who knew the way, they were lost.

"You have to help him!" Anjari ran back to Trinidad.

"I cannot do anything for him. He needs a doctor." Trinidad said.

"But can you not heal him?" She protested.

"I am not a healer. My abilities only show me what is going to happen. I cannot do anything to change what will happen."

"Then what good are your powers if you cannot change anything?" Anjari cried out. "We are going to die out here if you do not do something to save us!"

"I am sorry, Anjari. There is nothing I can do. I do not have the power to heal people." Trinidad said, and the sadness in her voice was real, because she knew Anjari was right about one thing. They might all die out here.

"You are wasting my time." Anjari stormed off.

Riley watched as she disappeared into the crowd. *An angry face.* "Yes, Riley, she is upset. I wish there was something I could do to help, but it looks like we are stuck until we can come up with a solution."

Some of the traders decided to head out on their own in the hopes of finding the next post, but not knowing what to look for or where to look, their chances were slim. They could travel south for a day and still be lost. Trinidad grew concerned about making it to Solos and meeting the people from her visions. *Trinidad with a worried face.* "Yes, Riley, I am worried. The longer we sit here, the greater the chance we will miss meeting the others."

Two days passed and still no sign of the traders who had gone ahead. The water was gone. The spotters had deserted the caravan.

Trinidad had a thought.

"Riley, I may be able to help after all." *A giant question mark.* "I could leave my body and travel the wastes much faster than anyone could walk them. I could search for the next trading post, and then send you the directions through our connection, and you could lead the people." She was almost beside herself at having thought of it. "This might just work."

Trinidad lay down and closed her eyes. She slipped into the silence.

It was nothing like walking. The physical world fell away, heavy and slow, and she was free of it. She did not move through space so much as shift across it, consciousness leaping from one point to the next, instant and weightless. The wastes that would take a caravan days to cross she covered in minutes, scanning the terrain below like a hawk riding a thermal. Within moments she believed she had found the next trading post, and as she drew closer she was certain of it. One of the traders who had gone ahead on their own was so close to it he could have reached out and touched it, were it not hidden so well. Trinidad entered the post and saw the people within, confirming her discovery. She sent the image to Riley immediately. *A cave in a hillside, people inside, lamps burning.* *A smiling Riley clapping.* She returned to her body.

When she awoke she went to Anjari. "I found the next trading post, and I can help us all get there. If you follow Riley he will lead you to it."

"How? I do not understand." Anjari replied.

"I will travel through the nonphysical world and send the directions to Riley telepathically, leading him so everyone else can follow." Trinidad explained. "Someone will have to carry me, because I will not be in control of my physical body."

Anjari nodded. She knew Trinidad to be a good and decent person, and she knew she would not offer help unless she was certain it would work. Whatever frustrations she had felt before dissolved in the face of this.

Trinidad slipped back into the silence. She traced the route from the trading post all the way back to where the caravan was located and, hovering above them, transmitted the directions to Riley. They placed her body and the body of the trade master in blankets and picked them up, carrying them toward the next destination. They traveled across the plains day and night. They had already lost a great deal of time and lacked the spotters needed to protect them, so they all kept their eyes to the sky. Riley led the caravan, his small body fighting dehydration and exhaustion. The travelers offered him berries and rootstock to chew on to keep his strength up. For such a small boy he was doing remarkably well, thanks to Trinidad's unceasing encouragement. *You are doing so well, Riley. You are truly a special boy.* *The lion with a red heart.* *That is right, Riley. You are brave.* *A smiling Riley.*

It took them a little over a day traveling without rest to reach the next post, but when they did the people were overjoyed. The trading post had been carved into the side of a mountain that rose from the outskirts of the valley, obscured by natural rock formations that made it nearly impossible to locate. At one point it must have been a tunnel through which a train passed, but after so many years the tracks had long since been overgrown. Now it was nothing more than an enormous cave. This time they all entered the tunnel instead of the buyers coming out. Trinidad was lowered gently and woke hungry and drained.

When they set down the blanket that carried the trade master, his body hung limp. He had died sometime during the journey. The traders mourned his passing and were left with a question none of them wanted to ask: who would lead them now? Trinidad could help in getting them to Solos, but after that they would need a new trade master.

"You know, you should give some thought to becoming the new trade master." Trinidad said to Anjari. "You are familiar with celestial navigation, which is practically like sorcery to me. You are assertive and independent. And you know the terrain."

"I do not think I could do something like that. It is one thing to help a few people who are stranded, especially when you have got supernatural intervention." She nodded to Trinidad. "But to lead groups of hundreds of people, keeping track of all the changing trade posts, navigating the various routes. That is a different creature altogether." Anjari said.

"Well, just something to think about. You would probably be very good at it. I, for one, would follow you." Trinidad said.

Anjari looked at her.

"You followed me." Trinidad added.

That night the traders packed up what was left of their wares, refilled their water bottles and canteens, and struck out for Solos. Riley walked at the front. Anjari walked beside him. Trinidad was carried in a blanket between two traders, her eyes closed, her consciousness already miles ahead of them, tracing the road they could not see, lighting the way home for a boy who could not speak and a woman who was learning she could lead.
