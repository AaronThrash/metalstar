Trinidad left her body the way one leaves a room: quietly, without ceremony, as though the flesh were simply a coat she had set down on a chair. Her incorporeal self hovered a few feet from the entrance to Solos, that great wound in the earth where humankind had burrowed to survive, and from this vantage she could sense Riley below, his small legs carrying him steadily forward across the cracked and sunblasted terrain, the remnants of the caravan strung out behind him like beads on a fraying thread. She reached for him through the bond they shared, that silent cord of thought and image that tethered her sight to his eyes and his hearing to her ears.

Riley, if you feel tired, or just want to take a break, let me know.

The image that came back was not words, for Riley had never spoken a word in his life and never would. It was the aspect of the Ox: broad-shouldered, immovable, pressing forward through heavy earth with the patience of something that had been born to pull weight.

You are such a strong and courageous young man, Riley. I'm so proud of you.

A smiling face returned to her mind. His own face, as he imagined it, round-cheeked and beaming, the expression of a boy who had been told he mattered by the only person in the world whose opinion he trusted.

Then something struck her, sudden and sharp as a blade drawn across still water. Not a sound, not a vision, but a sensation that lived below language, below thought, in the deep animal architecture of the body where instinct coiled like a spring. Someone was dying.

Riley, I need to go check on someone. You'll be all right to continue the walk? I will not be but a moment.

A thumbs up. Small and certain.

Her nonphysical self moved across the wasteland with the speed of thought, drawn by the distress signal that pulsed like a beacon through the aether. She had learned long ago that ignoring these feelings carried consequences, and the consequences were never hers. The landscape below her was a palimpsest of ruin: cracked riverbeds, collapsed structures, the bones of a civilization picked clean by time and neglect. She followed the sensation south and east until the earth split open beneath her and she was hovering above a gorge, its walls striated in rust and ochre, and at the bottom, three figures lay in the dust like dolls discarded by a careless hand.

She knew them. She had seen their faces in her visions: the tall one with the bearing of a warrior, the woman with one arm ending in something that was not an arm, and the young man with the heavy-lidded eyes and the braid that fell over his shoulder like a rope. They were fading, their bodies surrendering to the heat and the thirst, and if she did not act quickly they would be dead before the sun moved another inch across the sky.

Trinidad also sensed another presence. Within the canyon wall itself, hidden behind a hollow in the rock, there was life: a cluster of minds, wary and watchful, belonging to people who inhabited a trading post carved into the stone. She entered the wall like a breath passing through cloth and found one of them, a man whose thoughts were occupied with inventory and suspicion and the daily arithmetic of survival. She did not speak to him. She merely brushed the surface of his awareness with the suggestion that something was wrong outside — just enough to draw the eye.

Then she waited. She hovered above the gorge, incorporeal and patient, watching through senses that had no name, until the hollow in the rock wall opened and a group of men and women emerged carrying water skins and blankets and the cautious determination of people who had decided, against their better judgment, to help.

She watched them lift the three figures from the dust. She watched the water poured over cracked lips, the blankets draped over sunburned shoulders. She waited until she was certain they would live.

Then she returned to Riley.

I'm back. I had to help some people that you are going to meet soon.

Riley's mind offered her an image of curiosity: wide eyes and a tilted head.

Soon enough, little one. Soon enough.

He and Anjari and what remained of the caravan were only a few kilometers northwest of Solos now. Trinidad decided it was time to return to her body and walk the remaining distance with the others. She had been gone too long as it was, and the flesh grew cold when the spirit wandered.

Riley, you should take a break while I return to my body.

A thumbs up. He stopped walking and turned to Anjari, who halted the caravan with a raised fist. The traders lowered their packs and sat where they stood, grateful for the pause, their faces drawn and dust-caked, carrying the weariness of people who had been walking for longer than their bodies had agreed to.

Trinidad opened her eyes. The light was there: Riley's light, the borrowed vision that came to her through their bond, and through it she perceived the blurred shapes of the caravan, the dry ground, the pale sky. One of the traders placed a canteen in her hand, and she drank. The water was warm and tasted of tin and was the finest thing she had ever put to her lips. The caravan rested. They gathered their strength in silence, and then they took up their packs and walked the last stretch to Solos.

The entrance to the underground city was not a gate or a door but a passage: a fissure in the earth that had been widened and reinforced over decades until it resembled the throat of some enormous creature that had swallowed a civilisation and kept it alive in its belly. The traders entered one by one, descending into amber-lit tunnels where the air was cooler and smelled of stone and lamp oil and the accumulated breath of thousands. Anjari went ahead to meet with the board that handled the trade caravans, acting as their spokesperson, explaining what had happened to the previous trade master and informing them of her desire to assume the role until a new one could be agreed upon.

When she returned she found Trinidad standing in one of the wider corridors, Riley's hand in hers, his brown eyes scanning the tunnel walls and the people who moved through them with the relentless curiosity of a child cataloguing a new world.

"Without you we would surely have died," Anjari said. Her voice was hoarse from dust and gratitude. "I want to thank you personally. If there is anything you ever need, know that my people are your people."

Trinidad turned toward the sound of her voice. Through Riley's eyes she could see the woman: tall, weather-hardened, every line on her face earned. "You are the one who should be thanked," Trinidad replied. "Your enduring strength and fortitude is what saved all our lives. I wish I had a way to repay you. But know that we will meet again."

"Yes," Anjari said, and a smile broke across her face like light through a crack. "I believe we will." She reached into the folds of her garment and produced a pack, heavy and full, which she pressed into Trinidad's hands. "The caravan gave this to me to give to you. A token of our appreciation. We wish it could be more. Everyone put something in; we took up a collection."

Trinidad felt the weight of it, the shapes within: hard and soft, angular and round. Through Riley's eyes she caught glimpses as he peered at the contents: spices wrapped in cloth, coils of dried snakeskin, bundles of tea leaves still fragrant despite their desiccation, an assortment of valuable, mostly nonperishable goods that would have been worth their weight in gold, were it not a world where gold no longer had value.

"Thank you," Anjari said, and pulled Trinidad into an embrace that was tighter and longer than either of them expected. When she pulled back her eyes were bright. "You have got me believing in destiny, Trinidad. And my own does not seem so bad anymore."

She turned and walked away. Trinidad listened to her footsteps recede down the corridor until they were absorbed by the ambient hum of the city: ventilation fans and distant voices and the shuffle of feet on stone.

The chaos from the arena had not yet reached this part of the city, but it was coming. It moved through the tunnels like contagion: first a whisper, then a current, then something that could not be outrun. A person ran past, breathing hard, his sandals slapping the stone in a rhythm that had no discipline, only flight. Two women stopped in the corridor and began whispering with their heads drawn close, hands gripping each other's forearms. A man running a stall — dried meats and salvaged bolts of cloth — abandoned everything where it was and walked away with the hurried gait of someone who had received word of a death.

Then, as though some invisible levee had broken, the flood arrived. The murmurs became voices, the voices became shouts, the shouts became a keening that rose through the tunnels and pooled against the stone ceilings like smoke. Through Riley's eyes Trinidad watched as a man came running through the corridor with a woman in his arms, her skin hanging from her bones like a torn garment, the burns so severe that what remained of her was barely recognizable as a body that had once been whole. The smell reached them before the sight did: charred cloth and copper and something sweeter, something worse. Another stumbled past, his clothing fused to his skin in patches, his hands held before him as though he were carrying something invisible he dared not set down. A group moved through the tunnel in a dazed and ghostly procession, their eyes carrying the vacant stare of minds that had temporarily severed their connection to the bodies they inhabited. A woman sank to her knees in the corridor and began to wail, a sound so raw and unadorned that it needed no context, no explanation, no words. The oldest sound humankind knew. The sound the first mother made over the first dead child, unchanged, because grief does not evolve.

All of them coming from the direction of the arena.

Riley's eyes tracked every movement, every face, every stumbling figure that emerged from the smoke-choked eastern tunnels. His small body pressed against Trinidad's side, and she could feel through the bond the way his mind was cataloguing what it saw — not with understanding, but with the raw fidelity of a camera that records everything and judges nothing.

Stay close to me, Riley.

He stepped back and slipped under her arm, and the folds of her gown draped over him like a wing. She held him there, feeling his heartbeat against her ribs, fast and frightened and alive.

"I'm too late," she said softly. To no one. To herself. To the corridor full of wounded strangers who could not hear her above their own grief.

In Sid Mantelis, Lagos walked with the hurried stride of a man carrying news that burned in his mouth. The corridors of the cathedral city were wide and vaulted, lined with carved stone and lit by electrified sconces that cast a cold, sanctified glow across the floors — the light of a place designed not to illuminate but to diminish. Even the air was regulated, cool and faintly perfumed with incense, the scent of control dressed as devotion.

When he reached the Chief Cleric's salon, two Sentinella stood guard at the ornamental doors, massive men whose purpose was written in the breadth of their shoulders and the flatness of their gaze.

"I'm sorry, sir. He is not to be disturbed," one of them said, his voice carrying the bored authority of someone who delivered the same line a dozen times a day.

"He wants to be disturbed for this!" Lagos protested, his voice sharp enough to pierce through the carved wood.

A pause. Then, from within, rising as if from a great depth: "Open the doors." Leo's voice was sonorous and unhurried, the voice of a man who controlled the tempo of every room he entered and several he had never set foot in.

The Sentinella stepped aside, slowly, deliberately, making it clear that the permission had come from their master and not from Lagos. The initiate shoved past them and found Leo meeting with Lupus and Tike, two men whose bearing suggested they were accustomed to being the most important people in whatever room they occupied. The looks they turned on Lagos had the quality of heat — not enough to burn, but enough to be felt.

"Sir, you told me to come and inform you when the signal appeared again," Lagos said, the words tumbling out in a breathless rush.

"Yes." Leo raised his hand with the practiced grace of a conductor halting an orchestra mid-bar, and the room obeyed. "Gentlemen, if you will excuse me."

Lupus and Tike rose and left. As they passed Lagos they shared a glance that said everything their mouths did not. The doors closed behind them with the soft, definitive sound of stone meeting stone.

When they were alone, Lagos continued. "Sir, I noticed it only moments ago. The image had disappeared, and then it came back on." He paused, steadying himself. Leo's full attention had a weight to it that could carry a man under. "It looks like it is heading back in the same direction as before."

"Very good, Lagos. I will meet you in the sanctum momentarily. Go ahead. I will be right behind you." Leo placed a hand on the initiate's shoulder — less affection than ownership — and guided him toward the door.

When Lagos was gone and the doors were sealed, a thin smile developed across the Chief Cleric's face. It was the smile of a mechanism engaging, of gears finding their teeth.

"Where are you going?" he said to the empty room. The words drifted upward toward a ceiling carved to resemble the heavens but resembling, in this light, something closer to the lid of a coffin.

As the smoke dissipated and the air in the auditorium thinned from black to a bruised and churning gray, the faces of the screaming became visible, and they were worse than the screaming had suggested. The blast had turned the arena into a cathedral of ruin, its architecture inverted, the ceiling now the floor in places where beams had buckled and platforms had collapsed. The chaotic rush of bodies toward the exits had created a compression at the narrow wooden steps, a human bottleneck where those trying to help fought against the tide of those trying to escape, and between the two currents the bodies of the fallen were being ground against the concrete like grain between millstones. The air was thick with dust, ash, and a fine gray powder that coated everything — the wounded and the whole alike — giving the entire scene the washed-out pallor of a world photographed through gauze. Every breath tasted of char and calcium and the sharp metallic tang of blood aerosolized by heat.

ROGER moved into the devastation without hesitation, following the path that damage had carved. His senses — calibrated to detect the subtlest fluctuations in human physiology: heart rate, blood oxygen, the electrical impulses of a nervous system in distress — swept the wreckage with the systematic precision of a machine performing its primary function, identifying which instruments were still playing and which had fallen silent. He knew that working openly risked exposing what he was. But people were dying, and his directives did not allow inaction when lives could be saved. The programming ran deeper than caution, deeper than self-preservation, all the way down to the bedrock of what he had been made to be.

He knelt beside a man whose leg was bent at an angle no leg should describe and set the bone with hands that did not tremble because they could not. He moved to a woman whose breathing had become a thin, wet whistle and cleared her airway with the precision of a jeweler handling something irreplaceable. He pressed his palm to the chest of a child — a child, in this place, where children should never have been — and felt the heartbeat beneath his hand, faint but present, and he lifted the child and passed it to arms that were waiting, arms that had no skill but were willing, which was enough.

Gunther stood apart from the wreckage. He had positioned himself in the shadows near the back wall, his silk jacket dusted with ash, and he surveyed the damage not with grief but with arithmetic, tallying the cost, already scheming how to rebuild and who to punish. His small eyes moved from the charred platforms to the buckled walls to the bodies being carried out, and nothing in his expression suggested he saw a difference between any of them. They were all entries in the same ledger. The Po' Boys had done this. They had taken his weapons and turned them against his empire, and that debt would be collected, with interest, in flesh and fear. His jaw worked slowly, the muscles bunching and releasing beneath the skin. But his eyes never left ROGER. They tracked the robot's movements with the focused, acquisitive attention of a man who has just discovered something valuable in the wreckage.

Po had pulled Ivan from the rubble and was moving through the devastation with controlled urgency, assessing the damage with a fighter's eye — structural weaknesses, debris fields, the location of the fallen — and directing his efforts where they would do the most good. Rensa was hunting through the carnage for the twin pickpockets she and Dade had spoken to earlier, weaving between broken bodies and shattered planks, her hands turning rubble, her voice calling names that were swallowed by the din.

"We need to get as many of the injured to the hospital as we can," ROGER said, his voice cutting through the chaos with the clean authority of something that did not need to shout to be heard. "If they can walk, let them. If they cannot, we will need to make stretchers to carry them."

Po and several others fell in beside ROGER, and together they negotiated the terrible geography between the living and the dead, sorting one from the other with a decisiveness that was itself mercy: the mercy of certainty in a place where certainty had been blown apart. ROGER scanned the room, his lavender eyes seeing through the debris to the vital signs beneath, finding the living where they lay buried under the dead.

"Over here, a woman is pinned under a beam. Someone come help lift it off." He pointed, and those who had stayed to help moved as he directed, their hands finding purchase on the scorched wood, their backs straining against the weight, the shared effort binding them into something temporary and sacred — the congregation that forms in the aftermath of catastrophe.

"Over here, someone give me a hand!" Po shouted from across the auditorium. He was braced against a portion of wall that had crumbled in the blast, his legs trembling with the effort of holding it upright while someone, a man, conscious, bleeding from a gash across his forehead, wriggled free from beneath. Others came and they lifted together, and the man crawled out and lay on the stone floor, gasping, alive. Po did not pause to acknowledge it. He was already moving to the next sound, the next cry, the next hand reaching up from beneath the rubble.

They continued like that for some time, working in a grim rhythm that needed no coordination. The dust settled as they worked, coating their arms and faces, turning them all the same color — the gray of the wreckage itself — so that the rescuers became indistinguishable from the ruin they were pulling people out of. Eventually, when the only bodies remaining were the ones that no longer needed rescuing, they stopped. The auditorium was quieter now, the screaming reduced to a low collective moan.

"You two. Come with me." Gunther's voice arrived like something dragged across gravel, a sound that belonged to a throat lined with phlegm and old malice. He had not helped. He had not moved from his position near the back wall. He led Po and ROGER through what remained of the arena, past the shattered ring and the scorched platforms, past the stains on the stone that had been people an hour ago, into the back room where deals were brokered and fates were decided. It smelled of sweat and lamp oil and the sourness of a space where men conducted business they did not want witnessed.

Gunther circled ROGER the way a buyer circles livestock at auction, his eyes moving up and down with the slow, appraising deliberation of a man taking inventory of something he already considered his. "You have not been straight with me," he said, and the accusation was not angry. It was pleased. "I've seen one like you before. Several years ago." He stopped in front of ROGER and looked into those lavender eyes — that inhuman calm, that precision no amount of training could produce in a human being. "Called itself a ROMAN. That what you are? A ROMAN?"

"No. As I said, my name is ROGER. I am a RObotic Genetic EngineeR," ROGER replied, his voice carrying the patient precision of someone who has explained this distinction before. "What you are describing is a RObot for Manufacturing, Architecture and iNfrastructure. A construction robot."

"So you cannot do that, huh? Repair things?" Gunther asked, and beneath the question was the machinery of calculation, the gears of a mind that saw everything in terms of what it could be used for, broken down, repurposed, sold.

"Well, technically I do repair things. I just repair human things," ROGER said flatly.

Po saw where this was going. "ROGER is not for sale," he said.

Gunther did not look at him. "Everything's for sale," he said. "Just got to find out what you are not willing to part with." Then he turned, slowly, and let his gaze settle on Po. The smile that moved across his face was not a smile at all. It was a door opening onto something dark. "Now, you are a good fighter, I will give you that. Might have been that you could have even beaten Ivan. We will never know. But what is more important to you right now? Your life, or this ROGER here?"

Po straightened. The muscles in his jaw tightened, a motion visible beneath the bruises and the blood, the body's involuntary response to a threat that the mind has not yet decided how to answer. "Are you threatening me?"

"Easy there." Gunther raised a hand, and the gesture was not a placation but a signal. His men came through the door behind them, filling the room with the sudden mass of bodies that meant violence, and between them they held Rensa and Dade, bound at the wrists, rags stuffed into their mouths. Rensa's green eyes burned like something chemical above the gag. Dade's face held an expression that looked wrong on it, foreign — not anger, not defiance, but something quieter and worse. Helplessness. "Perhaps I should sweeten the deal. Your life, as well as the lives of these two, for your ROGER. Sound better?"

The room went quiet.

"You do not have to do this, Po." ROGER's voice was calm, measured, carrying no trace of fear — because fear was not among his capabilities — but something lived in those words that was adjacent to it. "He will not harm me. I will go with him. Save yourselves."

Po looked at ROGER. He looked at Rensa, whose eyes above the gag held a rage so concentrated it seemed to vibrate — held back by something stronger: trust in Po to find the right move in a room where every move was wrong. He looked at Dade, whose helplessness was the most honest expression the man had ever worn.

Po nodded. A single, silent motion. A man compressing everything he felt into the smallest possible gesture, a movement so slight it could have been missed by anyone who was not watching for it. Everyone in that room was watching for it.

Gunther's men shoved Rensa and Dade forward, and ROGER walked to Gunther's side with the unhurried gait of someone who has made a calculation and accepted the result. No ceremony. No words. Just the simple, ugly mechanics of leverage. One party with power. One party without.

"This is not over," Po said. His voice was low, and it entered the room not as a threat but as a fact.

"Oh, I believe it is," Gunther replied, and left the room with his men and ROGER in tow. The door closed behind them with the finality of a sentence passed in a court that does not permit appeals.

Po moved to Rensa and pulled the gag from her mouth, then loosened the ropes at her wrists. He did the same for Dade. His hands were steady. His face was not.

"We have to go after them!" Rensa shouted, the words exploding from her the moment her mouth was free, venom and volume in equal measure, the force of it propelled by everything she had been unable to say while the rag was between her teeth.

"No." Po's voice was quiet but it filled the room completely. "We are too vulnerable now. With Ideo sick and John, Jayk, and Sarah still gone, we are spread too thin. We need a plan before we act." He shifted his gaze to Rensa, then to Dade. "Do I need to ask where the Po' Boys got the explosives?"

Rensa and Dade exchanged a glance. Children caught with their hands in the fire.

"It was my idea," Rensa confessed.

"No, it wasn't." Dade stepped forward. "It was actually my idea. Ren simply came to me about havin' access to the medicine we needed, and I came up with the idea of tradin' the explosives for other goods that we could use to get 'em."

"We never expected this, though!" Rensa added, her voice cracking on the last word.

"I believe you," Po said. He looked at them for a long moment. "You may be careless and impulsive, but you're not sadistic." He turned toward the door. "Let's get back to Ideo's parents. I'm sure they're worried about us."
