Po left while the others slept.

He pulled on one of Hong's hooded cloaks, eased the door shut behind him, and moved through the tunnels with the deliberate patience of a man who understood surveillance. He had one objective: find ROGER. The rest — the how of a rescue, the who of the backup — that could come later. First, he needed a location.

He found it by following a man wearing a red armband, one of Gunther's Hotheads, out of a casino near the damaged arena. The ring was a heap of splintered planking and torn fabric now, so the gang had relocated. Po kept his distance and matched the man's pace, shadowing him through three turns and down a service corridor until the Hothead descended a ramp into what had once been a parking garage.

The space was damp and open, concrete support beams sweating with mildew. Someone had sectioned off a corner with dividing walls and furnished it with tables and chairs — a crude office in the guts of a dead building. Off to one side, translucent plastic curtains partitioned a makeshift medical unit: a hospital bed, an IV stand, a metal pan lined with scalpels, bone saws, sutures, and a supply of syringes. Functional. Grim.

ROGER was inside, tending to a man who'd been injured in the previous day's violence.

"They don't like our kind there, Doc," the man said, wincing as ROGER dressed the wound. "We're lucky the boss got you."

"Keep this bandage on and change it regularly. If it develops an odor or any discharge, come see me immediately." ROGER's tone was clinical. Detached. The bedside manner of an intelligence that understood anatomy better than empathy — though the distinction, by then, was narrowing.

Po watched from the shadows. He weighed the odds of a solo extraction and dismissed them. ROGER wasn't going anywhere. The Hotheads had him working, not caged. That bought time. He retreated through the catacombs the same way he'd come, silent as he'd arrived, and returned to the Goku household. He shrugged out of his clothes and stretched out on the couch where Sun Yi had laid sheets and a blanket. He cocooned himself in the soft fabric and forced himself to sleep. There would be time. There had to be.

Out in the wastes, the deluge had finally broken.

Baba spoke first, words tumbling out faster than her thoughts could organize them. "What was that back there? How were you able — I thought we were done for!"

"So did I," Sarah admitted. "I wasn't sure it would work. I'm glad it did."

"But how? How can you use their weapons when nobody else can?"

"I'm still working that out myself."

"We all are," Jayk added quietly. "When I first found her, she could just do it. Picked one up and fired. Saved my life."

Baba went still. Her eyes widened. When she spoke again her voice had changed — lower, trembling with something that had been sealed shut for years.

"Then you're the one. Sovran's advisor foretold of you — the blind prophet, Trinidad, they called her. She said someone would come who could wield their weapons. It's you." Baba's knees buckled. She knelt in the wet sand, and what came out of her was not crying. It was release. Decades of it. The death of her grandfather. The subjugation of everyone she'd ever known. The constant, grinding certainty that nothing would ever change — cracking open all at once, flooding out of her body in a wail that had no words because words were too small for what she was letting go of.

Sarah crouched beside her and placed a hand on her shoulder. "Please don't cry. It might not be me. Let's not jump to conclusions."

Baba looked up at her, dragging a sleeve across her eyes. "It has to be you. You have the gift. Nobody else has ever done what you can do. Nobody."

"She's right, Sarah," Jayk said.

Sarah stood and took a breath. She didn't feel different. She didn't feel chosen. She hadn't asked for this and didn't want it. But it was also too late to give it back. "I just need a minute," she said, and walked away from the rest of them.

Jayk moved to Baba and spoke low enough that Sarah couldn't hear. "We haven't been making a big deal out of it. We don't want to pressure her."

"But she has to know!" Baba shook her head, emphatic. "So many people will be overjoyed. Their lives can start to get back to normal. This isn't about her — it's about the fate of the human race. It's her duty."

"I'm sure she'll accept it. In time. Right now, our job is to get back to Solos and save our friend."

Baba nodded. Wiped her face. Stood. "Then let's get going."

They crossed the wasteland valley in silence, four figures picking their way across terrain that offered nothing and forgave less. When the ruins of the old city finally rose against the horizon, the architecture stopped them.

Steel and glass. Towers built when humanity still believed it could engineer its way out of anything. The structures stood as monuments to an age of technological confidence — anthropocentric, uncompromising — preserved now under layers of desert sand and desiccated vegetation. They were both a warning and a testament. Evidence of what humans could build, and a record of what that building had cost. Beneath these husks, invisible to the sky, a community of several hundred lived in absolute secrecy. Masked by the wreckage of a nearly extinct civilization.

The group descended through the abandoned subway entrance, through sewer corridors and past the dormant power station, deep into the catacombs until they reached the single guarded doorway into Solos.

The doorman recognized Baba and waved her through. "Thank you," she said, turning to present Sarah, but Jayk stepped between them.

"It's been a long trip. We're exhausted. How about we save the introductions for another day? Our celebrity needs sleep."

Baba hesitated, then conceded. They entered the city and were heading toward the restaurant when Baba stopped.

"I need to check on something. You go ahead — I'll meet you at the Red Fin." She turned toward the Caravan and Trading Commission office near the entrance. Solos was the central hub of the Spice Road, and while it operated as a voluntary community, the safety of those who traveled the routes required a degree of regulation. The trading board certified caravans and approved trade masters. Baba had been on the waiting list. She figured as long as she was here, she might as well check.

The trio continued to the Red Fin. The man at the counter told them Sun Yi was not available but offered to send a runner.

"That'll take too long," Jayk said. "We have medicine for Ideo. We've been traveling for days to get it back to her. Can the runner just take us to the Goku home?"

Recognition crossed the man's face. He spoke in rapid Japanese to Ichiro, the runner, then turned back with a bow. "My apologies. Please, follow Ichiro."

They followed.

The Goku household opened its arms.

Rensa reached them first — pulling Jayk into a hug that was half relief, half reprimand for being gone so long. Po clasped Jayk on the shoulder. Dade gripped his hand and held it, firm and sincere.

"A lot's happened since you left," Dade said. His brogue softened the weight of what came next. "I just want you to know ... yer alright in my book."

Then Sun Yi was there, padding through the group with quiet authority, and she wrapped her arms around Jayk with the kind of embrace that doesn't require words.

"I have the root." Jayk reached into his pack. "I didn't know how much you'd need, so I brought the whole plant."

Sun Yi took it from him, examining the stems and roots with practiced fingers. "This is good, Jayk. Now, we brew the tea. Come with me." She turned to the others. "Everyone sit. Be comfortable." To Ichiro she spoke in rapid Japanese, sending him back to the Red Fin for ramen. He bowed deeply and left.

"Now," Sun Yi said, leading Jayk into the kitchen, "I show you the good you have done."

She pulled a pot from beneath the basin and twisted the spigot of a faucet connected to a copper pipe that ran up through the ceiling. Water filled the pot in a thin, steady stream. She lit the gas stove with a small clicker and set the pot over the flame. Then she carried the plant to the island table and began separating root from stem with a silver knife, each cut deliberate, precise.

"The root of the Raceweed is poisonous," she said, carving the root into translucent wafers. "But poison is not always bad. Used in the right way, even things that are dangerous to us can save our lives." The knife never paused. "Life is not always black and white, Jayk. Many times, good things can be bad for us. Bad things can be good for us." She placed the last thin slice on the table and looked up at him. "Only wisdom remains constant."

In the living room, Po briefed Sarah and John on what they'd missed. ROGER had been taken by Gunther's gang. The weapons deal had collapsed with the arena.

"I tracked one of them back to where they're keeping him," Po said. "Later, we'll go together and get him out." Then he paused. "I almost forgot — there are two new people you need to meet. Trinidad and Riley. They're probably still asleep."

As if summoned, Riley appeared in the doorway. He teetered in yawning, rubbing his eyes with both fists. Trinidad followed behind him, one hand resting on his shoulder.

"How could we sleep with all this excitement?" Trinidad's blind eyes didn't search the room — Riley's did that for her, transmitting every face, every detail directly into her mind. "Sarah. It's truly an honor. And John — even more handsome in the flesh." She smiled warmly. "My name is Trinidad. This young man is Riley." She squeezed Riley's shoulders with both hands. "I've been waiting to meet you for many, many years."

Sarah studied her. "How do you know about us? And you've been waiting years?"

"Indeed. I have had visions of you both for quite some time." Trinidad tilted her head. Riley was scanning the room, his gaze moving from face to face. "And one other. Where is Jayk?"

"In here," Jayk called from the kitchen. He walked into the living room, wiping his hands. "You're Trinidad. The blind prophet from Sid Mantelis."

It wasn't a question.

"I am," she said. "And it is truly an honor. Finally."

"I've heard a great deal about you." Jayk extended his hand, then realized she couldn't see the gesture. Trinidad reached out and found it anyway. She smiled — the kind of smile that comes from knowing something the other person hasn't figured out yet.

"Well," Po said, breaking the stillness. "Now that we've all met, we should decide what to do about ROGER. But first — I'm sure our new arrivals could use rest."

"Riley and I have slept enough," Trinidad offered. "If Sarah and John would like the room, we'd be happy to give it up."

"That would be wonderful," Sarah said. "We could both use a real bed." She stood and headed toward the back bedroom. John followed.

"When the food arrives, we'll come get you," Po called after them.

Jayk excused himself and returned to the kitchen, drawn back to Sun Yi's quiet ritual. "How will you know when it's ready?" he asked. "And when it's safe?"

Sun Yi didn't look up from the pot. "Some things in life come from knowledge. Others come from experience." She paused. "When you know, you know." Then she turned to him and her expression softened. "But you will watch, Jayk. If you watch, you will see. Then you will know."

Jayk watched.

In the living room, Rensa brought the conversation back around. "So what did you find out about ROGER?"

Po laid out what he'd seen. The parking garage. The medical setup. ROGER working on injured Hotheads. The guards. The lack of urgency in their security — they weren't expecting trouble.

"Have you met me?" Rensa said, cracking her knuckles. "Trouble's what I do."

Dade grinned. "Dat's de understatement of de century, to be honest."

"We wait for Sarah and John," Po said, steering them back. "Sarah's our leverage. If they see the weapon, they'll hand ROGER over without a fight. Hopefully."

Behind the locked bedroom door, Sarah and John stood in a room decorated with Japanese silk paintings of seikatsu culture, fragile jade curios, and bedclothes covered in shodō calligraphy — thick brush strokes that made the fabric look like scripture.

"There's only one bed," John noted.

"I see that." Sarah looked up at him with a half-smile. "I guess we'll have to share. You don't mind, do you?"

"Not at all." He turned her gently and slid her coat off her shoulders. She wriggled her right arm free and he tossed the heavy jacket onto the floor.

"Now let me help you." She turned back and began untucking his shirt. He pulled it over his head and she studied his frame — the muscle, the scars, the geography of a body shaped by survival. She traced a finger from his shoulder down to his wrist.

"You know," she said quietly, "if I had both my arms, I could hold you close to me."

He looked down into her green eyes and brushed a strand of tawny hair from her face. "Then I'll just have to hold you in mine instead."

He pulled her in. They stood there for a long moment, bodies pressed together, neither one willing to break it.

She pressed her cheek against his bare chest and breathed in. "You smell good," she whispered, running her hand through his thick dark hair. "I wish we could have more moments like these."

"We should probably take advantage of them, then." He glanced toward the bed.

She rose on her toes and kissed him — slow, deliberate, like she was memorizing the shape of it. "We should," she murmured against his mouth, biting his lower lip gently.

Then she stepped back, pulled off her tank top, and stood bare before him. Paused. "Oh — I should probably disconnect this thing. Don't want it going off accidentally." She unplugged the cable from the microprocessor attached to the conducting sphere of her cannon, her wicked grin returning. "There. That's—"

Before she could finish, John pulled her into him — chest to chest, skin to skin, warm and whole and alive — and they tumbled onto the bed together.

In the kitchen, the boiling water had turned the thinly sliced roots into translucent discs, waxy and pale as old coins.

"You see now, Jayk?"

"I do."

"Now." Sun Yi lifted the tea service tray and placed the root pieces into the bottom of a cup, one by one, like small offerings. She poured water from a separate pot into the teapot. "We will use a small amount at first. She has been sick a long time. Her body cannot handle much."

She nodded to Jayk, lifted the tray, and carried it to Ideo's room. He followed. She set the tray on the bedside table and placed a hand on her daughter's forehead.

"Still very warm. The tea will make her warmer before it makes her better. We must stay and watch over her until she wakes."

She poured the steeped water into the cup. Steam curled upward. They waited.

Hong returned with Ichiro and the food. He welcomed Trinidad warmly. "Ms. Trinidad — it's nice to finally meet you. You're a legend in our community. It is an honor to have you as a guest." He bowed deeply, hands together, and carried the food into the kitchen. He spoke to Ichiro in quick Japanese, who began preparing the ramen for service. "I must get back to the restaurant," Hong said, already moving toward his daughter's room for a quick look. He paused in the doorway.

Inside, Jayk had Ideo's head cradled in one hand, tilting her up gently while Sun Yi held the tea to her lips. When it dripped from her mouth, Sun Yi wiped her chin with a cloth as delicately as if she were handling glass.

"How is she?" Hong asked.

"We have just begun. This is Jayk — the man who saved your daughter's life."

Hong watched Jayk holding Ideo's head as gently as if she were made of something that could break at a whisper. "Thank you, Mr. Jayk. Words cannot express my gratitude." He stood there a moment, staring at his daughter. Then he straightened. "I must go. But please — any change in her condition, inform me immediately."

He walked out. The door closed softly behind him.

In the living room, Ichiro laid out bowls of ramen. Hong stopped at the door on his way out and remembered something.

"A young lady named Baba came by the restaurant. She asked about Jayk, John, and Sarah. She said not to worry — she'd find her own way home. And to tell Sarah it was an honor to meet her."

Po sipped ramen from his bowl. "I'll pass it along. Thank you, Mr. Hong."

Hong and Ichiro left together. The rest sat eating.

After a few minutes Jayk emerged from the back room. "I thought I smelled something." Rensa got up without a word and went to make him a bowl. He sat down beside Po. "Do we have a plan to rescue ROGER?"

"Take Sarah as intimidation. Show the weapon. Demand him back. Hopefully it doesn't go further than that." Po kept it simple.

"What are we waiting for?"

"Sarah and John are ..." Rensa walked in carrying his bowl, a sly smile pulling at the corner of her mouth. "... occupied at the moment."

In the bedroom, the world had contracted to two bodies and the small warm space between them.

John and Sarah lay tangled together, legs intertwined, her face pressed against his chest, his arms surrounding her. He could smell her hair.

"You smell like nature," he said.

She looked up. "What does that mean?"

"Rain. Earth. Sweat. Trees." He struggled with it. "I don't know how to describe it. It's more like a feeling. You smell like life." He paused. "You smell like home."

She pressed closer. "Mmmm. I like that. I smell like home."

Her eyes found his. "You have tiny flecks of gold around your pupil. Did you know that?"

"No."

"They're beautiful." She reached up and lifted a strand of dark hair from his face.

"We should probably get back to the group."

She squeezed him tighter. "No. Let this moment last forever." She held on for a few more breaths, then let go. They got up and began dressing.

"Ugh." She held her coat at arm's length. "This doesn't smell like home. We need to have this cleaned."

"I'm sure the Gokus know someone. They seem well connected here. Almost like royalty."

John unlocked the door. Sarah caught his arm.

"Wait. You have something on your face." She tilted his head toward hers and kissed him — long, slow, final. "There. That's better."

They walked into the living area where the others sat eating and talking.

"I thought you were going to come get us when the food arrived," Sarah said.

"We thought you could use some alone time," Rensa said, with a wink that was the opposite of subtle.

"Real subtle, Ren," Dade said.

Rensa shrugged and stood. "Here, let me get you some ramen."

Jayk was seated next to Po and Trinidad. His eyes stayed on his bowl. On the table. On the wall. Anywhere that wasn't Sarah.

He set his ramen down. "I'm suddenly not hungry." He stood. "I need to go for a walk. Clear my head."

The door closed behind him.

Silence filled the room.

Po broke it. "Hong mentioned a girl named Baba?"

"Oh my God — we forgot about Baba!" Sarah turned to John, her hand over her mouth.

"She's fine. She said she'd find her own way home, and that it was an honor to meet you."

Sarah sat back, processing that. "A lot happened out there. I had to use the weapon to take down an alien airship. It was headed straight for us. Baba saw it, and now she thinks I'm some kind of savior." She looked at Trinidad. "Something about a prophecy?"

Trinidad was still for a moment. Then she spoke, and the room leaned in.

"She alone can hold the alien light; and use its power to end the fight."

"That's a stanza from the prophecy given to me," Trinidad said.

"Given by whom?" Po asked.

"By birth. It's a part of the universe." Trinidad folded her hands in her lap. "If you are patient, you can hear the world speaking. When it does, I sit as still as I can and listen. Most of it I remember. Some of it I've lost. But it's always there." She paused. "The world is in pain. But it knows it will be healed. So I listen, and it tells me what I need to know."

She let the silence hold for a beat.

"That's what brought me here. To you. I don't yet know why I must be here. But I know that I must."

Outside, Jayk walked without direction. His feet carried him back toward the small café where he'd met the old man — the one whose advice had led them to the Raceweed. He wanted to thank him. He wanted to talk to someone who had no stake in any of this.

The cook recognized him. "Is the old man still here?" Jayk asked. "The one I paid for the room and meals?"

"That old man never used the room. Never came back after you left. Haven't seen him since."

Jayk let that settle.

"How about one of those meals I promised him? You hungry?"

"Starving."

"Grab a seat."

Jayk sat at one of the small tables outside the kitchen and watched the cook work — tossing vegetables and meat into a wok over an open pit, moving with the practiced efficiency of someone who'd done this ten thousand times. Dead snakes hung from the ceiling like drying herbs. Jayk's stomach made a private negotiation with his conscience: I hope that's snake and not rat.

The cook brought out a plate within minutes. It looked good. It smelled better.

"This is wonderful," Jayk said, already three bites in. "You have a gift."

"It's snake," the cook said.

"Oh."

"I noticed you looking. I don't use rat. Others will, but you have to be careful. I only use snake." He wiped his hands on his apron. "Good meat."

Jayk ate quickly. With a full belly, the world looked different — not better, exactly, but more manageable. He decided to head back.

He took the slow route. Past the stalls of clothing and jewelry and tchotchkes. Past the neighborhood bars and launderers, the games of chance, the barkers crying their wares. The underground city of Solos hummed with the business of survival made habitual, and for a moment, watching it all pass, Jayk understood why people stayed here. It was dangerous and lawless and cramped. But it was alive. And it was theirs.

He rounded the corner to the Goku household — a steel door set into brick, opening into what was really just a few interlocking rooms carved from the underground. Once it had been a substation of some kind, maintenance or security. The Gokus had turned it into a home. Several bedrooms, a washroom, a kitchen, a study. Impeccably decorated by Sun Yi and Hong. Japanese silk paintings, jade curios, calligraphy on the bedclothes. Every surface said: we chose to live here, and we chose to live well. It was a home in every sense of the word, carved from a disused subway station deep beneath a dead city.

When Jayk returned, everyone was preparing to leave.

"I just want to check on Ideo," he said, and went into her room.

She was still in bed, still sleeping. But the change was visible — color returning to her skin, the tension in her face eased. The Raceweed was working.

Jayk sat on the edge of the bed and took her hand.

"I don't know if you can hear me." He paused. "I mean — I'm sure you can. I hope you can." He rubbed his thumb across her knuckles. "I just want you to know that we miss you. It's not the same without you. So ... hurry up and get better."

Sun Yi was standing in the doorway. She'd been listening.

"You are a very good man, Jayk," she said softly, padding up behind him and placing her hand on his shoulder. "Thank you for what you have done."

"You're welcome, Mrs. Goku. I hope she comes around." He stood, still holding Ideo's hand, then gently set it down on the blanket. "If she wakes up while I'm gone, tell her ..." He searched for the right words. Didn't find them. "Just tell her I'll be back soon."

He walked out into the living room where the others stood ready.

They had a friend to rescue.
