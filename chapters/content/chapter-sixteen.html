They took a small table in the corner. The candle flames continued their restless dance against the walls, and the shadows moved in a rhythm that had no source and no resolution, a beat that repeated without arriving anywhere. Po sat with his back to the wall, an old habit born of a life spent in rooms where the door was always the most dangerous feature. Rensa settled beside him, and Dade across, and ROGER took the remaining chair with a stillness that belonged to no living thing. Sun Yi's daughter Mei, no older than ten, brought their bowls with both hands extended, the way her mother had taught her, an offering, not a service.

With Ideo's life suspended on a thread too thin to measure, John, Jayk, and Sarah did not wait. They left the city by the same passage through which they had arrived, emerging into the wastes as the first edge of sunlight crested the eastern ridge. The sky above them was enormous and empty, making a person feel simultaneously exposed and forgotten, and the air already carried the dry electric taste of heat to come.

"We can travel until the sun reaches its highest point, but then we should rest," John said. He walked with the compact efficiency of a man whose body had been engineered for exactly this, movement across hostile terrain, economy of effort, the conservation of resources that might mean the difference between arriving and not arriving. Sun Yi had provided them with a small parcel of nourishment: dried vegetables wrapped in cloth, two canteens of water, and a handful of salted root strips that tasted of nothing but survival. They would need to ration every bite if they hoped for it to last.

The map Jayk carried was crude, hand-drawn on a scrap of hide by the old man in the market, with distances indicated by vague hatchmarks that could have meant hours or days. The old man had simply told Jayk it would be a few days' trek to the mountain, a statement that carried the imprecision of someone who had not made the journey himself in a very long time, if ever. Beyond those scraps of information, they were blind.

The city of Solos sat in the valley on the far side of a large mountain chain. Their march would take them across the widest part of that valley until they reached the opposite wall, where the mountains rose like the spine of some colossal buried animal. Once there, they would need to climb, how far, no one could say, until they found the plant. It bore clusters of white flowers and dark purple berries, juicy and deceptive, beauty that concealed a lethal interior. The root they needed was poisonous. However, if one brewed it into a tea, one could extract just enough of the toxin, decocting it slowly for its medicinal properties. A medicine made of poison. The wound and the remedy growing from the same stem.

They walked in something close to silence for the first hour, their boots finding a shared tempo against the baked earth, a rhythm that none of them established and none of them broke. Jayk moved with a pilgrim's patience, his long stride unhurried, his eyes sweeping the terrain ahead with the unconscious vigilance of a man who had spent years learning how to walk through land that wanted him dead. Sarah kept pace on his left, her weapon arm concealed beneath the heavy coat she wore draped across her shoulders. It hung from her frame like a vestment she had not yet earned. John brought up the rear, watchful, his gaze returning again and again to the sky.

As the sun climbed higher, the heat thickened. The mountains shimmered in the distance, a mirage that retreated with every step, and John called a halt.

"We're out in the middle of nowhere. Nothing will protect us from enemies, Sky Reapers or whoever robbed us back there. Any suggestions?" He looked to Sarah and Jayk, though his tone suggested he had already accepted whatever answer came.

Jayk surveyed the terrain, flat, featureless, offering nothing in the way of concealment. A few scraggly shrubs clung to the soil as though embarrassed to be there. "We could sleep in shifts. You two get some rest while I keep watch," he said. "Then we trade off."

"Sounds like a plan. If you see anything, let us know." John lowered his pack and Sarah did the same, both of them settling onto the hard ground with the practiced ease of people who had learned to sleep anywhere. The earth was warm beneath them, radiating the day's accumulated heat upward through their backs.

"Here. Cover yourselves with these, just in case." Jayk handed them some leafy twigs he had plucked from a nearby shrub, brittle things that smelled of dust and something faintly bitter.

John and Sarah accepted them politely and set them aside without discussion, a small, shared act of futile optimism, acknowledging the gesture while silently agreeing it would do nothing to save them if the sky opened up with enemies.

Sarah lay down on her right side. John lay behind her, and she turned her head. "Come over to this side," she said. "I have to sleep on my right, obviously, otherwise it's just unmanageable." She adjusted the weapon arm against the ground, finding some arrangement that did not press the mechanisms into the dirt. "Which is just fine because I've always slept on my right side." A pause. A recalibration. "I mean, she always slept on her right side. I have no real experience of what I have always done. They are just her memories."

John moved to her opposite side so she could see him. He settled into the ground and looked at her, and for a moment neither of them spoke. He was the only person on the planet who could understand what she meant, that strange doubling, the dissonance of inheriting a life one had not lived. That one thing, that shared frequency, bonded them together more than any two people left on Earth. To feel known. To feel understood by another human being. Such a small thing, a signal barely above the noise, but perhaps what everyone needed in life. "Have you been dreaming lately?" he asked.

"No. I did when I first came out of the cloning chamber, just once, but I haven't since." She stared past him at the sky, which offered nothing. "I wonder if that's normal. Or if it's just a product of this environment." She was quiet for a beat. "How could we know what's normal, anyway? None of this is normal."

"Certainly takes some getting used to," John said.

Sarah looked into his eyes, russet brown, flecked with something warmer near the iris, the way good earth looks when you turn it, and felt the weight of her exhaustion arrive all at once, like a wave that had been building since the cloning chamber. She closed her eyes. He closed his. They both slept there for a long while, peacefully, while the world continued spinning in its confusing and abnormal way, and the wind moved across the flats in long, restless passes that rose and fell and rose again without ever quite settling into silence.

After several hours the oppressive heat began to erode even Jayk's considerable endurance. The boredom was worse than the sun, a blank, featureless vigil over a blank, featureless plain, and his eyelids grew heavier with each pass of his gaze across the horizon. He went to John and woke him with a gentle hand on his arm. "John. Your turn to watch the camp for a while."

John surveyed the sky, gauging the angle of the sun. He had slept long enough. He looked over at Sarah, who had not moved, her breathing slow and even, her weapon arm tucked against her body like something she was protecting. "We'll let Sarah keep sleeping. The past couple of days have been rough on her body. She deserves the rest."

Jayk nodded. He did not argue. He lowered himself to the ground near where Sarah lay and watched her for a moment as sleep pulled at him, her face calm, her lips slightly parted, the tension that she carried in her jaw when she was awake entirely absent. He thought of better days. He could not remember them specifically, that was the nature of his affliction, the holes in his memory like missing notes in a melody he could almost hum, but he believed they existed somewhere, behind him, and that they might exist again somewhere ahead. He closed his eyes and let that belief carry him under.

John walked a short distance away to relieve himself, then took up his position on the low rise where Jayk had been sitting. The valley spread out before him in every direction, vast and indifferent, the mountains ahead still impossibly far. He sat and watched and listened to nothing.

The sun began its descent behind the western mountains, painting the ridge in shades of copper and ash, and at that exact moment John heard it, the unmistakable low-frequency hum of an alien airship. Hovering close to the ground, moving slowly, and the sound of it traveled across the valley like a bass note that vibrated in his chest before it reached his ears.

He went to Sarah and Jayk and woke them with sharp, quiet urgency. "Get up. Get up." The words came out in a hoarse whisper, stripped of everything but command.

They roused immediately, soldiers' reflexes, even from sleep, and he pointed. The ship hung in the distance, too far to detect them but too close to hide from.

"What do we do?" Sarah whispered.

"Get as low to the ground as you can. Lay face down, bury your head if you have to. Cover every inch of your body and stay as still as possible. Hopefully they'll pass by without recognizing us as a threat." Jayk was already pressing himself flat as he spoke, and the others followed. They covered their bodies with whatever they had, the leafy twigs, the packs, the coat, and lay motionless while the ship drifted past, its shadow passing over them like the hand of something that could not be bothered to close its fist.

The ship passed without incident, continuing in the exact direction they were headed.

Jayk lifted his head. "Probably a transport ship carrying supplies. Even if they had seen us, they probably wouldn't have done anything. I've had ships like that pass right over me before without stopping."

"Still," John said. "Did you see the direction it was headed?"

"Yeah. Right where we're going." Jayk pulled out the map and studied it, turning it as though a different angle might reveal information that was not there. "The old man didn't mention anything about an alien garden in our path. So, either it's new,"

"Or the old man lied." John finished. He stared off toward the mountain, which had grown no closer. "This mission just got a lot harder."

In the Goku household, Po, Rensa, Dade, and ROGER settled into their borrowed quarters with the careful gratitude of people who understood that hospitality, in this world, was a form of courage. The house was modest but well-kept, its walls hung with small tapestries that Sun Yi had woven from salvaged thread, geometric patterns in faded blues and greens that gave the rooms a warmth their bare dimensions would not have suggested. A ceramic vase on a shelf near the entryway held three dried flowers, stems brittle, petals long since leached of color, arranged with a care that suggested they were not decoration but memorial.

ROGER had installed himself at Ideo's bedside almost immediately and had not moved since. He sat in a wooden chair beside her sleeping mat, his posture perfect, his lavender eyes tracking the faint rise and fall of her breathing with a precision no human physician could match. The others met with Hong Goku in the main room.

"Your home is much nicer than I expected," Po said, and he meant it. The walls were solid, the floor swept clean, and through a narrow window he could see a small courtyard where someone had planted herbs in clay pots arranged in a row. "Amazing that this city is able to exist here, completely undetected by the invaders."

Hong sat across from them on a low cushion, his hands resting on his knees. He was a slender man with careful eyes and a quiet authority that did not require volume. "We are very fortunate. "We are aware that anything can happen at a moment's notice. The world can change in the blink of an eye. You should always be grateful for what you have." He paused. "I am very grateful to you for bringing our daughter back to us, Mr. Po."

"Mr. Hong, I want you to know that we did nothing to protect your daughter, other than accept her and provide her with a place to sleep. Your daughter is an extremely strong woman. We're honored to have her." Po stated this with a directness that was itself a form of respect, he would not inflate his role in another man's family story.

"I appreciate your candor, Mr. Po. But you must understand, I lost my daughter years ago, when she was just a child. I am not ready yet to accept her as a woman." Your wife wouldn't take it as payment for the food, so we're at a loss as to its value."

"Where did you get this?" Hong took the coin and turned it in the lamplight.

"A man named Gunther, at the fighting pits, gave it to me. At the time I assumed it was payment for beating one of his fighters. But now I'm not so sure."

That night Po and the others slept, knowing the next day would be challenging. Po and ROGER had arranged to share a room, leaving Dade and Rensa to share one of their own. Po had introduced ROGER as his personal trainer when they met with Gunther, and the charade needed to hold. These were dangerous people who operated by a calculus of leverage, and if it were discovered that ROGER was something far more valuable than a fitness coach, that he was, in fact, not a human being at all, extracting themselves unharmed from Gunther's territory would become considerably more difficult. This was Gunther's ground. They played by Gunther's rhythm, or they did not play at all.

ROGER, who was supposed to share the room with Po, could not bring himself to do so. He preferred instead to sit beside Ideo and watch over her through the night, monitoring her vital signs with the quiet attentiveness of a machine that did not need sleep and a mind that chose not to rest. The candle beside her bed burned low, its flame guttering in a draft that crept through the wall, and ROGER adjusted her blanket without being asked.

Po awoke the next morning before the others and began his Kata, the system of body movement he executed to keep his technique in proper form and to prepare for the fights ahead. He moved through the forms in the half-light of the room, his bare feet silent on the stone floor, each strike and block flowing into the next with a tempo that was entirely his own. When he finished he cleaned up and met the others for the food Sun Yi had prepared.

"How is she doing, Mr. ROGER?" Sun Yi stood in the doorway, one hand resting on the frame, her body angled as though she might need to catch herself.

"I'll do my best," he replied with a smile that did not reach his eyes.

"Let's get going." Po nodded to Rensa and they stepped through the doorway into the noise and dust of Solos, leaving ROGER alone with the girl whose breathing he counted like a metronome.

Out in the wastes, John, Jayk, and Sarah had been on their guard for two more days. They walked with their eyes divided, half on the sky, watchful for enemy vessels, half on the ground, searching for any sign of edible plants or water. The mountains looked deceptively close, the way grief looks manageable from a distance, but the valley floor stretched on and on, each kilometer indistinguishable from the last.

Reaching the mountains mattered. But finding the plant mattered more. And between them and the plant, now lay the question of the garden.

John and Sarah had been talking more on this leg of the journey, conversations that happen when two people have exhausted small talk and arrived at the harder, quieter territory underneath. Their words had the quality of two people learning to walk in step, tentative at first, a thought offered and tested, then gradually finding the pace that felt natural.

"She used to play the cello for the London Philharmonic," Sarah said. She was not looking at John when she said it, but ahead, at the mountains, as though the memory were projected onto them. "I can remember the vibrations of the instrument against her body. The way it hummed through her sternum. The smell of the city afterward, rain on clean stone, linden trees blooming along the Southbank, the particular damp of the concert hall." She paused. "The way her fingers ached in winter, and how that ache was welcome because it meant she had played well."

"I remember the food," John said. "The taste of good barbecue. A nice juicy steak. So strange to have all these memories, birthdays and friends and places that felt like home."

"Surreal. It happened, but it did not happen to us." Sarah let the paradox sit between them without trying to resolve it. "But that's not who we are now. And we'll never be those people again. We'll never go to those places again, no matter what happens. I'm pretty sure my apartment building doesn't even exist anymore."

"You know the strangest part, to me, anyway, is scars that no longer exist. Aches and pains I used to have but don't anymore. Almost like a phantom limb. I expect the pain to be there, but it's not." John stopped walking. The words had left his mouth before the implication caught up to them, and when it did he turned to her with visible regret. "I didn't mean, when I said that, it was just an analogy. I'm sorry."

Sarah did not break stride. "Relax, big guy. I know what you meant." She glanced back at him, and the smile she gave was strained but genuine, the smile of someone who had decided, consciously, that this particular wound would not be allowed to fester between them. "Not like I don't know how strange this is. For both of us. No hard feelings." She lifted the weapon arm slightly, a gesture so casual it almost disguised the weight of what she was saying. "This is the mother of all phantom limbs, because I still have to use it. And if I'm not careful, I could blow my own leg off." The strain in her smile softened into something closer to acceptance. "We're all getting used to this new normal, I suppose."

They continued in that vein for two more days, talking, resting, rationing their water in sips that grew smaller with each passing hour, until on the morning of the third day they crested a low rise and saw the alien garden spread before them at the foot of the mountain.

Small. That was the first thing John registered, and it gave him a cold, clinical hope. Compared to the enormous forest they had traversed on the way to Solos, that cathedral of alien flora that had risen around them like the architecture of a nightmare, this was a nursery. The alien vegetation stood no higher than bushes, stunted and newly planted, their tendrils still thin, their root systems still shallow. A work camp sat at its center, guarded by a handful of Sky Reapers whose silhouettes moved between the rows of dwarf shrubs like acolytes tending a garden they did not understand but served without question.

The three of them knelt behind a small berm, pressing themselves into the earth. John studied the camp through narrowed eyes, his mind already running calculations, force ratios, sight lines, avenues of approach. "This is a small garden compared to the last two we've seen. Most likely it was just begun recently," he said. "We'll need to distract them somehow. We don't have the weapons or manpower to launch an effective campaign against them right now."

"What about the people inside?" Jayk asked. His voice carried a weight that had nothing to do with volume. He could see them, human figures moving among the alien vegetation, their bodies bent, their movements slow with exhaustion. Brothers and sisters. Members of the human family, imprisoned in a garden that was not theirs.

John's jaw tightened. "I wish we could do something to help them," he said, and the sadness in his voice was genuine, though it sat beside the calculation like two notes played simultaneously, neither canceling the other. "We can come back once we help Ideo and get the weapons we need. But right now we can do nothing." He was accustomed to making difficult decisions where human lives were concerned, decisions that required a man to hold compassion and pragmatism in the same hand and squeeze. It did not sit well with Jayk. To Jayk, every captive was a parishioner he had failed, though he could not have said why the word occurred to him.

But he knew John was right. Especially considering that Jayk had bartered his blade for food and lodging and was himself weaponless, a pilgrim arriving at the gates of the enemy with nothing but his hands and his faith that the journey had purpose.

"Not really. Except that they only grow on the mountain and not in the valley."

"Probably an altitude requirement of some sort, then. The higher you can get, the better. You should get a good view of the vegetation from above, easier to spot the white of the flowers against the rock." Jayk nodded.

"If everyone's ready, we should take the opportunity now." John looked at Sarah. "I don't know what sort of distraction you,"

"Don't worry." She patted him on the back, once, firmly, and rose to her feet. "I got this."

She walked toward the camp entrance with the unhurried stride of someone who had made a decision that could not be unmade. With a whip of her arm she cast off the coat from her shoulders, sending it rippling to the ground behind her like a shed skin, and the weapon arm was revealed in full, strange and terrible and beautiful in the way that all instruments of precision are beautiful, a mechanism that had become part of her body and her body part of it.

Then, in one explosive motion, she dropped to her knees and fired.

The blast of electrically charged particles tore through the first beast's torso and struck the wall behind it, sending a shower of debris into the air. She paused for the span of a single heartbeat, just long enough to register what she had done, to feel the frequency of the weapon still humming in her bones, and that pause gave the second creature time to fix its shoulder cannons on her position.

She fired again. The blast sheared its arm off at the shoulder and the harness buckled with it, one of the mounted cannons tearing free and clattering to the ground, spinning end over end before coming to rest in the dust.

"Run, Jayk, NOW!" She was on her feet and moving toward the entrance before the echo of her own voice had faded.

Jayk ran. He launched himself over the berm and hit the ground at a sprint, his legs driving him forward in a criss-crossing dash between the rows of alien vegetation. The tendrils reached for him, stunted arms grasping with the desperate hunger of things that had not yet grown large enough to kill, and he leapt and dodged and twisted past them, his feet finding purchase on the uncertain ground with the surefooted grace of a man whose body remembered how to walk through terrain his mind could not recall having crossed. By the time he reached the foot of the mountain, his lungs were burning and his hands were shaking, but he was through.

He looked back.

Five more of the creatures had emerged from within the camp, drawn by the chorus of destruction, unaware of what waited for them outside. Sarah spun, dropping flat to the ground as two bolts of crackling electricity passed through the space where her head had been a fraction of a second before. She returned fire from the ground, one blast catching a creature in the leg, dropping it sideways, another punching a hole clean through the skull of the one beside it.

"What," The syllable crawled from Jayk's lips, unfinished, inadequate. He stood at the base of the mountain and watched something he had no framework to understand.

Sarah moved through the battlefield like a song that had found its tempo and would not be stopped. She spun and twisted, leapt and dropped, the weapon arm discharging in sharp, cracking bursts that split the air like lightning finding ground. Her body and the weapon had achieved a unity that transcended the mechanical, a psychophysical resonance, the instrument and the player becoming indistinguishable, each movement flowing into the next without hesitation or thought. Not fighting. Something closer to a performance, years of pent-up rage and grief and the particular fury of a woman who had been given a body she did not ask for and had decided, in this moment, to make it sing.

Twenty of the creatures fell in a matter of moments. They dropped around her like stones shaken from a crumbling wall, and when the last one collapsed she stood in the center of the carnage, breathing hard, her weapon arm still raised, scanning for any sign of movement that the silence had not yet accounted for.

Neither Jayk nor John had ever seen anything like it.

Jayk turned from the spectacle below and began his ascent. The mountain rose before him like a wall of judgment, sheer in places, crumbling in others, the rock face scarred by centuries of wind and rain and the slow, patient work of gravity. Handgrip by handgrip he scrambled upward, his fingers finding cracks and ledges with an assiduousness that bordered on devotion, his body nimble and surefooted as a goat climbing a cliff face it had been born to climb. His breathing settled into a cadence, reach, grip, pull, breathe, a litany of effort that repeated itself like the responses of a congregation following a call they could no longer name but still remembered in their bones.

He kept his eyes open for the white clusters of flowers. They would stand out against the grey and brown of the rock, he told himself. They would announce themselves, as things of healing always did, by being conspicuously alive in a place devoted to death.

Below, John crouched behind the small berm, one eye on Sarah and one on Jayk, watching both of them with the divided attention of a man who had assigned himself the role of conductor without possessing an instrument of his own. He did not see the creature approach from behind. He did not hear it. The first thing he knew was the grip of talons around his torso, the sudden violent compression of his ribs, and then he was airborne, tossed aside as effortlessly as one tosses a coin, and the ground arrived with a force that drove the breath from his body.

He rolled, scrambled, and regained his footing. The Sky Reaper stood before him. Enormous, its body a study in brutal engineering: hands that terminated in razor-sharp claws, rows of serrated teeth set in a jaw that seemed designed for nothing but destruction, twin horns curving forward from its skull, and two massive legs that rooted it to the earth like stumps driven deep into bedrock. It had no weapon. It did not need one. Every part of it was a weapon.

John had no weapon, either. What he had was his body, engineered, cloned, encoded with the combat instincts of a man who had spent a lifetime learning how to fight, overlaid with the desperate improvisational intelligence of a man who understood that no second chances existed. He raised his fists and felt the absurdity of the gesture and made peace with it in the same instant.

The creature lunged. John dodged, stepping sideways with a quickness that surprised even him, and felt the displacement of air as the claws passed through the space where his chest had been. He had never fought one of these things before. He had never been this close to one before. And so, rather than attack, he retreated, dodging, ducking, circling, studying the creature's patterns the way a scientist studies a new dataset, looking for the signal hidden in the noise.

They were powerful. They were designed to kill. But they were slow. Lumbering. The massive torso that gave them their terrible strength also limited their agility, and their arms, built for grasping and tearing, could not easily reach behind their own heads. That was the variable John needed.

The creature grasped again and John moved, not away this time, but toward it. He planted a foot on the creature's thigh and used it as a step, launching himself upward onto the thing's shoulders in one swift, climbing motion. He grabbed the horns from behind and kicked off backward, throwing all of his weight against the creature's center of gravity, and the beast went down onto its back with a sound like a tree falling.

John landed on his feet. The creature lay stunned, and in that half-second of stillness John began to stomp.

He drove his booted foot into the creature's face with everything he had, every ounce of force his engineered body could produce, every gram of rage and terror and the primal, ancestral fury that lived in the oldest part of his brain, the part that preceded language and reason and civilization, the part that knew only one truth: kill it before it kills you. The creature flailed, its arms reaching desperately for John's legs, but its own massive torso worked against it, the muscles that gave it such devastating forward power could not bend far enough to reach behind its own head. John had found the flaw in the design. The Achilles' heel. And he exploited it without mercy.

He stomped and stomped and stomped. Each impact landed with the wet, percussive finality of a drumbeat counting down to zero. The creature's face began to give beneath his boot, the bone structure softening, the features losing their definition, and still he did not stop. "Arrrrggghhhh!" The sound that tore from his throat was not a word. Older than words. The sound humankind had been making since before it learned to speak, the scream of a species that had always known it was prey and had decided, in the face of every predator, to become something worse.

The creature's arms went limp. It stopped moving.

John stood over it, his chest heaving, his breath coming in ragged gasps, his boot slick with what had been a face. He looked around, the reflex of a soldier confirming the perimeter, and found nothing coming. No more of them. Just the silence of the valley and the distant sounds of Sarah's battle winding down.

He dropped to the ground and sat there, glaring at the dead thing before him. His hands were trembling. His heart hammered against his ribs in a rhythm that would not slow. And then, like sunlight breaking through a crack in a wall, a grin appeared on his face. A laugh escaped his lips, small, incredulous, alive.

"Ha."

He was still alive.

Jayk climbed. The mountain did not care about him, and he found comfort in its indifference. The rock face offered handholds without generosity and ledges without invitation, and he took what was given and asked for nothing more. Below him the sounds of combat had faded to occasional reports that echoed up the mountainside like distant rolling thunder.

The air grew thinner as he ascended. Cooler. The vegetation changed, the scrub brush of the valley floor gave way to hardier things, lichens and mosses that clung to the rock with the tenacity of the faithful, and then, higher still, to grasses that bent in the wind like supplicants. He pulled himself over a ledge and paused to catch his breath, scanning the terrain above him.

And there they were.

White flowers, small and clustered, trembling on slender stems in a breeze he could barely feel. They grew from crevices in the rock where enough soil had accumulated to sustain them, modest, unassuming, plants one could walk past a hundred times without noticing. Beside the flowers, dark purple berries hung in tight clusters, their skins taut and glistening, swollen with a juice that was beautiful and deadly. The leaves were narrow and pale green, dusted with a fine silver fuzz that caught the light.

Raceweed.

Jayk knelt beside the plant with the reverence of a man approaching a sacrament. He did not pick it immediately. He looked at it, really looked, at the delicate white petals, at the dark jewels of the berries, at the roots that disappeared into the rock. A medicine made of poison. A remedy that could kill as easily as it could heal. He thought of Ideo, lying on her mat in the Goku household, her breathing counted by a machine that did not sleep. He thought of the root descending into darkness, drawing up from the earth the very substance that could save her.

He began to dig, carefully, working his fingers around the base of the plant, loosening the soil, extracting the root system intact. It came free in his hands, pale, fibrous, smelling faintly of something sharp and medicinal, something his body recognized as danger even as his mind told him it was hope.

He gathered as much as he could carry, wrapping the roots in the cloth from his pack, bundling the flowers and berries separately to preserve them. When he had enough, he secured the bundle to his back and began the descent.

The climb down was harder than the climb up. Always was. Gravity, which had been his adversary on the way up, now became an unreliable ally, pulling him forward when he needed to move slowly, offering speed at the cost of control. But Jayk had been climbing things his entire life, or at least the parts of his life he could remember, and his body knew what to do even when his mind wandered.

By the time he reached the base of the mountain, Sarah and John were waiting for him. Sarah sat on a rock with her weapon arm resting across her knees, her coat retrieved and draped over her shoulders again. Something different about her, a stillness that had not been there before, the particular calm that follows a storm. John stood beside her, his boot stained dark, a bruise forming on his shoulder where the creature had gripped him. He looked like a man who had fought an animal with his bare hands and won, because that is exactly what he was.

"Did you find it?" John asked.

Jayk held up the bundle. The white flowers caught the fading light and seemed, for a moment, to glow. "I found it."

Sarah looked at the flowers and then at Jayk and then at John, and something passed between the three of them that did not require language, a current that moved through all three at once, the recognition of people who had walked into the impossible together and walked out the other side.

"Then let's get it back to her," John said.

They turned east, toward Solos. Toward Ideo. The sun was setting behind the mountains at their backs, and their shadows stretched out long before them across the valley floor, leading the way home.
