ROGER carried her as if she were something sacred.

The last stretch of wasteland between the airfield and the bunker seemed to exist outside of time. Every step forward felt like penance. Every moan that escaped Sarah's lips, soft, animal, the sound of a creature retreating into itself, pulled at something in Jayk's chest that he could not name.

He wanted to carry her himself. But ROGER moved with a tireless precision that Jayk's exhausted body could never match. So he followed. Kept pace. Pushed through the ache in his legs and the burning in his throat and the grime of battle that had settled into every crease of his skin.

She had saved his life. A stranger. Someone she had no reason to trust. He intended to find out why.

 

 

The bunker entrance crouched in the earth like a scar the wasteland was trying to heal.

Jayk reached it first. His fingers found the concealed latch from memory, and he hauled the heavy doors apart, rust grinding against rust. The dark exhaled stale air, cool, ancient, the breath of a world that had been sealed away.

ROGER descended without breaking stride, carrying Sarah into the passage below.

Jayk followed.

The corridor stretched before them, walls of concrete and old metal, lit only by the faint emergency strips that still hummed with power after all these years. Their footsteps echoed. The sound was rhythmic, the tolls of arrival, of homecoming, of something that might still be saved.

Then they were met. Wax Griffin emerged from the shadows at a run, flanked by others whose names Jayk barely knew. Their faces shifted from alarm to urgency to something like hope when they saw Sarah in ROGER's arms. In moments, she was lifted away. Borne toward the medical unit on a tide of worried voices and reaching hands.

Jayk watched her go.

"How far away is everyone else?" Wax asked, falling into step beside him.

"Half a day." Jayk gestured toward ROGER, who walked with that strange, fluid gait. "This is ROGER. He's a medical doctor."

ROGER tilted his head slightly.

"'Medical doctor' is an oversimplification." His voice was calm. Measured. Clinical. "I am a genetic engineer. But I can perform the functions of a surgeon, and serve in place of much of your own medical equipment."

Wax glanced back, curious.

"I possess the protean abilities of an electroencephalograph, electrocardiograph, genome sequencer, hemocytometer, and blood typer."

"You possess the what?"

"I can monitor heart rate, blood pressure, and blood-oxygen levels through direct observation." ROGER continued walking, unfazed. "I contain the functional equivalents of a spectrometer, a toxicology scanner, and diagnostic imaging devices, fMRI, CT, X-ray, all accessible through nanoparticle biomagnets within my system."

He glanced at Wax, lavender eyes steady.

"In a pinch, I can serve as a blood dialysis machine."

Wax stopped walking. Stared.

"So... you're a robot?"

His eyes moved up and down ROGER's frame as if examining a priceless artifact. Which, for his capabilities, he was. A superintelligent mind given form. Unlimited energy drawn from the biomimetic nanoparticles that comprised every fiber of his being.

"Perhaps you could help us solve the problems we've been having with getting our own robots to work," Wax said, his voice brightening.

ROGER paused at the entrance to the medical unit, his lavender eyes settling on Wax for a fraction of a second longer than necessary.

"I would be happy to assist with your machines." He turned toward the operating room, where Sarah lay motionless on a thin metal table. "But first, I would prefer to help with getting your humans to work. If you don't mind."

Wax nodded quickly. "Of course. Please. Thank you."

The medical unit was cold.

Jayk stood beside the operating table, watching ROGER's hands move with impossible precision. They were all filthy, ROGER's white garments stained with dust and dried fluids, a dingy testament to everything they had survived. Sarah's face was slick with sweat and grime. Her breathing came shallow. The place where her arm had been was a landscape of cauterized flesh and angry tissue.

They injected her with a sedative. Jayk watched her face grow peaceful as she drifted away. He stood beside her for as long as he could.

Her eyes, hazy beneath heavy lids, caught the harsh light of the operating room. Green, flecked with gold. He reached down and lifted a red lock of matted hair from her brow, brushing it gently aside.

She looked small. Fragile.

Something stirred in his chest: a protectiveness that felt almost holy in its intensity. He barely knew this girl. Yet he felt bound to her, as if their fates had been woven together the moment she chose to save him instead of herself.

ROGER worked without pause. Cleaning. Suturing. His hands never faltered. His face never changed.

There was nothing more Jayk could do. He found an uncomfortable chair beside the bed and collapsed into it. His body screamed for rest. His mind refused to grant it. But exhaustion won.

His sleep was a war.

His parents died again. And again. Each death worse than the last. He watched the monster that had taken Sarah's arm escape through smoke and flame, its laughter echoing like broken bells. He tried to chase it. His legs would not move. He tried to scream. No sound came.

He woke. Looked at her face. Drifted away.

There was no day or night underground. Time blurred into a single gray mass, punctuated only by the rhythm of his breathing and the soft beep of whatever machines ROGER had managed to coax back to life.

He dreamed of fire. Of blood. Of promises he could not keep.

When he woke again, ROGER was standing vigil.

The robot, the man, the whatever-he-was, stood motionless beside Sarah's bed, watching her with an expression Jayk could not read. Patience, perhaps. Or something deeper.

Jayk rose. His body protested every movement.

"How is she?"

ROGER did not look at him.

"The damage to the underlying tissue is significant." A pause. "But she is stable. All vital signs are normal. I expect a full recovery."

Full recovery.

The words rolled through Jayk's mind like a prayer without an answer. How could someone make a full recovery when they had lost a part of themselves?

She was fortunate to have survived at all. The electrostatic weapons the creatures used burned even as they tore; cauterizing the wound in the same instant they created it. A small mercy wrapped in horror. The tear was sealed as it was being made.

He stood beside her bed and watched her breathe. He wanted to help her somehow. Put her back together. Make her whole.

But how?

The question hung in the silence like incense that refused to dissipate.

That night, the rest arrived.

They came through the bunker entrance in waves; a slow procession of the wounded and the weary. Survivors from the slave facility. Soldiers who had fought to free them. The injured and the dying, carried on makeshift stretchers or dragged in slings across the rough stone floor. The bunker swelled with bodies and voices and the quiet sounds of suffering.

Among them, unnoticed, walked a man who did not belong.

John had been watching them for two days. He had slipped in among the refugees during the sandstorm, adopting their exhausted shuffle, their hollow eyes. No one had questioned him. No one had even looked twice.

Improvise. Adapt. Overcome.

The old motto had carried him through worse than this.

They had walked for miles past the airfield where military aircraft had gone to die: B-25s and A-10s, a few Apaches, an overturned Chinook. The skeletal remains of a world that had believed it would last forever.

His mother had always told him: Don't visit the past. We don't live there anymore.

But the past was everywhere here. The past was all that remained.

Now they had arrived at this place: an underground bunker, half-swallowed by the earth. A command station, perhaps. Or a research facility. The kind of place where soldiers had once planned operations, where scientists had developed technologies meant to stay buried.

It was nothing now. Just shelter. Running water fed by an aquifer. Electricity that still hummed in the walls. Food preserved beyond any reasonable expiration. At last, some semblance of normal life.

John moved through the labyrinth of corridors and empty rooms, watching the others marvel at technology they had clearly never seen. They examined dead screens and control panels like children discovering artifacts from a myth.

How long had he been out?

Long enough for civilization to become legend. Long enough for an entire generation to be born without any concept of the world he had known.

A terrible thought settled into him like a stone dropping into still water.

What had that experiment really been about?

They gathered in a large common area.

It might have been a rec room once. Now it was filled with the rescued and the broken, clustering into small groups for warmth or comfort. Their faces were hollow. Haunted. These were people who had known nothing but captivity.

John stood at the edge and watched.

Are these people the last remains of humankind?

And if they are, what chance do they stand against what's out there?

The answer was obvious. None. They had no training. No discipline. No understanding of what they were facing. If it was left to them, humanity would be lost within a generation.

He fought the urge to speak. A Marine did not interfere unless asked. A Marine stayed in his lane. But a Marine also did not stand idle while civilians suffered. A Marine did not hide his training when it could save lives.

The burden grew heavier with every passing moment. It began like a pressure in his chest. A tickle in the back of his throat. A demand that would not be ignored.

He looked away. His mouth twitched. Opened.

"I can help."

The words came out like a confession. Quiet. Almost a plea. The room went still.

"I don't know how I got here, or what's happened to the world." He heard his own voice as if from a great distance. "But a little over a month ago, I was a Gunnery Sergeant with the United States Marine Corps. My company was assigned to guard a watershed on the Balsas River."

He paused.

"I don't remember anything after that. Until I woke up in total darkness and found the world overrun by those creatures."

The leaders, the ones who seemed to be in charge, exchanged glances and spoke in hushed tones.

John did not wait for them to decide what to do with him. He pushed through the crowd and approached the big one. The man who carried himself like a warrior. There was a stillness to him, a centeredness... the kind that came from years of training the body and the mind together.

"I've been living in that city alone ever since." John met his eyes without flinching. "Now, if we work together, I think we can..."

The man raised a hand.

Silence.

"What's your name?"

John stood firm. Shoulders back. Spine straight. The posture of a man who had spent years learning how to be unshakeable.

"John. John D'Arby." A pause. "Gunnery Sergeant, United States Marine Corps."

He brought his hand up in a crisp salute. "Oorah." His lips curled into a thin smile.

The man before him did not smile back. But something shifted in his expression. Recognition, perhaps. Or the beginning of respect.

They were of a height together. It was rare that Po met anyone who could look him level in the eye. For a long moment, neither of them moved. Then Po lowered his hand. And something in the room changed.

The bunker did not sleep.

Through the walls came the sounds of the wounded, groans and whimpers, the occasional howl that rose from somewhere deep in the corridors and echoed through the concrete like a plea no one knew how to answer. Dissonant. Raw. The kind of sound that made you want to cover your ears and listen harder at the same time.

Jayk lay on a cot in a room that had once been something else: an office, maybe, or a storage closet. The walls were bare. The ceiling was stained. Somewhere above him, hundreds of tons of earth pressed down, and he tried not to think about how easily it could all collapse.

He did not sleep. Every time he closed his eyes, he saw her. The red hair. The arm that was not there anymore. The way she had looked at him before pulling the trigger, not with fear, not with hope, but with nothing at all. Emptiness. Or maybe something colder. The look of someone who had stopped caring whether she lived or died, so long as she could hurt the thing that hurt her.

Morning arrived without light. Underground, you measured time by the sounds that changed, the shift from night-groans to the shuffling of feet, the clatter of cookware, the low murmur of voices beginning to plan.

They gathered in what had once been a command room.

Po stood at the head of a long metal table, its surface scarred with the ghosts of maps and diagrams long since erased. Around him sat the others: Jayk, still wearing exhaustion like a second skin. Ideo, her fingers drumming silently against something wrapped in heavy canvas. Rensa, coiled in her chair like a spring waiting to release. And Dade, cleaning the dried blood from beneath his fingernails with the tip of his knife.

"We acted without a plan," Po said. His voice was calm. Measured. The voice of a man who had learned that anger was a luxury. "We struck at the slave facility, and we won. But now we have hundreds more mouths to feed and no place to put them all."

Dade snorted. "So what're we s'poseta do, send 'em back?"

"We adapt." Po's gaze swept the room. "One of the men we rescued claims to be a former military officer. He says he's from the past, before the fall. He has no memory of the Dark Epoch, no knowledge of the usurpers."

"What is he, mental?" Dade's knife paused mid-scrape.

"I doubt it. He seems sincere." Po turned to Jayk. "I appreciate what you've done for us. Without you, we wouldn't have had the courage to strike. You're a strong fighter, there's no denying that."

Jayk waited. He could feel the but coming.

"But now we need more than courage. We need strategy. Discipline. The kind of training that turns survivors into soldiers." Po's eyes held no apology. "John has offered to lead that effort. I've accepted."

Silence settled over the room like dust. Jayk nodded slowly. He understood. It was not about him, it was about what they needed. And what they needed was not a boy who had learned to fight by watching everyone he loved get slaughtered.

"Good," he said. "Then put me somewhere useful."

"Algae production," Rensa offered. "We'll need to ramp it up. And hunting, trapping, scouting for additional food sources." Her eyes brightened. "I can lead that. I've been itching to get out of this hole."

"Dese new recruits will have to earn dere keep," Dade said, his knife resuming its slow scrape. "Dey work as soldiers, or dey cook and clean. Whatever dey want. But nobody gets a free ride." He looked up, and for a moment his face was something other than insufferable. It was hard. Cold. The face of a man who had buried his softness a long time ago.

Po nodded. "Dade, you and Rensa will work with John. Food production and military training. Conscript who you can. Get the rest performing other duties." He turned to Jayk. "You and Ideo handle the logistics, bedding, clothing, medical support. We'll need all hands."

His gaze softened, just slightly.

"You may yet get your army, Jayk."

Ideo waited until the others had begun to rise before she spoke.

"Before you all scatter." She pulled back the heavy canvas, revealing what lay beneath. "I brought us a present."

It was an alien weapon, or what remained of one. The stock was shattered, the framework torn away. There was no handle, no trigger mechanism. But the barrel remained intact, and within it, the conducting sphere and interior belt that had once produced the killing electricity.

"Where did you get that?" Jayk asked.

"Battlefield salvage. Most of their weapons self-destruct when the creatures die, but this one didn't go off properly." She turned it over in her hands, her eyes bright with something between curiosity and hunger. "The core is functional. With some tinkering, I might be able to fashion a trigger mechanism. Something a human could actually use."

Jayk stared at the ruined weapon. And somewhere in the back of his mind, a thought began to form; strange, reckless, impossible.

Her arm.

The missing arm.

What if...

"Can I borrow that?" he asked. "Just for a minute."

ROGER was tending to a patient when Jayk arrived, a woman with burns across half her face, her eyes glazed with the particular emptiness of someone who had stopped hoping. The robot worked with the same tireless precision as always, his hands moving through procedures that would have taken a human surgeon hours.

"ROGER," Jayk said. "I need to ask you something."

"To what do I owe the pleasure, Jayk?" ROGER did not look up. His lavender eyes remained fixed on the wound he was treating.

"I have an idea. I don't even know if it's possible. But if it is..." He stopped. Breathed. Started again. "Sarah lost her arm. And we have this weapon. The nerve endings in the stump, could they be connected to a firing mechanism? Could you make the weapon part of her?"

Now ROGER looked up.

For a long moment, he said nothing. His expression, if you could call it an expression, was unreadable. Calculation, perhaps. Or something deeper.

"A weaponized prosthesis," he said finally. "Bonded to her nervous system. Her thoughts would become the trigger."

"Is it possible?"

"It is... highly unconventional." A pause. "But yes. The human body transmits sensory impulses through motor neurons. If I could join her central nervous system to the weapon's firing mechanism, her nerves would complete the circuit. She could fire it with a thought."

Jayk's heart was pounding now. "Will you do it?"

"That depends entirely on her."

Sarah was awake when he found her.

She lay propped against the thin hospital pillow, her remaining arm resting on the blanket, her eyes fixed on some middle distance that Jayk could not see. The place where her left arm had been was wrapped in clean bandages now, ROGER's work, but the shape of the absence was unmistakable.

She did not look at him when he entered.

Jayk sat in the chair beside her bed. He did not know how to begin, so he just began.

"ROGER says he can do it. He can bond the weapon to your arm, to what's left of it. Your nerves would control the firing mechanism. It would become part of you." He leaned forward, searching her face for something: anger, fear, hope. He found none of them. "You're the only one I've ever seen who could use their technology. I don't know why. I don't know how. But I believe you could help us win this war."

Still nothing.

"Please." The word came out rougher than he intended. "You've got to help us. Get your revenge on the creatures who did this to you. To all of us."

Sarah turned her head slowly. Her eyes met his for the first time since he had entered the room. And in their depths was something he had not expected, not anger, not fear, but a kind of terrible clarity. As if she had seen the shape of her future and was deciding whether to step into it.

For a long moment, she said nothing. Then she nodded.

The surgery lasted thirteen and a half hours.

Jayk lost count of the blood transfusions. Lost count of the times Sarah's monitors screamed and ROGER's hands moved faster than any human's could. Lost count of the prayers he did not know he was saying, fragments of half-remembered hymns, words his mother had taught him before the museum, before the smoke, before everything ended.

He stayed through all of it. Watching through the glass when they let him. Pacing the corridor when they did not. At some point Ideo appeared beside him, her tools spread across a worktable, reconfiguring the weapon's housing to fit the shape of a human arm.

"You should sleep," she said.

"I can't."

She did not argue.

Twice during the surgery, Sarah's heart stopped. Twice, ROGER brought her back, his hands moving with a precision that seemed almost reverent, as if the body on the table was something sacred that must not be allowed to fail.

The wound is where the Light enters.

The words surfaced in Jayk's mind from somewhere he could not name. A book, maybe. Or something his father had said once, when there were still fathers and books and time.

When it was finally over, when ROGER had bonded the weapon to Sarah's nervous system, when Ideo had fashioned the elbow joint from salvaged robotic parts, when the whole terrible apparatus had become part of Sarah's body; they put her into a medically induced coma.

"Two days," ROGER said. "Her body needs time to integrate the changes. When she wakes, she will be... different."

Jayk stared through the glass at the girl who had saved his life. Her left arm was no longer missing. In its place was something that was neither flesh nor machine but both; a fusion of human nerve and alien technology, ending in a barrel that gleamed dully in the low light.

She looked small. Fragile. Dangerous.

A Secret Weapon.

The words came to him unbidden, and he knew with sudden certainty that this was what she would become. Not just a survivor. Not just a fighter. Something new. Something the world had never seen before. Something that might actually give them a chance.

For two days, Jayk kept vigil.

He sat beside her bed while the bunker transformed around him, while John drilled the newcomers in the training hall, while Rensa led scouting parties into the wasteland, while Dade barked orders at anyone who moved too slowly. He heard it all through the walls, through the floor, through the constant low hum of activity that meant the resistance was finally becoming something real.

He should have been part of it. Should have been training, planning, contributing. Instead, he sat in an uncomfortable chair and watched a girl breathe.

ROGER came and went, checking readings, adjusting medications. Once, he paused at the door and studied Jayk with those unreadable eyes.

"You should rest," he said. "Not in a chair. The drugs have kept her sedated. When she awakens, I'll come find you."

Jayk shook his head. "I want to be here. I want my face to be the first thing she sees."

"Why?"

The question caught him off guard. He did not have a good answer... only the truth.

"Because she saved me. Because I couldn't save her. Because..." He stopped. Swallowed. "Because maybe if she sees a friendly face, it'll be easier. Waking up to... to all of this."

ROGER was silent for a long moment.

"She's been through tremendous pain," he said finally. "The past few weeks. The surgery. What comes next. She'll need someone to help her adjust." A pause. "I think that should be you."

Jayk looked up. There was something in ROGER's voice he had not heard before: warmth, maybe, or something like it. A machine's attempt at compassion.

"Thank you," he said.

"Don't thank me yet. Thank me when she's whole again." ROGER turned to leave, then paused. "Or as whole as any of us can be, in this world."

Eventually, exhaustion won.

Jayk found himself in the common area, the rec room that had become a training ground. The sounds of combat filled the space: flesh against flesh, the thud of bodies hitting mats, the sharp crack of wooden practice weapons. John was at the center of it all, moving among the recruits like a conductor drawing notes from an untrained orchestra.

Jayk watched from the edge of the room. He did not want to be here. Did not want to see John turning these broken people into soldiers, doing the work that he had tried and failed to do.

But there was nowhere else to go. Sarah was sleeping. The surgery was done. And his body, finally, had declared mutiny.

He found a corner away from the sparring. Slid down the wall. Let his head fall back against the concrete.

Around him, the sounds of training continued: grunts, impacts, the occasional cry of pain or frustration. The sounds of war being learned.

He refused to make eye contact with anyone. Especially not John.

Sarah woke to silence.

Not the silence of sleep, that had been deeper, darker, a place without edges. This was the silence of a room that was waiting for her. Low ceiling. Bare walls. The hum of machinery somewhere behind the concrete.

She was alone. Her body felt wrong. Heavy in places, absent in others. When she tried to move her left arm, nothing happened, not the nothing of numbness, but the nothing of absence, a signal sent to a limb that no longer existed.

Except something did exist there. Something cold. Something that was not her. She turned her head and looked.

The weapon extended from what remained of her arm; a fusion of flesh and metal that gleamed dully in the low light. The barrel was sleek, alien, ending in a conducting sphere that seemed to pulse with dormant energy. Where her elbow should have been, there was a joint of polished chrome. Where her forearm should have been, there was only the apparatus.

She stared at it for a long time.

What have they done to me?

The thought surfaced and sank again, too heavy to hold. She did not have the strength for horror. Not yet. So she just looked, cataloguing the details like a scientist examining a specimen. The way the metal met her skin. The thin line of scarring where organic ended and synthetic began. The faint warmth emanating from somewhere inside the mechanism.

Eventually, she turned her head away and closed her eyes. Sleep came like a mercy.

When she woke again, ROGER was there.

He stood beside her bed with his hands folded in front of him, patient, still, his searching eyes catching the dim light like polished glass. For a moment she thought she was dreaming. He looked too perfect to be real. Too clean for this filthy world.

"How are we feeling?" His voice was soft. Measured. The voice of someone who had learned to calibrate kindness.

She tried to speak. Her throat was dry, her tongue thick. When the words finally came, they scraped like gravel.

"Who are you?"

He tilted his head slightly, a gesture she was beginning to recognize. Calculation. Or curiosity. It was hard to tell.

"We've met before," he said. "Though you may not remember. You were in a very different state."

"I feel like I know you." She frowned. "From somewhere else. Somewhere... before."

"You do." He moved closer, his fingers brushing a curtain of red hair from her face. His touch was cool, not unpleasant, but unmistakably inhuman. "You were part of the Protogaia Project. Your DNA was uploaded into a neural network of biomagnetic nanobots. Your grandfather selected you to be one of the survivors."

Grandfather.

The word triggered something, a flash of images, half-formed and flickering. A lab. A kind face. Equations on a whiteboard. The smell of antiseptic and ozone.

"I helped give birth to you," ROGER continued. "I was the one who brought you out of the facility. But we were separated. The world... changed. I searched for you for a very long time." A pause. "When I found you, I never left your side."

She processed this slowly. The words made sense individually, but together they formed a shape she could not quite grasp.

"So what am I?" The question crawled out of her before she could stop it. "Some kind of robot?"

ROGER's expression shifted, something almost like warmth flickering across his features.

"No, Sarah. You are not a robot." He knelt beside her bed so that their eyes were level. "You are a clone. Something far more beautiful and delicate than anything I could ever hope to be."

She held his gaze. Tried to read what was behind those lavender eyes. Found nothing she recognized.

"Then what are you?"

He told her.

She listened as he explained, the biomagnets, the nanobots, the breakthrough that had allowed machines to think as fluidly as humans. He spoke of her grandfather, Dr. Lemky, with something that sounded almost like reverence. He described the architecture of his own mind: the nested subroutines, the heuristic agents, the "society of the robot mind" that allowed consciousness to emerge from silicon and code.

"Alone, one nanobot is nothing," he said. "A simple program executing simple commands. But aggregated together, millions of them, billions, they become something more. They become me."

She watched his face as he spoke. It was perfect. Symmetrical. Utterly without flaw. And yet there was something in his eyes, not emotion, exactly, but the ghost of it. The echo of something that might have been feeling, if feeling could be simulated.

"Your grandfather created us both," ROGER said. "In different ways, for different purposes. But we are both his children."

Children.

The word sat strangely in her chest. She was someone's child. Someone's creation. A copy of a woman who had lived and died centuries ago, a woman whose memories now flickered through Sarah's mind like scenes from a film she had watched but could not quite remember.

Who was she? Who am I now?

The questions had no answers. Or too many.

ROGER unwrapped the bandages from her arm with careful, precise movements.

"Your body is reacting well to the device," he said. "We were able to bond the weapon to your upper forearm. The brachialis muscle was severely damaged, but the cauterization mitigated the nerve damage. I was able to attach the firing mechanism to your remaining nerve groups."

She watched him work. Watched her own arm, her new arm, emerge from layers of gauze.

"Easy as making a fist," ROGER said. He raised his own hand and demonstrated, fingers closing, opening, closing again. "When you're ready, your thoughts will become the trigger. You'll fire it as naturally as you'd reach for a glass of water."

She did not say anything. Did not trust herself to speak.

"I've deactivated the conducting sphere," he continued. "So you won't discharge the weapon accidentally. When you're ready, this cable connects to the microprocessor." He showed her a thin wire, coiled neatly beside her bed. "And then you'll be... operational."

Operational.

The word landed like a stone.

He gave her clothes. Food. A room that would be hers for as long as she needed it. He explained that they had moved her to a private area, somewhere ROGER could monitor her recovery without arousing suspicion from the others.

"They might not understand," he said. "The nature of what you now possess."

She nodded. She understood. She was a secret now. A weapon hidden in a body that used to be human.

Used to be.

The phrase echoed in her mind as ROGER turned to leave. She watched him go, this machine that had searched for her, that had given her back her arm (or something like it), that spoke of her grandfather with the closest thing to love she had seen in this broken world.

"Thank you," she said. The words came out hoarse, reluctant. "For taking care of me."

He paused at the door. Did not turn around.

"It's what I was designed to..." A pause. Not a malfunction. Something else. "It's my programming."

Something flickered in her chest, irritation, maybe, or the first stirring of the person she was going to become.

"How very diplomatic of you." Her voice came out sharper than she'd intended. Almost like herself. Almost like someone she remembered being. "Did they teach you that at robot school?"

ROGER stood motionless for a moment.

Then, without another word, he left.

Sarah stared at the closed door. Then at her arm: the metal, the chrome, the conducting sphere that hummed with dormant lightning.

She was a clone with a dead woman's memories. She was a weapon. She was alone. And somewhere deep inside her, beneath the confusion and the grief and the numb weight of everything she had lost, something small and fierce was beginning to wake up.

She stared at her arm long after ROGER left.

It lay beside her like something that did not belong, cold metal fused to warm flesh, the conducting sphere dormant but humming with potential she did not understand. When she tried to lift it, nothing happened. The signals went out from her brain and vanished somewhere in the junction between what she had been and what she now was.

Like a boat anchor, she thought. Tethering me to this bed. To this body. To this life I did not ask for.

She remembered everything now. The project. The cloning chamber. The searing light of premature birth and the chaos of the slave camp, the alien vegetation, the argon-thick air, the creature that had taken her arm before she had taken its head. The memories crashed through her in waves, and she let them come. Trauma plays tricks on the mind, but she was done playing tricks on herself.

Anger arrived first. Then confusion. Then a resentment so cold it felt almost like peace.

Who was she? Who had she been? Did it matter anymore?

The questions circled without landing. She was too tired to catch them.

A woman appeared in the doorway.

Dark hair, matted and thick. Green eyes that seemed to calculate everything they saw. She moved with the coiled energy of someone who had learned to fight by never letting her guard down.

"Hi. I'm Rensa." She set a bundle of clothes on the foot of the bed. "ROGER asked me to bring these. Figured you'd want something other than a hospital gown."

Sarah looked at the pile. Shirts. Pants. The simple necessities of being a person again. Then she looked at her arm.

Rensa followed her gaze. For a moment, neither of them spoke.

"So," Sarah said finally. "How exactly am I supposed to get a shirt over this?"

Rensa stared at the apparatus, the chrome joint, the barrel, the barrel of a device that bulged where a forearm should have been. Her expression shifted through several phases: confusion, assessment, decision.

Then she pulled a blade from her belt and cut the sleeve off. "There." She held up the modified shirt, proud of her handiwork. "Should work for now. Until we find something better."

Sarah almost laughed. Almost. Instead, she reached for the shirt with her right hand and began the awkward process of pulling it over the device. The fabric caught on the barrel. Snagged on the joint. Refused to cooperate with this new geometry of her body.

Rensa watched for a moment, then stepped forward and helped guide the sleeve-hole over the weapon.

"Sometimes it's better to let a person try and fail," she said, "than to offer help unwanted."

Sarah met her eyes. Found something there she had not expected, not pity, but recognition. The look of someone who understood what it meant to carry something you did not choose.

"Thank you," Sarah said. "This'll do for now."

Rensa nodded. Turned to leave. Paused at the door.

"For what it's worth," she said, "I've seen a lot of people come through here broken. Most of them stay that way." A beat. "You don't seem like the staying type."

Then she was gone.

Time became a blur.

Hours melted into days. Days dissolved into weeks. Sarah drifted in and out of consciousness, her body cycling through pain and numbness, exhaustion and restless waking. People came and went, faces she could not hold onto, voices that blended into a single murmur of concern. Tall and short. Slender and broad. Male and female. They checked on her, brought her food, adjusted her blankets, and she processed them all with the same detached awareness.

Input. Output. Repeat.

The arm shrieked with pain, then went numb, then shrieked again. No position was comfortable. She ate when they brought food. Drank when they brought water. Passed out when her body demanded it. Woke when the pain became too sharp to ignore.

She had no idea what the sun looked like anymore. No concept of whether it was morning or midnight. The bunker had its own rhythm, the hum of generators, the shuffle of feet in corridors, the distant sounds of training, but none of it belonged to her.

She was adrift in a body that did not fit. A mind full of memories she was not sure were hers. A weapon she did not know how to use.

And then, one day, something shifted.

She sat up.

It took everything she had. Her muscles had atrophied. Her balance was wrong, the weight of the device pulling her to one side like a compass needle seeking north. But she did it. Swung her legs off the edge of the bunk. Let them dangle there while she caught her breath.

The arm lay in her lap now, this thing that had been grafted onto her, this fusion of alien technology and human nerve. She cradled it with her right hand. Felt the cold metal against her palm. The faint vibration of dormant systems waiting to wake.

She thought about everything she would never do again. Play the cello. Braid her hair. Hold someone with both arms. Simple things. Human things. The ordinary miracles of having a body that worked the way it was supposed to.

Doctor, will I be able to play the piano?

Of course you will.

That's good, because I never could before.

The old joke surfaced from somewhere, a memory, or maybe just a reflex. She whispered it to herself. And then she laughed.

It was an ugly sound. Raw. A little unhinged. The laugh of someone who had looked at the abyss and decided to tell it a joke.

Then she grabbed the apparatus with her right hand and slammed it against the steel bed frame.

The impact rang through the room. Left a sizable dent in the metal. She stared at the damage. At the arm that had caused it.

It was time.

She had finally regained her strength.

ROGER appeared shortly after, drawn, perhaps, by the sound of violence against furniture. His inquisitive eyes swept over her: sitting upright, dressed, the device resting heavily in her lap.

"Your blood pressure has returned to normal balance," he said, moving to her side with that characteristic glide. He lifted her wrist, took her pulse. "And your body temperature has improved significantly. How do you feel?"

"Groggy." The word came out rough. Unused vocal cords scraping against each other.

"That's to be expected. You've been on a regimen of intense pain relievers for several weeks. Your body will adjust as you metabolize them." He released her wrist. "Do you remember the people who have been visiting you?"

She shook her head. "Faces. Blurs. I couldn't keep track."

"They're friends. They rescued you from the slave facility where you were being held. They were forcing you and others to cultivate alien vegetation, the gardens that are reengineering this planet's atmosphere." His voice remained clinical, informational. But there was something underneath it now. Something that might have been pride. "Jayk and I carried you back here after you were injured. You've been convalescing ever since."

Convalescing. Such a civilized word for what she had been through.

"If you're ready," ROGER continued, "there's some physical therapy I've prepared. And the others would like to meet you properly. They've been... eager."

Sarah nodded. It was the only response she had left.

Getting dressed was easier than before. Rensa had left her sweatpants and tank tops, simple clothes that could be pulled on without fighting the apparatus. Sarah managed it alone this time, though it took longer than she wanted to admit.

When ROGER returned, he held the door open and she made her way into the corridor. Each step was an effort. The device pulled at her shoulder, threw off her balance, turned walking into a negotiation with gravity. But she walked.

They entered a smaller room, some kind of common area, repurposed for rehabilitation. And there they were, waiting for her.

Jayk stood near the back, his posture uncertain. When their eyes met, something flickered across his face: relief, maybe, or wonder. He smiled, bashful and brief.

"I... we've been waiting for you," he said, and stood up quickly, as if he had been caught doing something private.

Rensa was there too, leaning against the wall with her arms crossed. And beside her, a young woman with bright eyes and restless energy: Ideo. The one who had salvaged the weapon.

Sarah moved to the center of the room. They crowded in around her, not too close, but close enough. She could feel their eyes on the arm. On her. Taking in the full reality of what she had become.

Her shoulder was already aching. Her body listed to one side from the weight. She was nervous, and they were staring, and she felt like a specimen under glass.

"Glad you're up and about!" Rensa said, breaking the silence. "Sorry about the shirt. But what you've got now is a lot better."

Ideo stepped forward, practically bouncing. "Have you tried it out yet?"

"No. Not yet."

"Bummer!" Ideo's disappointment was genuine and immediate. "I can't wait to see what happens when you do! I want a full report: details, everything!" She grinned. "You know, we've started calling you our secret weapon."

"Nobody calls her that," Jayk said quietly.

"Well, I do." Ideo shot him a look. "Anyway, she is! She has the only real weapon we've got. Except for me, of course, but I'm a weapon all by myself." She turned back to Sarah, gleefully. "You could probably take on the whole alien horde!"

Sarah did not know what to say to that. So she said nothing.

ROGER interrupted before the silence could stretch too thin.

"Sarah, I've prepared some exercises for you. Physical therapy to ensure mobility of your remaining limbs." He gestured toward an adjacent room: a training space, she realized. Mats on the floor. A broken mirror on one wall. "These movements will help restore muscular strength, joint motion, and flexibility."

She followed him in. The others stayed behind, watching through the doorway.

ROGER positioned her in front of the mirror. For the first time since waking, she saw herself fully: the pale face, the tangled hair, the tank top that exposed the junction where flesh became metal. The device extended from her left side like something that had grown there. Something that belonged.

Or something that was learning to.

"We'll start simple," ROGER said. "Shoulder shrugs. Raise and lower, like this."

She mimicked the motion. Felt the pull of muscles that had not been used in weeks. The arm moved with her: heavy, unwieldy, but moving.

They continued for several minutes. Shrugs became lateral raises. Raises became controlled extensions. Her muscles burned. Her joints protested. But she kept going, watching herself in the mirror, watching this new body learn its own capabilities.

Eventually, fatigue won. She sat down on the mat, breathing hard.

"Good," ROGER said. "We'll do this every day. Your strength will return. And when it does..." He paused. "When it does, we'll begin weapons training."

She looked up at him. At those sincere eyes that held something almost like hope.

"The others don't know what to make of you yet," he said quietly. "Neither do you, I suspect. But I believe you will become something remarkable, Sarah. Something this world has never seen."

Secret weapon.

The words echoed in her mind. Not Ideo's version, gleeful, excited, treating her like a toy. Something deeper. Something that might actually be true.

Jayk had found her a coat.

It was oversized, intentionally so. Long enough to cover both her body and the apparatus that was now part of it. When she put it on, the weapon disappeared beneath heavy fabric. She could pass for ordinary. For human. For someone who had not been rebuilt from the fragments of what she had lost.

Beneath this cloak of deception, she thought, she would come and go in peace.

She stood in the corridor outside her room, wrapped in the coat, testing the weight of it. The device was still there, would always be there, but hidden now. A secret she carried.

Jayk appeared at the end of the hall. He stopped when he saw her, and for a moment, neither of them spoke.

"Thank you," she said. "For the coat."

"I thought..." He hesitated. "I thought it might be easier. Until you're ready for people to see."

She nodded. Pulled the fabric tighter around herself.

"What happens now?" she asked.

"Now?" He took a step closer. His eyes were steady, serious. "Now you heal. Now you train. And when you're ready..." He stopped. Started again. "When you're ready, we fight back."

Sarah looked at him, this boy who had carried her from the battlefield, who had sat vigil while she was remade, who was looking at her now like she might be the answer to prayers he did not know how to pray.

She did not feel like an answer. She felt like a question that had not finished being asked.

But somewhere deep inside her, beneath the confusion and the grief and the cold weight of alien metal, something small and fierce was still there. Waiting. Growing. Learning what it meant to be a weapon.
