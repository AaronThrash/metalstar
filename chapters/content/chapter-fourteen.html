Trinidad and Riley were invited to stay until the caravan arrived. In exchange, Riley would get some experience "fishing," a euphemism that simply meant digging through the garbage. The modest sofa in the living room had been turned into a makeshift bed for Trinidad, and a collection of variegated pillows had been arranged on the floor for Riley. Together they would travel with the traders along the Spice Road once they arrived. "Get some sleep, Riley. Tomorrow Ramesh is going to teach you how to fish like them." Trinidad said, smiling. *A fishing rod with a line.* "No, not like that. They use a net to capture the leftovers from the sewers. It is more like hide-and-seek." Riley was having trouble keeping his eyes open after such an exhausting day, and before long he had fallen into a deep sleep. Trinidad followed soon after.

She left her body.

It happened the way it always did. Not a choice, but an unconscious loosening. Like a hand releasing a string. Her physical form remained on the sofa, breathing slowly, while something else rose through the ceiling and the earth and the dark. She had been doing this since she was a girl. Born blind, she had discovered early that in sleep she could leave her body and travel anywhere she wished, and for the first time in her waking life she could see. Some of these journeys were visions: things that had happened long ago, or things that had yet to happen. Others were transcendental: events unfolding at that very moment, somewhere in the world. She had learned to tell the difference. A dream will offer you a memory to confirm what you want to believe, and the memory will feel real because the dreaming mind is generous with its lies. But a vision offers nothing. It simply shows you what is, and lets you decide what to do with it.

This was not a dream.

She traveled underground, her nonphysical body passing unhindered through steel and concrete. She discovered rows of small shops and trading stalls, eateries and casinos, and thousands of lamps and lanterns lining the cavernous walls throughout the city. It was noisy, full of people coming and going, each one trying to buy or sell something. As she passed through the crowded warren of passageways and side streets, she came upon the same group she had seen before. She could barely make out their voices at first, but the closer she drew to them the more distinct they became. They seemed to be discussing the city and its inhabitants.

"We met with Gunther today. Got a feel for his operation. There was an exhibition bout to prove my sincerity. And my ability." She overheard Po describe. The dusky candlelight drew slender shadows that appeared to argue animatedly across the walls of the room they occupied.

"Dat explains de bruises." Dade said.

"And the cut over your eyebrow." Rensa pointed out.

"He told us about a championship prize fight taking place in a week that I could attend. Good pay. Barter coin, but it would give us the money to get the weapons and medicine we need. What did you guys find out?" Po asked.

Dade turned to Rensa. "Do you want me to go first?" Rensa tilted her head and deferred the floor to him with an open hand. "Ok, I'll go first. So, dere are five rival gangs in Solos. De Hotheads, also known as de Reds. Dey're Gunther's men, known by de red sash dey wear eider on dere arm or dere leg."

Po interjected, looking at ROGER. "They must have been the ones we saw at the pits today."

Dade shot her a look. "I was gettin' to dat. No bullets, o' course. Just for show." He recovered his rhythm. "After dat we have de Gents. Dey run de brot'els. Some fella calls himself Fat Jack's dere topman."

"Dat's what dey call him. I don't name 'em, I just report 'em." Dade ticked the next one off on his fingers. "Den we have de Bullriders. No tellin' how many o' dem dere are. Dese guys are mainly offgrid. Dey handle protection for de traders, bootleggers and smugglers along de Spice Road."

Po stopped him. "Spice Road?"

Po nodded. Trinidad's nonphysical body stood in rapt attention as they discussed the Spice Road and their plans for the following week. Once she returned to her physical body she would have to determine where this city known as Solos was located, she decided.

"Dose were probably de guys we ran across on de way here, come to tink of it." Dade looked away thoughtfully and then shook it off. "Anyway, finally dere's de Po' Boys, led by a guy named Randall. Rensa drew an arcing motion with her hand and simulated a dive bomb sound as she brought it crashing down onto the table, then mimicked the sound of an explosion.

"Men tend to be free with their words in the presence of an attractive woman." She said confidently.

Trinidad's nonphysical body snapped back into her physical one and she was startled awake. "Looks like I have at least a week to catch up with them." She said to herself.

Later that next morning Trinidad described to Kabir the place she had seen in her vision. He said it reminded him of a city he had once visited with the traders called the Free City of Solos, a large underground barter town that acted as a central hub for merchants, traders and smugglers. Trinidad filed the name away. Once she had secured passage with the trade master, Solos would be her destination.

Leo presented himself to the council of elders caparisoned in brilliant vesture. He moved gracefully to the rostrum where he planned to address the men and women of the clergy. As he did so he made sure all eyes were on him when he made a grand display of removing his persona. Shock spilled through the crowd as the murmurs of the people could not be contained. Leo began.

"My dear clergy, I would like to begin by thanking you for attending this emergency convocation. I know you probably all have somewhere else you would rather be, so I will make this brief. A cacophony of "Omaha's" filled the auditorium, and Leo's first speech as Chief Cleric had been delivered. He was a startlingly powerful speaker, and a strong leader. There was no denying that. And he would weaponize the faith, crushing anyone who opposed them and the safety of their people.

The only problem was nobody opposed them, or their safety.

Among all the pomp and circumstance of his speech, everyone seemed to forget that Leo had removed his persona and never replaced it. Now that he was the acting leader of a militarized religious order, anybody who might have had something to say about it went silent out of fear they might be counted a heretic. The new Chief Cleric had radicalized his followers and begun ruling the ecclesia with an iron fist.

Just outside the city, in the cottages nestled along the outskirts, Trinidad and Riley were completely oblivious to the organization of fanatics preparing to arrest her followers and launch a campaign to discover their whereabouts.

It was a peaceful day. Ramesh and Kabir taught Riley how to cast a net to trap the garbage and debris that escaped the rocks below the sewers and flowed out into the shallow riverbed. Riley smiled and clapped to alert the Sharmas of a particularly nice haul. Sometimes Ramesh would pick something valuable out of the rocks and toss it toward the river just so Riley's net could capture it. The boy did not know. He did not need to know. This small deception provided more entertainment for him than all of his days spent in the city combined. Riley's laughter was infectious, and Ramesh was beside himself with pleasure at the little joy he could offer the boy.

Trinidad had taken a liking to Safi and stayed in the house while the boys were outside. She watched everything through Riley's eyes, however, so she did not miss a thing.

Safi read aloud, her small voice giving life to heroes and monsters she would never see, while Trinidad filled her imagination with the wondrous story. She was transported away from where she was, as a good book so often will do, and for a brief and tender moment she forgot the state of the world, and her place in it.

Ramesh and Kabir were gathering their nets for the day and helped Riley bring in his haul. The sun was going down in the west, painting the sky with red and orange lights. In the east, the sky was already a deep blue with hints of rich purple. As the men brought in the nets Napreet came out of the house, where she had been preparing dinner, to help them.

"You boys got quite a nice haul today!" Napreet commented. "Perhaps you should take Riley with you every day." She said, smiling at Riley.

Riley beamed with pride at his bounty.

They brought the nets into the house and Napreet began separating them while the men changed out of their dirty clothes and got washed up for the evening meal. With Riley back in the house, Trinidad could see to help with whatever needed to be done. The days went by quicker than expected.

When the traders came into town Safi was so distraught at losing her new friend that she cried upon hearing the news.

"Just one more day, please! I will be good! I promise, please Mama!" She begged her mother to let Trinidad stay, her lower lip trembling.

"It is not about you being good or bad, Safi. It is time for Ms. Trinidad and Riley to leave." Napreet explained to her daughter.

Trinidad knelt down and took Safi's hands in hers. "I have to go, my dear Safi. But one day, we will meet again. And when we do you can read to me the one about the magicians. The one we never got to finish. Would you like that?"

Safi could not answer. She threw her arms around Trinidad and buried her face in the folds of her gown, her small body shaking with the kind of grief that only a child can carry so openly. Trinidad held her, saying nothing, letting the girl cry. There was nothing to say. Nothing like Trinidad had ever come into Safi's home, and perhaps nothing like her ever would again. The love they shared was gentle and pure, and it had been given freely, and it had not been enough time. It is never enough time.

After a long moment Safi pulled back and wiped her eyes with the backs of her hands. She looked up at Trinidad, who could not see her but smiled down at her all the same.

"You will come back?" Safi asked.

"I will come back." Trinidad said.

Kabir had spoken with the trade master and he had agreed to allow Trinidad and Riley to travel with the caravan provided they each carry a pack and do exactly as they were told. Trinidad and Riley were given ghillie suits, heavy cloaks camouflaged to resemble the natural terrain, and taught what to do when a spotter raised the red flag: cover yourself completely and drop as low to the ground as you can. From above, the alien airships would see only what appeared to be an outcropping of rocks or bushes and would continue on their way.

They said their goodbyes and joined the caravan.

The trade master's name was Karesh. He was a wiry, sun-darkened man who spoke little and observed everything. He did not use maps. No trade master did. A map could be captured, and a captured map would betray every trading post along the road. Instead, Karesh navigated the way sailors once navigated the open sea: by the stars, by the position of the sun, by a mental record of every trip he had ever made. He counted his paces. He noted the wind. He knew how many hours of walking lay between one waypoint and the next, and he adjusted his route constantly, because the Spice Road was never the same road twice. Posts closed. Posts moved. Posts were discovered by the creatures and had to be abandoned overnight. A trade master who walked the same path twice was a trade master who got people killed.

The caravan was sixty strong, and they carried thousands of pounds between them. Trinidad felt the weight of her pack settle into her shoulders and said nothing. Riley walked beside her, his eyes scanning the horizon, her scout whether he knew it or not. The spotters ranged ahead and to the flanks, watching the sky for the dark shapes of alien airships, their red flags folded and ready.

They walked. The Spice Road stretched before them, invisible and ancient, a lifeline drawn across the dust by the feet of those who refused to stop moving. Dried spices, peppers, fruits and vegetables, herbs and roots. The last remaining pleasure on Earth, carried on human backs from one hiding place to the next. Food had become the most valuable commodity on the planet, and these were the people who kept it flowing.

Trinidad walked among them with the prophecy already delivered, already placed on a mantle next to a lion god by a child who thought it was just a poem. Ahead of her lay Solos, and the strangers she had seen in her vision, and whatever came after that.

Behind her, a little girl sat on a sofa holding a book about magicians she would have to finish alone.
