The group stood still and lowered the duffel bags and packs onto the ground slowly. "Now, everybody step away from the bags. Head forward, no turning around. WALK!" I've got marksmen with crossbows pointed at you, so if you move, well ... you know, an arrow through the heart." There was nothing they could do. No way of knowing whether the voice was telling the truth or bluffing. They were forced to comply. The sounds of shuffling, unzipping and rezipping, movement, then silence.

In a hushed tone John said to Po, "I think they're gone." Slowly, he snaked his neck around, and saw nothing. He jumped up. "They're gone. Get up, spread out, see if you can see them. John bent down and touched his fingers to the ground, picked up some sand, and let it fall from his hand, then looked to the rocky crag faces. "They knew this area very well. "Come on, Jayk, don't be naive! Dis was all her idea! I've ne'r even heard o' dis Solos place anyway! Dis has all been a trap from de very beginnin'!" Dade said, getting louder and more certain.

"Alright, alright, calm down. We don't know anything for sure. And, until we arrive at Solos and let the truth bear itself out, it's all just opinion." Po reasoned.

"We'll never make it now. Dey took all our supplies. If dere's not a city, we'll die out here!" Dade argued.

"Why would Ideo bring us out here to die? That'd be suicide. It makes no sense." John said. "What actually does make sense is that somebody saw the bag we left behind and tracked our movements until we got to the rocks, a place where they could make an escape without leaving a trace. It also gives them a clear shot. It's called a box trap, and it's highly effective."

"Do we have any food or water left?" Po asked.

"I have water." John replied.

"So do I." Jayk chimed in.

"Ok, so we have enough water if we ration it. According to the map Ideo made, this ridge is about a day's hike from the outer limits of the city, which is located in the valley. She said we'd see the abandoned structures before we got to the entranceway. That will be proof she was telling the truth." Po informed them all. "Let's get moving. Without the cargo the trip should be quicker and easier."

They struck off once again in the direction of Solos. This time with suspicion settling between them like dust.

Dade caught up with Rensa and spoke to her in sotto voce. "I've been against dis from de very beginnin'."

"I know." Rensa replied.

"Well, don't you agree wit me? We gotta do sometin'. I'm not gonna die out here."

"Where are we gonna go, Dade? Let's be reasonable for just a moment. Think about it. She wouldn't drag us out here to die, killin' herself in the process. What's in it for her? I know it looks like she's responsible in hindsight, but plannin' somethin' like this for months, even years ... Dade, it's lunacy."

Dade was wrapped in silence as he walked beside her.

"Let's just wait a day, do that for me. If the city doesn't exist, then I'll kill her myself." Rensa said.

"Do you really think it was Ideo?" Sarah questioned John.

"I doubt it. It doesn't make sense. I've never found her to be dishonest. Now, Dade, if you told me it was him ... I'd probably believe you. But, Ideo? No, not at all." John stated.

"I agree. Ideo was genuinely concerned about making this trip and seeing her family again. She was almost against it."

"I guess we'll find out soon enough." John said.

"You want to tell me that story now?" She asked him, smiling coyly.

"Are you ever going to stop asking me?"

"Doubtful."

"Fine." He said, cutting her a narrow look with his eyes. "There was this man..."

"Yes." She said cheerily.

"Fine. Once upon a time there was this man who loved his wife very much. Then one day the wife became ill and died. The man went to the shaman for his village. The shaman prepared an acorn for him and told him to burn an item that belonged to her and then plant the acorn in the ground that same night. If you don't burn the item that belonged to her when she comes back to life, her spirit will not recognize her body and she won't know who you are. You must do this exactly as I have instructed you. The man went home that night and found her ponytail that he had cut off after she died. This was traditional in their culture. They would weave the hair into a bracelet or necklace or some other jewelry in order to carry the person with them forever. He was reluctant to burn it, so he decided to just plant the acorn instead. The next day he found his wife alive and standing in the field, disoriented. When he tried to speak to her she didn't recognize him and she screamed, recoiling in fear. This hurt the man very much, worse even than the pain of losing his wife, because now his wife was afraid of him."

"What did he do?" Sarah asked.

"He went back to the shaman who promptly admonished him for disobeying his instructions. He told the man that he must find the woman and kill her, and this time perform the ceremony exactly as he was told. The shaman gave him another acorn, and explained the ritual to him again. This time the man did as he was told, and the next morning he found his wife waiting for him. They were together again and happy."

"It's just a folktale, they don't have morals." John replied.

"Oh come on, there has to be a moral." Sarah insisted.

"Especially when bringing the dead back to life." She added.

"That's for sure." He agreed.

Neither of them said what they were both thinking. They didn't need to.

They continued walking. The sun set and a new moon left the landscape bathed in darkness, the only light cast by the stars. The group moved slowly, stopping only to relieve themselves, most of them determined to prove Dade wrong. Jayk had the best eyesight of all of them, and he was the first to see it. Just a glint, like the twinkling of a jewel lost in a riverbed. "I see something!" He called out.

"I see it too! A reflection coming from that direction." Sarah pointed toward the light source.

There, in the valley, lay the Free City of Solos exactly where Ideo said it would be. They took off at a full sprint.

From above, the city was nothing. Boarded-up buildings, defunct businesses, empty homes covered by debris and sagebrush that made them indistinguishable from the surrounding terrain. A graveyard of a place. The kind you walk past without slowing down.

Below was a different world entirely.

They found the entrance where Ideo's map said it would be: an abandoned subway turnstile, broken to allow travelers through without any token. They descended deeper into the station, following her instructions toward an old electrical subsystem once used by technicians, and down to a steel door where a single man stood waiting.

"Who are you here to see?" The man asked.

Without Ideo's help they were lost. They looked at each other, and then Po stepped forward. "We're here to see Sun Yi at the Red Fin."

The man stepped aside, opened the door, and they entered the Free City of Solos.

It hit them all at once. The clanging of pots and pans, barkers crying out their specials, a mob of barefoot children running past them howling with laughter. The lower parking lots and abandoned sewers had been connected into a labyrinth of trading posts, restaurants, casinos, fighting pits, bars, brothels, even a library and hospital. Gaslights flickered along the corridors. Steam hissed from pipes overhead. The air smelled of broth and sweat and candle wax. A cacophony of human existence, thrumming through the tunnels, filling every passage and stairwell. And beneath all of it, beneath the shouting and the bartering and the laughter, a silence that Sarah noticed and no one else did. A silence where something should have been but wasn't. She couldn't name it. Not yet.

They had reached it. The city that wasn't supposed to exist.

Po was quick to warn Sarah. "Keep your coat on at all times, especially in here. If someone were to see what you're carrying it could be very bad for you. For all of us." Sarah nodded and used her right hand to pull the oversized coat further over her left arm, concealing the alien device completely.

"I was wrong." Dade said, taking it all in. "I admit it, I was wrong."

Rensa smiled broadly and took his arm in hers. "Told you! Oh ye of little faith!"

"Oh, don't give me dat. You were ready to kill her as fast as I was."

"I said that to calm you down. I never had a doubt in my mind." Rensa said.

"We came here to find the Red Fin, so let's get to it." Po reminded them.

They asked the locals, who pointed them through the winding passages while trying to ply their wares: clothing, hats, daggers, eyewear, foodstuffs. The city was a bazaar of monumental import, hidden beneath the skin of the world. The Red Fin was a tiny restaurant, a literal hole in the wall that sat no more than forty people comfortably. Its sign hung above the entrance on rusted iron chains: a whale's fin painted red on a wooden plank. Once found, they brought Ideo into the small eatery and asked for Sun Yi. From the back, a tiny woman teetered out wiping her hands on an apron around her waist. She recognized her daughter immediately. She ran to her. She spoke in Japanese to another woman who took off running out of the building.

"Where did you find her? Is she alive?" Sun Yi begged ROGER for answers.

"She is stable, but she's been out a long time. Almost too long. We need medicine that I don't have. Epinephrine or adrenaline." ROGER said.

"I can give her Hawthorn. Gotu Kola. But we don't have what she really needs. Someone will have to find it." Sun Yi stated.

"What does she really need?" Jayk asked.

"Raceweed. It's a powerful herb. The root is poisonous, but when made into a tea it stimulates the heart. That's what she needs now." Sun Yi instructed.

"How do we find this Raceweed?" Po asked.

"It used to grow wild. Now, I haven't seen it in years. Maybe it doesn't grow any more. But that's what she needs." Sun Yi said.

Po consulted with ROGER and Jayk separately, leaving Ideo in her mother's care. "How does she know this herb is what her daughter needs?"

"The only thing I can determine is that she has given it to her daughter before. Ideo may have a condition we don't know about. Perhaps a heart or lung condition that doesn't present itself immediately." ROGER suggested.

"If she says that herb is what Ideo needs, then that's what she needs. I'll try to find it." Jayk offered.

"Ok. The rest of us will have to find a way to make up for the lost items we had planned to barter with." Po said, then turned back to Sun Yi. "You mentioned something about finding Ideo?"

"Yes. She disappeared years ago when she was just nineteen. Her father, he has been looking for her ever since. So have I." Sun Yi explained.

"I've known Ideo for several years now. She's been living with us in a defunct military bunker a few hundred kilometers from here to the west. She's been a very valuable asset to us. We're grateful to have her." Po stated.

"I'm more grateful to have her home. Her father will be happy to see his only daughter. I'm sure of that." Sun Yi continued. "Thank you for rescuing her. For taking care of her."

"You're welcome, but I should tell you, your daughter doesn't need anyone taking care of her. She's a very strong woman." Po said. "We have to leave her with you, but we will return. Hopefully, with the medicine she needs."

"Thank you." Sun Yi bowed ever so slightly. She looked at all of the weather-beaten travelers and decided they needed to eat. "Please take some food. My Ramen will give you energy for your travels."

Po thanked her and they all took a seat at a small table in the crowded restaurant. While they waited for their meal they decided on a plan. "We should split up, we'll cover more ground that way." John suggested. "There's got to be something we could do in this city to make money."

"Ideo mentioned a man named Gunther who runs a fighting pit, didn't she?" Po asked John.

"He was going to be our contact for the weapons. She wanted me to train some of his fighters. Give them some pointers." John said.

"Ok, I'll look for Gunther. ROGER, you come with me. Rensa and Dade, you two see if you can find a contact for medicine. Antibiotics, painkillers, anything useful. John, you and Sarah see what you can come up with." Po said.

"I'll look for the herb." Jayk said. "Somebody's bound to know somethin' about it around here. After all, Ideo said we could find anything we wanted in Solos."

"We can meet back up here tonight." Po suggested.

The Ramen arrived. The soup was extraordinary. Better than anything they had ever tasted. They devoured the meal and were truly energized by it, just as Sun Yi said they would be.

"Sun Yi, this is remarkable. How on earth do you make it?" Po asked.

"It is tradition. Days to prepare. The pot has never been washed. That is the secret." She paused. "I tried to teach Ideo. She is not interested in cooking. Too domestic for her, she says." Sun Yi shook her head disapprovingly. "My daughter fights. She does not cook. So the art may die with me."

Once they had finished, they left Ideo in her mother's capable hands and set out to find what they came for.

Solos was a place that could swallow you whole. The serpentine paths created by rows of small shops and stalls wound through the underground city like the tunnels of a rabbit warren, and getting lost was not a matter of if but when. Every corner offered a new distraction, a new vendor, a new voice calling out. The city of ten thousand candles earned its name honestly: gaslights, lanterns, oil lamps, and open flames lit the underground passages in amber and shadow, giving the whole place the warmth of something sacred and the danger of something that could burn.

They split up.

Dade found a casino. His talents put him uniquely suited for the tables, and he sat down to earn what he could with the only skills that mattered in a place like this.

Rensa was on her way to find a brothel when something stopped her. A silversmith's stall, tucked between a weapons dealer and a cobbler, lit by a single oil lamp that caught the metalwork and made it sing. Nose guards. Ear cuffs. Septum rings hammered from reclaimed silver into shapes that were part armor, part jewelry. Fierce and delicate at the same time. She picked up a nose guard, a crescent of hammered silver with fine etched lines that caught the candlelight. The woman behind the stall watched her with knowing eyes. Rensa held it up to her face and looked at her reflection in a sliver of polished metal. The same hands that gripped a crowbar turned the piece over gently, tracing the craftsmanship with her thumb. She didn't have anything to trade for it. Not yet. But she remembered where the stall was. A child of the demimonde, she knew how to gather information from brothel staff better than anyone. She'd start there. But she'd be back for the silver. Po and ROGER went looking for the fighting pits. John and Sarah found a bar where they could strike up conversations with travelers and locals. And Jayk went alone, searching for the one thing that could save Ideo's life.

Jayk wandered deeper into the city, running his fingers across silk and woolen clothing at one of the stalls. Having hunted virtually all the mammals into extinction, the inhabitants had been forced to resort to the painstaking process of unthreading fabric from clothing left over after the Dark Epoch and then reweaving it by hand. The result was astonishing. Long coats and top hats, silk neckties and vests. The dress in the free city could compete with any 19th-century French haberdashery. From gaslights to steam-powered clockwork mechanisms, Solos was abound with gadgetry and contraptions that would make Rube Goldberg jealous. Jayk was marveling at the ingenuity of a clever device when he heard an old man crying out in pain.

"Ohhhhhh ..." The withered old man complained. "Ohhhhh ..."

Jayk came over and crouched down beside him. "What's wrong? Are you hurting?"

"Oh, what do you care?" The old man said and spit at Jayk's feet.

"There's no need to be rude. Maybe I can help." Jayk replied gently.

"There ain't nothin' you can do! Now, move along!"

"Why don't you leave me alone? What's wrong with you? I'm tired and hungry, and I want to be left alone! Just let me complain in peace!" The old man argued. "You want me to go away, but I won't! Let them see what they're doin'!"

"I don't understand, but I can at least get you something to eat. Will you let me do that for you?" Jayk said as he reached for the old man's arm and gently helped him to his feet.

"Fine! You can buy me a meal, but after that leave me be!" The old man argued, but went along with Jayk just the same.

The pair of them came to a stall with a few rusty tables and chairs set out for patrons. Jayk sat the man down and went to order him something to eat. "Excuse me, I don't have any money, but maybe I could barter something with you?" He said, reaching into his pocket. He pulled out a gold coin with an eagle imprinted on it, rescuing it from some lint and dirt. "This is very rare and valuable. My mother gave it to me. It's all I have to remember her by."

The cook took the coin in his hand. Flipped it over. Examined the inscription. Looked at Jayk. "What am I supposed to do with this? Useless hunk of metal!" He looked Jayk up and down. "Your sword. Let me see it." Jayk unsheathed his sword from his back and presented it to the cook. "Now, this has some value. Nicely balanced. I'll give you a meal, and you can rent the bed upstairs for the week in return for the sword."

Jayk thought about it for a moment and looked back at the old man, who was forlorn and miserable, and said over his shoulder to the cook, "Throw in meals for the week and you've got a deal." They shook hands. Jayk removed his scabbard and handed it over. "For the sword. Keep it covered."

The cook came out with the meal and offered it to the old man. "This your father?" The cook asked.

The old man took offense. "Hell no! Do you think I'm as ugly as he is?! If he were my son, I'd have shaved his head and taught him to walk backwards!"

"No, he's just a friend." Jayk smiled, stifling a laugh, as the cook headed back to the kitchen.

"This don't make us friends! If it's friends you want, better go to a brothel. With the looks of you, you could only buy friends, or rent them by the hour!" The old man chided him between slurps of soup and chunks of stale bread. "That what you want? A friend?"

Po and ROGER wandered the narrow streets marveling at the depth of the city.

"How could a city like this survive for so long without being detected by the enemy?" Po asked.

"I suppose it's like Ideo said. Anonymity." ROGER reminded him.

Po stopped at a stall and examined some of the scarves on display. "This is some fine work." He remarked. The shopkeeper nodded, thanking him. "We're looking for someone by the name of Gunther. Do you know where we might find him?" The shopkeeper's smile disappeared. He demanded they move along.

They walked on a bit before Po said quietly to ROGER, "This man Gunther is either not well liked, or the people who associate with him aren't. Either way that puts us at a disadvantage. Perhaps we should just inquire about the fighting pits themselves, and go from there."

"Agreed." ROGER concurred.

They passed a few bars before stopping at a casino. "Somebody in there should know something." The pair walked inside and went up to a man behind a cage. "We're looking for the fighting pits." The man simply pointed his finger to another man dressed in a suit and tie with a red sash around his arm. A beefy man with a thick neck. "Thank you." Po said, and went up to the well-dressed man. "We're looking for the fighting pits."

The man looked them both up and down before asking, "You lookin' to fight or watch?"

"We're looking for Gunther. Someone told us he may be able to get something for us." Po replied.

"Well, I can take you there, but I doubt he'll speak to you unless you plan on fightin'. He ain't much for conversation."

The man led them out of the casino and around a back way behind the main thoroughfare, down some rickety steps to a doorway that led to even more rickety steps, which opened into a large hall with a caged ring raised on planks. "If I see this kind of show tomorrow night, you'll both be workin' the road hawkin' herbs and roots!! Half of fightin' is the show!! So stop kissin' each other and fight!!" The heavyset man in the dress shirt bellowed. His sleeves were rolled back displaying two large forearms with thick cords of muscle matted with dark fur.

"Wait here." The guide said. He went up to the big man and said something unintelligible.

The big man shouted to the two of them. "You two here to fight?" He turned and looked, inspecting ROGER with a pair of deep-set eyes. Something flickered across his face, brief and unreadable. "No, 'course you aren't. Not you anyway. You look more like the type that fixes things." He held ROGER's gaze a beat too long, then turned to Po. "Maybe the bald one. "I am. Who are you?" Gunther replied.

Turning to John, Jayk said, "I suppose you can come, too. You've got pretty good survival skills."
