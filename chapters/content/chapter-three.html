Nightfall approached like a judgment he hadn't earned.

John's hunger had become a creature of its own, gnawing at the hollow place behind his ribs with teeth he couldn't name. He had no way of knowing when he had last eaten. Hours? Days? The last clear memory was a sandwich from Magulias' Deli on 54th Avenue, the bread still warm, the mustard sharp on his tongue. A small pleasure from a world that no longer existed.

From the look of him, it could have been years.

He watched the creatures from behind the rocks, motionless as the stone itself. They moved in formation through the ruined city: systematic, purposeful, their massive silhouettes black against the darkening sky. Soldiers. Not mindless beasts. His trained eye recognized the spacing between units, the way their heads turned in coordinated sweeps. Military discipline transcended species, apparently.

*Improvise. Adapt. Overcome.*

The mantra surfaced unbidden, old as boot leather and just as worn. He let it settle into the rhythm of his breathing and waited.

When the last of them disappeared beyond the horizon, he moved.

He crossed from the desert to the first outlying structures at a measured pace, conserving energy, scanning for threats. The storefronts gaped like mouths frozen mid-scream. Whatever life had once bustled through these streets; shoppers, children, dogs on leashes... had been pinched out entirely. Not a weed broke through the sidewalk cracks. Not a bird called from the empty eaves.

A rusted vehicle caught his eye. He crouched beside it and examined the license plate. The tax sticker read '34.

Two years after the last date he could remember.

"Two whole years?" The words escaped before he could stop them, loud in the silence.

He looked in every direction; between the stores, along the sidewalks, into the parks where swings hung motionless on their chains. Nothing green remained. The world had been bled of color and life, leaving only the bleached bones of what had been.

Night settled over the ruins like a shroud. A sliver of moonlight penetrated the cloud cover, throwing pale geometry across his path. Beyond that thin illumination: nothing. The darkness was total, absolute, a negation of sight that pressed against his eyes like cloth.

He stood for a moment and took inventory of what he needed. Water. Food. Shelter. In that order. His training reasserted itself, the procedures drilled so deep they'd survived whatever had happened to him in that facility.

*Three days without water. Maybe longer if I don't burn energy.*

Water heaters. The thought arrived with the clarity of a briefing room lecture. After a natural disaster; earthquake, hurricane, blizzard... between thirty and sixty gallons of clean drinking water could be found in residential water heaters. Find a heater. Find a pharmacy for bleach and iodine. The sun would handle sterilization.

He set out.

The first building had been an insurance office. Tattered posters lined the walls, their cheerful slogans made obscene by context: Plan for Tomorrow. Protect What Matters Today. Life Insurance for Peace of Mind. He moved through the rooms in silence, checking corners, listening. In a closet he found an old coat, oversized, the fabric still sound. He pulled it on. First score.

The water heater had been crushed and drained dry.

The restaurant next door had been ransacked. Someone had taken everything useful; canned goods, utensils, chairs for firewood. All that remained was a bottle of disintegrated bleach and a container of salt. Even the water heater was empty. He found a length of hose coiled behind the stove and took it anyway.

Building after building told the same story. The survivors before him had known what to take: weapons, food, anything that could cut or carry. They'd left behind the useless artifacts of civilization; hair dryers, irons, a child's electronic toy with dead batteries.

In a ransacked convenience store, he found a bag of balloons. Yellow, blue, red. The colors startled him. He shoved them into his pocket without knowing why.

Exhaustion finally caught him as dawn began to gray the eastern sky. He found a corner where two walls still met at their proper angle and let his body fold down onto the cold floor.

Sleep came like falling.

*"Wachichu, you see the way the wolves hunt in packs?"*

His uncle's voice carried across years and miles, arriving whole and warm as summer.

*"Yes, sir."*

*"That's family. The pack survives because they are together. Watch them."*

Three wolves had brought down a deer. They ate from its belly while the creature still breathed, its head lifted toward John in something that looked like pleading. Mercy. Help. Anything.

As he lay dying, John watched.

He knew he couldn't help the deer. He also knew he shouldn't. This was the pattern beneath all patterns, the rhythm the Earth had been drumming since before humans walked it.

*"That is the nature of things, Chichu. The deer eats the fruit. The wolf eats the deer."*

The landscape shifted. Suddenly he was small again, back on the reservation, the schoolyard dirt hot beneath his feet.

*"Chichu! Chichu! Wouldn't wanna meetchu!"*

The boy's laughter cut like glass. John watched himself run at the boy, push him down, jump on top of him and start swinging. The other children formed a circle around them, their voices rising in that ancient playground chorus. But no matter how hard John hit, the boy kept laughing. The sound wouldn't stop. It never stopped.

The dream morphed again.

His mother's kitchen. The smell of fry bread and sage. Evening light slanting through windows that needed washing.

*"Wachichu, come and eat."*

He sat down sullenly, refusing to meet her eyes. *"Don't call me that. The kids make fun of me at school because you call me that."*

His mother looked at him with the kind of love that hurts. Her hands, rough from work, folded in her lap. She had spent her whole life healing others. She could not heal this.

*"Wachichu, you cannot deny what you are. Your father loved you very much, but he's gone. You have to decide for yourself who you're going to be."*

He stared at the table. At his hands. At anything but her face.

*"But who am I? What am I supposed to be?"*

His mother took his hand. Her palm was warm and calloused, and it held his the way she'd held it when he was too small to walk alone.

*"You be you. Don't look at it as a handicap, look at it as a gift. The best of both worlds."*

She kissed his forehead. His big brown eyes stared up at her, wanting to believe, wanting to belong to something, wanting the world to stop asking him to choose between halves of himself that couldn't be separated without drawing blood.

The dream shifted again, years scrolling past like pages in the wind.

Basic training. Parris Island. The heat like a physical weight, the drill instructors' voices like weather you couldn't escape.

Another Marine; young, buzz-cut, earnest... saw where John had scrawled *WACHICHU* on his helmet in black marker.

*"What's that mean?"*

*"White man."* John's voice was different now. Deeper. Steadier. His body had caught up with his will, and nobody teased him anymore. *"It's a nickname my mom used to call me. She was Native American. My father was white."*

*"That's cool."*

*"It wasn't cool when I was a kid. The boys on the reservation used to tease me. I got into a lot of fights growing up."*

*"Must've made you pretty damn tough."*

*"I guess so."* John hadn't thought about it that way. He'd just been trying to survive. Trying to find somewhere he fit in. *"I never really thought about it. I guess I just always wanted to belong somewhere, and never really felt like I did. I wasn't white, I wasn't red. I was all alone."*

The other Marine clapped him on the shoulder. Somewhere behind them, another private walked past completely naked, towel in hand, heading for the showers. They both looked at each other and laughed.

*"Doesn't matter. You're a Marine now. You're part of something. And you'll never be alone again."*

John woke with the words already in his mouth:

"But I'm alone right now."

The sound of his own voice startled him. He lay on the cold floor of a building he didn't remember entering, dawn light filtering through cracks in the walls, the ruins of a dead civilization pressing in from every direction.

The wolves had eaten the deer. The children had laughed. His mother had loved him with everything she had, and it hadn't been enough to make him whole.

But she had given him something. A name that meant white man in a tongue older than English. A gift that looked like a wound. A wound that had made him strong enough to survive this.

Whatever this was.

He rose. Checked the coat, the hose, the bag of colored balloons in his pocket.

*Improvise. Adapt. Overcome.*

The world had ended. The world had always been ending, one way or another. The deer died so the wolves could live. The old world died so something new could rise from its bones.

Amorfati. The love of fate. His mother's people had understood this truth before the philosophers gave it a name.

John D'Arby stepped out into the pale morning light and began, once more, to walk.

They reached the airfield at dusk.

The planes lay where they had died: cargo jets with their bellies split open, helicopters listing on broken rotors, small aircraft scattered across the tarmac like birds that had forgotten how to fly. Rust had claimed them all. Whatever cargo they'd carried, whatever passengers they'd ferried, whatever destinations they'd promised... all of it silenced now. A congregation of the grounded, waiting for a service that would never come.

The group had been chattering; Dade and Rensa trading barbs, Oso laughing at something no one else found funny... when Po raised his hand.

"Quiet."

They dropped like shadows behind the nearest wreckage. Above them, an alien airship slid through the darkening sky, its hull catching the last light like the carapace of some enormous insect. It moved without sound, without urgency, scanning the earth below with the patience of something that knew it had already won.

No one breathed.

The ship passed. Continued into the distance. One by one, they emerged from their hiding places, brushing dust from their clothes, trading looks that said what no one wanted to say aloud: *They're everywhere now.*

"We'll stop here," Po announced. "Still a full day to the bunker. Eat. Rest. Then we move."

Jayk found a patch of ground near a collapsed fuel truck and lowered himself onto it. His body ached in places he couldn't name. The headache had faded to a dull throb behind his eyes, but the emptiness remained; that strange hollow where memory should be.

Po settled beside him and offered a dented container. Inside: a chunk of green paste, viscous and unappealing. Algae, probably. Jayk took it without comment.

"How's your lip?" he asked.

Po's grin split his weathered face. "How's your head?"

"Better." Jayk rubbed the back of his skull where the pain had been sharpest. "I think."

"Does that happen often?" Po watched him with the careful attention of someone who had learned to read people the way others read weather. "The collapsing."

Jayk considered the question. The truth was worse than he wanted to admit.

"More frequently lately." He stared at the paste in his hands, not seeing it. "I couldn't tell you where I was coming from. Or where I was headed." He looked around at the dead planes, the cracked tarmac, the bruised sky. "Where are we?"

"Nowhere worth naming." Po slurped his portion of the paste with the enthusiasm of a man who had long since stopped expecting food to taste like anything. "We found the bunker a few years back; Wax, myself, a few others. Been hiding out ever since." He wiped his mouth with the back of his hand. "Lately the airships have been coming more often. Wax thought they might've planted another garden nearby, so we went to check."

"And?"

"He was right. Big one, too. Must've been there a while."

They sat in silence. The sky deepened from purple to black. Somewhere in the distance, metal groaned as wind moved through the graveyard of aircraft.

"So what's your story?" Po asked finally. "Running from something?"

Jayk turned the question over in his mind. Running from something. Running toward something. He couldn't remember which. Maybe both. Maybe neither.

"The last thing I can remember clearly is my parents." The words came slowly, pulled from somewhere deep. "Being killed. By those... things." He stopped. Swallowed. "Everything else is fog."

Po said nothing. Just waited.

"I was raised by the Church after that. The adherents. Learned to fight. Learned to survive." Jayk shook his head slowly. "But how I got here, to that post office where you found me... I couldn't tell you. It's like waking up in the middle of a sentence someone else started."

"That's rough." Po's voice carried no pity, only acknowledgment. In this world, everyone had lost something. The details varied; the weight was the same.

"Anyway." Jayk met his eyes. "Here I am."

"Here you are." Po clapped him on the shoulder; firm, brief, the gesture of a man who understood that some wounds couldn't be healed, only carried. "Wax'll get you sorted. He's good at reading people. Better than me, anyway."

"High praise."

"Don't let it go to your head." Po stood, brushing dirt from his knees. "Get some rest. We've still got a ways to go."

He moved off to check on the others, leaving Jayk alone among the remains of machines that had once defied gravity.

Above him, the stars emerged one by one; cold, indifferent, unchanged by everything that had happened below. He wondered if his parents could see them from wherever they were now. If they were anywhere at all.

He closed his eyes and let the darkness take him.

Sarah woke to the sound of dripping.

It came from everywhere; walls, ceiling, the dark corners she couldn't see... a constant percussion that marked time in a place where time had stopped meaning anything. The structure sweated around her, moisture beading on stone and running in thin rivulets to pool on the floor. The air was thick, fetid, registering on her senses like static on a frequency she couldn't tune out.

Her body screamed with a hunger she had no framework to understand. She had never eaten solid food. The realization arrived with clinical clarity: her stomach had never processed anything but the nutrient solution of the chamber. This was her first true hunger, and it was enormous.

Lights flooded the cell without warning.

The others moved toward the door like a current finding its channel; automatic, resigned. Creatures she couldn't look at directly slopped thick yellow mush into metal bowls, and the prisoners descended on it. They scooped the substance into their mouths with bare hands, letting it run down their chins and stain the rags that passed for clothing. No utensils. No dignity. Just input and consumption, the biological minimum required to keep the machinery of their bodies running another day.

Sarah watched. Her stomach clenched. She turned away and vomited onto the floor.

For a long time after, she sat alone, staring at her own hands. These hands that had never held a bow. These fingers that had never pressed strings against a fingerboard to draw out the deep resonant tones of a cello she remembered playing but had never touched.

*Whose hands are these? Whose memories?*

The question had no answer. Only the dripping. Only the hunger growing louder than her revulsion.

Eventually, she dragged herself to one of the pails.

The sludge was vile; thick and faintly sulfurous, the color of infection. She plunged her hand into it and brought the substance to her mouth. The alternative was starvation. The alternative was surrender.

She ate.

Morning brought the chains.

The creatures linked the prisoners together in long lines and led them out into light so harsh it felt like violence. The sun baked their skin. The soil blackened their hands. They worked the fields from dawn until their shadows stretched long and thin across the furrows; plowing, tilling, ripping the earth open to receive seeds they didn't recognize.

They were terraforming their own planet. Rewriting it with their labor. The irony was not lost on Sarah, whose inherited memories included lectures on ecology and climate systems. She knew what they were building. She knew what would happen when it was finished.

The radiation bleached their hair. The work carved lines into their faces that wouldn't fade. Most wore rags or nothing at all. By the time the light began to fail, Sarah's body had become a catalogue of new sensations: blisters forming on her palms, muscles burning in configurations she'd never felt, skin tight and hot across her shoulders.

They were returned to the cells at nightfall; a maze of interlocking passageways, each corridor identical to the last, designed to disorient or simply built without regard for the comfort of its inhabitants. The prisoners shuffled to their designated spaces and collapsed.

Sarah pressed her back against the wall and pulled her knees to her chest.

Around her, the others had gone somewhere she couldn't follow. Their eyes held nothing. They moved through routines without intention, like recordings playing on damaged equipment. Whatever had made them people had been worn away by repetition and despair until only the biological remained.

One man sat in a murky puddle that seemed to reach toward him from all directions. He stared at nothing and spoke to no one:

"Every morning we get to leave this place. Every night they bring us back."

The words landed in Sarah's chest like a stone dropped into still water.

*Sisyphus*, she thought. The name surfaced from memories that weren't hers; a lecture hall, a professor's voice, a myth about a man condemned to roll a boulder up a hill for eternity, watching it roll back down each time he reached the summit.

This was that hill. These people had stopped believing they would ever reach the top.

The lights died. Darkness swallowed everything except a dim red glow that seemed to come from nowhere and illuminate nothing.

Sarah closed her eyes and tried to remember music. Any music. A phrase, a melody, something to hold against the silence.

Nothing came.

Just the dripping. Just the dark. Just the endless repetition of a song with no resolution.

The bunker swallowed them whole.

They entered one by one through a hidden passage that revealed itself only to those who knew where to look; a seam in the earth, a door disguised as debris. Steps descended into darkness, and the darkness gave way to the pale glow of fluorescent tubes that hummed their endless monotone hymn. The sound of it filled the corridors like an organ note held too long, vibrating in the bones.

Jayk followed the others through the labyrinth. Rooms branched off on either side, filled with the detritus of a world that had stopped making sense: boxes of tangled wires, machines whose purpose had been lost along with the people who built them, shelves of components that might have been treasure or trash depending on who was left to remember. The air smelled of recycled breath and old metal.

At a junction where the corridor split three ways, the group dispersed without ceremony. Rensa and Dade vanished down one passage, Oso down another. Po lingered.

"We'll leave you here." His voice echoed slightly off the concrete walls. "Bathroom's that way. Make yourself at home. Wax will find you and explain everything."

Then he was gone, and Jayk was alone in the belly of the earth.

He stood still for a moment, listening. Somewhere deeper in the facility, he could hear a sound; metal grinding against metal, rhythmic and labored. An ungreased machine perhaps, or something else entirely. The noise pulled at him like a thread he couldn't help but follow.

The corridor grew warmer as he walked. The fluorescent hum gave way to a different frequency: the whir of fans, the tick of cooling systems, the subtle percussion of a facility keeping itself alive. The smell of burning oil reached him before he found its source.

A doorway opened onto a washroom; filthy, functional, forgotten. Someone had left garments piled against the wall: leather trenches blackened by weather and use, scarves stiff with old sweat, boots worn through at the soles. The vestiges of survival. A basin stood beneath a single faucet, and above it, a mirror.

Jayk looked at the man reflected there.

He didn't recognize him.

The face was his; the same bones, the same eyes... but something had shifted. He had grown thin. Old before his time. His skin had taken on the texture of the wasteland itself, weathered and cracked. And behind his eyes, something was wrong. Something was missing.

The headaches had been getting worse. Not just pain now, but intrusion; memories that didn't belong to him crowding into his mind like uninvited congregants at a service he hadn't called. He remembered things he had never done. Faces he had never seen. Words in voices that weren't his own. It was as though his soul was being torn, page by page, and someone else's story was being written into the margins.

*Who am I becoming?*

The question had no answer. Only the hum of the lights and the drip of water from a faucet that wouldn't quite close.

A hand touched his shoulder.

He spun, reaching for a blade that wasn't there, and found himself facing soft, serious eyes set in a thin face. The man was tall, dressed in clothes that fit him precisely, as though he had been measured for them in another life. He smelled faintly of sweat and something else, something cleaner. Purpose, perhaps.

"Come with me, Jayk."

The voice was calm. Unhurried. The voice of someone who had stopped being afraid a long time ago.

Jayk followed.

"Where are we?" he asked as they walked.

"Among friends." The thin man glanced back at him. "I'm Wax Griffin. You'll be safe here."

The words should have been comforting. Instead, they landed like questions. *Safe from what? Safe for how long?*

The corridor opened onto a wider passage lined with rooms fronted by large glass windows. Through them, Jayk glimpsed workbenches covered in half-assembled machines, screens flickering with data he couldn't read, people bent over tasks that seemed both urgent and futile. This had been a research facility once; something military or scientific, built to last, then abandoned when whatever it was meant to research had become irrelevant. Now it was a sanctuary. A hiding place. A tomb with a heartbeat.

Then he saw it.

Through one of the windows, something looked back at him.

It sat perched on the edge of a table; a figure made of wire and fiber, carbon filament where muscle should be, fiber-optic lines tracing paths where veins might run. Its face was artificial, smooth as a mannequin's, but its eyes... its eyes were present. Aware. Two men worked around it: one studying a screen, the other making adjustments to its frame.

The machine tried to rise. Failed. Tried again.

Then it waved at him.

Jayk stopped walking. The gesture was so human; tentative, almost shy... that for a moment he forgot what he was looking at. The machine's eyes studied him with something that might have been curiosity. Might have been recognition. Might have been nothing at all, just light reflecting off glass and metal in patterns his brain interpreted as intention.

It attempted a smile.

Jayk raised his hand to the window and smiled back.

Wax appeared beside him, watching the robot with an expression that held equal parts pride and grief.

"We've been trying to reverse engineer them for years," he said quietly. "The robots. But power is scarce, and the science is... beyond us, mostly." He shook his head. "If we could get them working, really working, we'd have an army that doesn't bleed. Carbon fiber frames, three times stronger than steel at a fifth the weight. They could fight the Sky Reapers without us losing a single human life."

"Sky Reapers?"

"What we call them. The invaders." Wax's mouth twisted. "Grim reapers from the sky. Seemed fitting."

The robot had settled back onto the table, its brief animation exhausted. But its eyes still tracked Jayk through the glass, patient and strange.

"Come," Wax said. "I'll show you the rest."

The tour wound through corridors and stairwells, past rooms that served purposes Jayk could only guess at. Wax narrated as they walked; not boasting, just informing, laying out the facts of survival in a voice that had long since made peace with them.

"Generator keeps the lights on, though we ration carefully. Algae farm provides most of our nutrition. Water filtration, air recycling... the facility was designed to be self-sustaining." He paused at an intersection, choosing a direction. "We come and go under cover of darkness. The Sky Reapers haven't found us yet. We take great pains to keep it that way."

"So you hide," Jayk said.

Wax glanced at him. "We survive."

"Is there a difference?"

The thin man considered this. "I suppose that depends on what you're surviving for."

They emerged into a larger room where a group sat around a table covered in playing cards. Jayk recognized Dade immediately; the wiry one with the sharp tongue. Oso sat beside him, studying his hand with the concentration of a man defusing a bomb. Three women rounded out the game: Anya, Iri, and Zara, their faces carrying the same weathered patience as everyone else in this place.

"Everyone," Wax announced, "this is Jayk."

"We met," Dade said without looking up. He examined his cards, then finally raised his eyes. "So, you gonna pull up a chair and watch me bust these folks' heads, or just stand there looming?"

"You're only winning because these cards are marked," Oso muttered.

"'Course they're marked. How else am I supposed to tell which ones are the aces?" Dade grinned. "But I don't need marked cards to beat the likes of you. You missed the two pair on the turn and nearly cried out loud."

Jayk shook his head. "I don't play poker."

"This ain't poker. It's Texas Hold'em." Dade dealt another card to the center of the table. "Where it differs from poker is that it simulates real life. Perfect blend of skill and luck. A thinking man's game." He looked up. "So, you gonna stick around?"

"I was thinking about it." Jayk glanced at Wax. "As long as I'm welcome."

"Stay out of my way and we'll get along fine." Dade studied his cards again. "You're welcome." A pause. "I still don't trust you."

"I'll remember that."

"Why couldn't we have found a bambi?" Dade complained to no one in particular. "Could use a few more of those around here."

Anya threw a card at his head.

The meal was green and tasteless and exactly what Jayk needed.

He sat across from Wax at a steel table in what passed for a mess hall, shoveling algae paste into his mouth with the urgency of a man who had learned never to assume there would be more. The texture was like congealed swamp water. The nutrition was sufficient. That was all that mattered anymore.

Between bites, they talked.

"Were your parents nationalists?" Wax asked. The question was casual, but his eyes were not.

Jayk didn't look up. "No."

"I only ask because some of the survivors here were. The last of Sovran's people." Wax let the information hang in the air. "There are spies among us, Jayk. Always. We remain constantly on guard. When Po and the others found you, they thought you might be one of them."

"I'm not."

"I believe you. You stood your ground. Showed strength and courage. And when you had the chance to take a human life, you refused." Wax leaned back. "That's admirable. That's rare."

Jayk scraped the last of the paste from his container. "I grew up in the wastes. My family wasn't political. We were just... trying to survive."

"Like us."

"No." Jayk set down the container and met Wax's eyes. "Not like you."

Something flickered in the thin man's expression. Interest, perhaps. Or recognition.

"Tell me."

Jayk was quiet for a moment, gathering words that had been buried for years.

"I was twelve when they found us. Maybe thirteen." The memory surfaced like something dredged from dark water. "I remember the sound of their footsteps; heavy, crushing the ground beneath them. The screaming from inside the museum. That's where we lived, my parents and the other families. We thought we were hidden."

He stopped. Swallowed.

"They took everyone. Their weapons crackling with lightning, set everything they touched on fire. My parents... they didn't stand a chance. None of them did."

The fluorescent lights hummed overhead. Somewhere in the facility, a machine cycled on and off.

"That was the day I decided I was done hiding." Jayk's voice was steady now, hardened by repetition into something that could bear its own weight. "I swore I would hunt them. Every last one. I'll keep hunting them until I take my last breath."

Wax studied him for a long moment.

"I admire that," he said finally.

"Admiration doesn't win wars."

"No." Wax smiled faintly. "But people do. The right people, in the right place, at the right time." He stood. "We've had more Sky Reaper activity lately. The new garden is a hundred miles out and growing closer. If they find us, we'll have to move. Again."

"Or you could stop running."

The words hung between them.

"Po is one of the strongest men I know," Wax said slowly. "From what I heard, you overpowered him without breaking a sweat."

"He's strong. I'm trained."

"Then train us." Wax's eyes were steady. "You said you don't have formal military strategy, but you've read books. You're good with a blade. You could teach these people to fight."

"To fight," Jayk repeated. "Not just to survive."

"Is there a difference?"

The question came back to him like a hymn he'd heard before, the melody familiar even when the words had changed.

"I think that's the point," Jayk said.

Wax stood and extended his hand.

"Welcome to Gibraltar, Jayk. Let's see if we can hold it this time."

Jayk took his hand and gripped it firmly.
