The building hummed with the rhythm of over a thousand men and women going about their daily routines. Training. Eating. Drinking. Living out their lives in the belly of the earth, preparing to go to war. The noise rose through the corridors like a low chorus, the percussion of boots on concrete, the clatter of trays, the murmur of voices that never quite resolved into words. With so much sound, silence was hard to come by.

John sat on his bunk and looked around at the spartan furnishings. A table. A chair. A broken mirror that split his reflection into two men who didn't quite align. A fluorescent bulb that flickered just enough to remind you it could die at any moment. Patchwork blankets. A foam pillow gone flat with use.

He gripped the cold steel of the underside of the rack and stared at his pant legs.

Somewhere down the hall, a door slammed. Laughter. Then nothing.

He was trying to justify his memories. Far too much time had passed, it seemed, since he was a soldier. Was he even the same person? He didn't feel the same, and yet he was. He had to be. Tomorrow he would lead a group of men and women into unknown territory, and he needed to reconcile the man he remembered being with the man sitting on this bunk in this bunker with his hands gripping cold steel.

His hand went under his shirt and fingered where the gunshot wound should have been.

The skin was smooth.

He didn't notice. Not yet. His fingers moved across the spot the way they always had, tracing the topography of a scar that existed only in his memory, and then something else rose in him, sudden and violent, a memory he had nearly forgotten.

"Sergeant D'Arby, I'm glad you chose to volunteer for this program. It will help future generations. You will be remembered as a hero."

Diodes connected to his head and body ran the length of the room to a machine. He looked like a deranged marionette, laid out on a gurney, wires trailing from his temples and his chest and the soft hollows behind his ears. The room smelled of antiseptic and something else, something metallic and wrong, like pennies held too long in a sweating palm.

"Just lie still and let the machine do its work."

His body began to seize.

A dull whistling bored into his skull, and with it came shocks of electricity that pulsed through the bone in staccato bursts. All of his memories were being siphoned from his head, replaying in no particular order and without any help from him.

They were being extracted.

The images flashed absent his control. His mother's hands kneading dough. The smell of creek water in July. His uncle's laughter, deep and round as a drum. A girl whose name he couldn't remember kissing him behind the bleachers in tenth grade. All of the wonderful memories from his childhood, things he had forgotten, things he didn't want to remember... all funneling out of him like water through a cracked vessel.

It went on for several minutes.

Five monitors kept vigil over his body's functions: heart rate, blood pressure, brain waves. A three-dimensional image of his brain rotated on one screen, the white shadows of the fMRI filling with color as the machine whirred, mapping region after region. He assumed they were charting him for future generations to study. He assumed wrong.

Nearing completion, his body began to spasm involuntarily. His face contorted into grizzly caricatures, expressions no human face should make, his arms and legs seizing and releasing in a tempo he could not control. The friendly voice was there again, steady and warm, a hymn of reassurance sung over his convulsing body:

"Everything is going to be fine, there's no need to panic... what you're experiencing is normal and nothing to be afraid of. It's all regular occurrences as the machine performs its scan. You're doing very well. Only a few minutes more."

His eyes darted in saccades. He swallowed huge gulps of air that pressed down on his diaphragm like fists. It was suffocating, and he began to fight it, thrashing against the restraints, certain that his lungs would seize next, that the last breath he'd taken would be the last one he'd ever take.

His blood pressure spiked. His heart hammered against his ribs in a wild, syncopated beat.

He heard himself cry out in a voice that seemed foreign to him.

Somewhere else in the room, a different voice whispered something he couldn't quite make out.

Tears rolled down his cheeks as a great agony consumed him, every nerve ending singing at a frequency no body was built to sustain. He thought he was going to die.

Instead, he began to giggle.

Then a great roar of laughter as joy flooded his head, pure and inexplicable and enormous, as if the last thing the machine took was the barrier between pain and bliss. Every little measly thing that was him was ripped out of his body. Every facet of his being, stripped away.

And as quickly as it began, it was over.

He gripped the underside of the bunk and attempted to stand.

His legs felt wrong. His hands felt wrong. The fluorescent light buzzed overhead like a trapped insect, and the room swayed, and the broken mirror showed him two halves of a face he wasn't sure belonged to him.

He grabbed the side of his head and tried to shake the thoughts away, but they wouldn't go. They rolled around inside his skull, bouncing in and out of consciousness like echoes in a canyon.

What happened to me? Why am I only now remembering this? Why was I in that place, and who had taken me there?

Most importantly... what happened to me after?

They say that people who have experienced trauma often lose their memory of the events leading up to the incident. It could be seconds. It could be minutes. Depending on the severity, those memories may return with time, or not at all. His were returning now, and with terrible intensity.

Has something happened to me?

He needed answers. And the only thing in this bunker that might have them was the doctor.

John stood still for a long moment, his breath steadying.

He was there. I recognized his voice. He was there then, and he's here now.

He made his way down the corridor to the medical facility. The fluorescent tubes overhead cast everything in the same sallow light, and the walls were close, and the air smelled of recycled breath and iodine. He found ROGER standing at a workstation, bent over something mechanical, his movements precise and unhurried.

In his memory, John had heard that voice clearly. That same matter-of-fact tone. Both comforting and reassuring and, he now realized, the last thing he'd heard before the world went dark.

"I remember you."

ROGER turned and looked at him. The flat lavender lenses of his eyes stared back, unblinking, reflecting John's face in miniature.

"You performed some kind of experiment on me. What was it?" John's voice was harder than he intended. "I can't remember. I need to know."

ROGER regarded him for a moment. When he spoke, it was without hesitation, without apology, without anything resembling surprise.

"I was designed by the government under a defense contract code-named Operation Phoenix. We processed thousands of people just like you."

John felt the floor shift beneath him. Not physically. Something deeper.

"The exams focused on innate abilities and individual characteristics that would prove useful to the survival of humankind in the event of a disaster. Your bodies would die, however, your memories would be kept intact, as well as samples of your DNA tissue for future use."

"Future use," John repeated. The words tasted like ash.

ROGER continued, undeterred. "Until such time as the planet returned to suitable conditions favorable for the human species to thrive."

John shook his head slowly. "What are you telling me?"

"The Protogaia Project, in conjunction with defense contractors, built a vast interlocking network of cloning chambers where your body and the bodies of others would literally be grown and then birthed with the memories of your previous life downloaded via biomagnetic nanobots."

The room was too small. The light was too bright. John felt his hands trembling and shoved them into his pockets.

"Wait a minute." His voice came out wrong, too thin, too high. "What you're saying is that I volunteered for this experiment and it failed?"

ROGER's lavender eyes held him. "John, you were a part of that project. You were, for all intents, born inside that facility."

A pause. The fluorescent tube above them buzzed and flickered.

"And the memories you have now are those of a long since dead person."

The words landed like stones dropped into still water. John felt the ripples moving through him, concentric, widening.

"The original John D'Arby, the double nought whose memories you now possess, died over two hundred years ago."

He shook his head. He thought he was going to be sick. The floor tilted, and the walls leaned in, and everything he had ever known about himself, every certainty, every anchoring point, began to slide.

"No." He looked away from ROGER. The android's calmness was unnerving, the voice of a man explaining weather to someone standing in a hurricane. "No, that makes no sense."

ROGER stood undeterred. "I know this is hard for you to accept, but you must. You and Sarah are both clones of the original people who volunteered for the project."

John was flooded with memories. His childhood. His family. The reservation. His mother singing in the kitchen while rain drummed on the tin roof. His uncle showing him how to tie a fly, the line glinting silver in the afternoon sun. Friends whose faces he could still see, whose voices he could still hear, people he had loved with every fiber of a body that, apparently, was not his.

"But I remember," he said. His voice cracked. "I remember everything."

"Of course you do, John. Because I put them there." ROGER's voice softened, as much as a synthetic voice could soften. "But while those memories are not your own, they belong to the man you once were."

His mind raced. The room was spinning and he was standing still and both things were true at once.

"This is impossible. I am me. I know who I am."

ROGER stood motionless. "John, you only know who you are because the memories are there. But you are not John D'Arby. You share the same DNA, same blood type, same allergies. But you are a different person." A beat. "You are a clone."

John's hand went to his side. The old reflex, the gesture he'd made ten thousand times, fingers reaching for the ridged scar tissue of the bullet wound he'd carried for years. The proof of who he was. The mark on his body that said: this happened to me. I survived this. I am real.

"But that can't be. That's impossible." He was grasping now, reaching for anything solid. "Here, I can prove it. I have a bullet wound, here on my side..."

He pulled up his shirt.

"John, there is nothing there. Your skin is smooth. See for yourself."

He looked down.

The wound wasn't there.

He stared at the place where it should have been, stared longways at the smooth, unmarked skin, and the absence was louder than anything ROGER had said. The scar was gone. The story it told was gone. The proof of his life was gone, and in its place was nothing. Just skin. New skin. Skin that had never been punctured, never healed, never bore witness to anything at all.

"What have you done to me?!" He shouted.

"Calm down. I only did what you asked me to do. Everything was in accordance with your decision. While of course it may not have been you specifically, it was the former you." ROGER looked at him, and in those lavender lenses there was something that resembled compassion. "You have been offered a great opportunity, John. A chance to live out the rest of your life. Soon after the project began, your donor body died. You're lucky to be alive. You may not be the original John D'Arby, but pieces of him, perhaps the greatest pieces, still live inside of you."

He pointed to John's heart.

John lingered there, dumb for a moment. The fluorescent light buzzed. Somewhere down the corridor, someone laughed, and the sound was so ordinary it felt obscene.

"But you put them there," he said quietly. "These memories?"

"I did." ROGER smiled. "They're as much a part of you now as your kidneys, or your spleen."

John took it all in. All the memories of the kids on the reservation, his uncle teaching him how to fish, his mother who they called Sweet Medicine, who taught him how to heal. They were his, but they weren't. They were a song he'd memorized so thoroughly he'd forgotten he hadn't written it.

The only way he could reconcile what had happened to him was to either do right by the memory of the first John, or be a different man entirely.

But the melody would always be with him.

He decided he needed to talk to Sarah.

John had made his way to Sarah's dorm, but it was empty. He went searching and found her in the private training facility they had set up for her: a section of the bunker partitioned off from the rest, accessible only to those with clearance. He could hear the hum of the weapon before he saw her, a low vibration that resonated in his molars.

"Sarah," he called out. "We haven't been properly introduced. I'm John."

She had been working out and was wearing a tank top, her left arm uncovered for the first time in his presence. She turned toward him and brushed the hair out of her eyes.

He stopped.

She felt the stop. The hitch in his stride, the way his gaze snagged on her left side and held there. She knew that pause. She had catalogued it over two months of interactions with medics and technicians and the rare guard who wandered too close to her restricted section: the sharp intake of breath, the half-second freeze while the brain tried to reconcile what the eyes were reporting. She had learned to brace for it the way a cellist braces for a difficult passage, every muscle prepared, face composed, the vulnerability held deep in the chest where it could not show.

She watched him. Watched his eyes track the seam where her skin ended and the weapon began. Waited for the verdict. Horror, usually. Sometimes fascination, which was worse, because fascination made her a specimen. Sometimes pity, which was worst of all.

His face did something she hadn't expected. It opened.

The weapon began where her arm ended. Alien metal fused to human tissue, dark and luminous and alive in a way that metal should not be. The conducting sphere pulsed with a faint charge, the barrel extending from her forearm like something that had grown there, organic and mechanical at once. He had heard the rumors. He had been told.

But seeing it was something else entirely.

"They said you had a secret weapon. Actually, they said you were a secret weapon." He let out a slow breath. "But this is... impressive."

Something loosened in her ribcage. Not much. A quarter turn of a tension she carried every waking moment.

"That's encouraging, I suppose." She lifted it, pointing the muzzle toward the ceiling to give him a full view. "They've been keeping me hidden for fear of what people would think. At least until I've learned how to use it properly."

She raised it slowly, deliberately, the way you might extend a hand to someone who could flinch. This was the most alien part of her, the thing that made her something other than human to everyone who saw it, and she was holding it up for a stranger to examine. An act of trust so small it barely registered, and so large it made her pulse knock against the base of her throat.

"Wow." He smiled, looking it up and down in utter disbelief.

"So, something tells me you didn't just come in here to ogle the secret weapon, now did you?"

"Well, not entirely, but I never had an excuse before... and I've been a little busy." He flashed a charming smile. "You were in the cloning facility too, right? That's what ROGER said. That we're both clones."

"Yeah, that's right." She picked up a towel and started wiping the sweat from her brow.

"Well, what do you think about that?"

There it was. The question she'd asked herself for weeks in this sealed-off corner of the bunker, turning it over and over the way ROGER turned circuitry in his careful hands. She had thrashed through the same vertigo this man was standing in now. The dizziness of it. The way the ground falls away when you realize every memory, every formative moment, every melody your grandfather hummed while he called you Birdie, might belong to someone who has been dead for centuries.

She could see it on his face. The same unmoored look she'd worn two months ago, staring at the ceiling of her dorm, trying to remember if the green roads and lush trees and safe water she carried in her head were hers or just recordings played back into borrowed tissue.

"I try not to think about it, if you want to know the truth." She draped the towel over her shoulder. "At first it was difficult to accept. But I had to make a choice. I decided, at the end of the day they're just memories. Who I am moving forward is what matters most."

The words came out steady, and she meant them. But they were not easy words. They had been beaten into shape over sleepless nights and mornings where she'd had to convince herself to get out of bed, forged in the same fire she could see licking at the edges of his composure. She had earned that sentence the way a scar is earned. You don't get to say who I am moving forward until you've stood in the wreckage of who you thought you were and chosen to walk out.

She spoke with a self-possessed quality about her that was disarming. There was a steadiness in her voice that John recognized as the sound of someone who had already walked through the fire he was just entering.

John was quiet for a long time. He wanted to say something wise, something that matched the calm certainty in her voice. But the words wouldn't come. The truth was he wasn't there yet. Not even close.

"I don't know how you got to that place," he said finally. "I'm still back at the part where nothing makes sense."

She studied him. Not with pity, which he would have hated, but with the patience of someone who remembered what the first day felt like. She remembered it precisely. The nausea. The sense that her own hands didn't belong to her. The way she'd caught herself humming a song she'd loved since childhood and then stopped, struck by the thought: Was it me who loved it? Or her?

She'd found her answer eventually. It didn't matter. She was humming it now. That was enough.

"You'll get there," she said. "It doesn't happen all at once."

He nodded. Didn't quite believe her. But it was enough to keep him on his feet.

"Thanks, Sarah." It came out rougher than he meant it to. He cleared his throat. "I should get back."

"Anytime." She smiled at him.

"You know, when you're ready you can come out of hiding, and come work out with us."

"Really? So, what are you guys doing today?" She asked, a sly edge in her voice.

"Pushups, dips, pullups, some burpees, crunches, suicides and crab walkers."

"Wow! I got tired just listening to that. You guys have fun."

"You're not going to join us?"

"No, I think I'll pass. Besides, the only muscle I need to work out is this one." She winked as she raised the cannon and ignited it, sending an electrostatic charge crackling around the barrel. The air shimmered. The hum climbed in pitch, a note rising toward some resolution only she could hear.

This was not a demonstration. Not a performance. This was the experiencing self, alive and present in the body she had now, the body that was neither the original Sarah Greene's nor anyone else's. The charge sang through the conducting sphere and up into her shoulder and she felt it the way a guitarist feels the vibration travel from the strings through the body of the instrument into the chest. This was who she was. Not the remembering self, cataloguing someone else's life. This self. Here. Now. The hum in her bones. The light at her fingertips.

John watched the charge dance along the barrel's surface, blue-white arcs leaping between contact points, and realized he had met his match. She didn't just know how to use the weapon. She was proficient with it. Thoroughly, casually proficient, the way a pianist is proficient when they play without looking at their hands. It was as much a part of her as she was of it.

And if he intended to keep up with her, his soldiers would need better weapons. As a former gunnery sergeant, it was his duty to acquire them.

He headed back to his ready room to prepare.

John met with Wax and Po later to explain the situation.

"New plan, gentlemen." He stood at the head of Wax's ready room table, a thick canvas tarp bundled under his arm. "I was a gunnery sergeant in the Marine Corps, which meant that I was in charge of weapons, parts, and ammunition the company needed."

He unrolled the tarp and let its contents fall heavily onto the table.

They landed with a series of thuds: a handful of crowbars, a bat with nails hammered through it, a few rusty blades, and a sledgehammer. The sound they made was dull and graceless. No ring of good steel. No resonance. Just dead weight on wood.

"These are not weapons." John looked between them. "These are tools, and not even good tools. One of them is considered sporting goods."

He let that sit for a beat.

"This isn't a cobra in your backyard that you're gonna cut the head off of with your spade. These are superiorly intelligent life forms from another planet with weapons we can't even begin to fathom. They have us beat in numbers. They have us beat in strength. They have us beat in technology. We don't stand a chance."

He looked to Wax, imploring.

"Now, I know you guys took out a small group of them when you had the jump, but you almost lost as many lives as you rescued. You can't keep that up. They'll be prepared next time. We need better weapons or we will lose this war."

The room was quiet. The fluorescent tubes hummed their single, tireless note.

Then ROGER walked in.

"We've got a problem in medical as well. Without life-saving medicines like antibiotics and painkillers, we won't be able to survive another battle, much less win a war. I can manufacture most things, but it will take time. We will need to accumulate the rest from somewhere else."

Wax nodded soundlessly. He knew he couldn't argue. The evidence was on the table in front of him: a collection of blunt objects that wouldn't last five minutes against an enemy that had crossed the stars.

"Gather the others," he said. "Perhaps we can all come up with a solution together."

Rensa, Dade, and Ideo entered the ready room and found John, Jayk, Sarah, Po, and ROGER awaiting them with Wax. He briefed the newcomers on the medical situation, then turned to John to finish laying out the weapons problem.

"If we intend to attack more gardens, it will only be a matter of time before the Sky Reapers begin fortifying their installations. We will need better weapons to launch a successful offensive. We'll need to put off our recon missions until then." John leaned back against the wall and crossed his arms. The nail-bat sat on the table between them all like a punchline nobody laughed at.

"Suggestions?" Wax invited.

Silence filled the room. Everyone stared at one another, waiting for someone else to speak first. The quiet stretched until it became its own kind of pressure.

Until, finally, Ideo spoke up.

"Well, there's one place that I know of." She looked around the room, meeting each pair of eyes in turn. "We could get both weapons and medical supplies there. But it's a little ways out, and not an easy trip." She paused. "My home. The Free City of Solos."

"Never heard of it," Dade responded.

"That's what I said!" Jayk added.

"Well, it exists. And it exists because of its secrecy." Ideo cut Dade and Jayk a sideways glare. "It's a trading post used by bounty hunters and scavengers. And the only people who know how to find it... are the people who know how to find it." She held the glare a beat longer. "You could literally be right on top of it and not have any idea it was there."

"Well, let's put it to a vote," Wax said. "Anyone in favor of going to Solos to get the weapons and supplies we need?"

The vote was almost unanimous. Only one dissenting voice.

"It's too great a risk." Po leaned forward, his jaw set. "We can use the metal from the airstrip to forge weapons, and ROGER said himself that he has access to the facility where Sarah was cloned. It has medical supplies. This just seems too dangerous to me."

"We'd never be able to forge enough weapons. We don't have the resources," John said.

"And that place is a last resort," Sarah added. "We don't want to draw attention to it unnecessarily. The fate of the Earth resides in that place."

A beat of silence.

"Sounds good to me." Dade nodded. "We can catch two birds with one song."

Sarah looked to Rensa, who simply shook her head.

"What?" Dade gawped at them. "Birds like songs."

"Then it's settled," Wax said, not giving the moment time to breathe. "How far away is Solos, Ideo?"

"It's about a week's travel east of here through the worst part of the wastelands." She straightened. "We should only take what we need. We can resupply once we get there."

John looked at the crowbars on the table. Looked at the nail-bat. Looked at the sledgehammer. Then he looked up.

"Ok. So. Impossible trek. Through the worst of the wastes. To an invisible city. Full of raiders and scavengers." He paused. "When do we leave?"

Wax put the next question plainly. "Volunteers?"

It would be a dangerous mission. But without medicine and real weapons, the odds would be stacked against them in every engagement to come. This was a temporary solution; ultimately they would need to become self-sufficient. But for now, it was what they had.

Everyone in the room volunteered. Including Po.

"Though, I want it to go on the record that I think this is a bad idea," Po said.

"Noted." Wax looked back to the group. "Logistically, how do you plan to carry everything? And what do you plan to barter with?"

"Well, I was thinking about that." Ideo stepped forward. She was the only one in the room who had ever set foot in the underground city, and she knew what its people wanted. "We have tech... and knowledge. ROGER has been repairing and modifying the robots here, and John has a wealth of information that could be a valuable commodity to the right person." She smiled. "And I think I have just the right person in mind. His name is Gunther and he runs a fighting pit. He's always looking for any advantage he can give his fighters."

She looked at John and nodded.

"I'm sure there's a few tricks I could give them to enhance their fighting skills," John said.

"As far as carrying things, we can use one of the trailers from the airfield," Jayk suggested. "ROGER could pull it."

"Consider it pulled," ROGER chimed in.

"If there isn't anything else," Wax said, "ROGER can go ahead out to the boneyard and get a trailer. John, you help Jayk and Ideo load up the robots and any other useful tech, while Rensa, Dade, and Sarah gather and pack supplies."

The meeting broke apart, and the bunker filled again with the sound of purpose. Boots on concrete. Voices carrying orders down corridors. The rhythm of preparation, steady and deliberate, like the tuning of instruments before the overture begins.

ROGER left the bunker and headed toward the airfield graveyard to retrieve a trailer. It would take him a day to get there and a day to return. In the meantime, the rest could prepare the gear and pack everything they would take with them on their journey.

Ideo was in her dorm packing knives, hatchets, and anything else of value when Sarah walked in.

Ideo stopped. She was holding a small eight-inch shuriken, turning it in the light, and her eyes had gone somewhere far away. She held it the way her father had taught her, thumb resting on the flat of the blade, fingers curled beneath, the weight balanced at the center point. She didn't think about the grip. Her hands simply knew. The Goku name had passed down through generations from the Sengoku era, and Hong had put a blade in her hand before she could read, had shown her how to feel the balance point the way other fathers taught their daughters to hold a pen. The knowledge lived in her tendons now, deeper than thought.

"This is going to be a pilgrimage of sorts for me." She ran her thumb along the flat of the blade. "I haven't been home in almost seven years. Haven't spoken to my parents in even longer." She placed the shuriken carefully into a leather sheath. "I didn't leave on the best of terms."

Seven years. She didn't know if Hong still kept the training room in the back of the house, the mats worn smooth at the center where she used to drill footwork until her calves burned. She didn't know if Sun Yi still opened The Red Fin each morning, didn't know if the restaurant even stood anymore. She didn't know if either of them were alive. The pilgrimage wasn't just going home. It was going to find out.

"I'm sure that whatever happened, you've been forgiven by now."

"Then you don't know my father." Ideo pulled another blade from the pile and tested its edge against the pad of her thumb, the way Hong had shown her, reading the sharpness by feel rather than sight. A shadow warrior tests in silence, he used to say. Your eyes can be deceived. Your skin cannot. "He's a hard man. Stern and decisive. Once he makes up his mind, he doesn't change it." She held the dagger in her hand, turning it end over end with practiced ease, the steel catching the light in quick flashes. The flip was unconscious, a rhythm drilled into her at twelve, thirteen, fourteen, hours in the back room while Sun Yi's voice carried from the kitchen, calling her to come help serve the evening rush. She'd chosen the blade over the ladle every time. "He used to call me his little Kogatana. It's a small blade that tucks into a special fitting on the side of a scabbard."

"What did you two fight about that made him so upset?"

"You see this blade?" Ideo held it up. "I took it from a box in his room when I was fourteen or fifteen. He had been teaching me how to fight, to defend myself if I ever got into trouble. Maybe he wanted a boy, I don't know. But I took the knife and of course I cut myself stabbing at boxes." A ghost of a smile. "My mom was furious with him that night. She said it was his fault. Blamed him for teaching me. She's very traditional. Said if he hadn't been training me I never would have taken the knife. It wasn't a bad cut, you can barely see the scar." She turned her wrist, showing the thin white line. "But that didn't matter. He stopped training me from that day forward. He decided he wanted me to marry. To be a good wife, like my mother. I wanted something else."

She could still see Sun Yi standing in the kitchen doorway that night, backlit by the lanterns of The Red Fin, smelling of ginger and sesame oil. The way her mother's voice had gone quiet and flat, which was worse than shouting. Sun Yi never shouted. She just got still, the way water gets still before it freezes. You see what happens? she had said to Hong. You see? As if the thin line of blood on Ideo's wrist were proof of everything she'd been warning him about since Ideo could walk.

She stuffed the weapons into her bag, each one placed with the deliberate care of someone who understood what every blade was for. The short tanto beside the longer wakizashi. The shuriken in their leather roll. The hatchets wrapped in cloth so the edges wouldn't nick each other. Every item nested and secured, the way Hong had taught her to pack a kit: nothing loose, nothing wasted, every piece in its right place. Her mother had wanted those hands arranging bowls and folding napkins at The Red Fin. Her father had trained them for this.

"He wanted me to stay in Solos. I guess he figured if I married, I'd be protected."

Sarah listened and felt something tighten in her own chest. She knew what it was to be caught between the person you were supposed to become and the person you already were. She'd been a cellist in another life, or in another woman's life, and now she was a weapon. Ideo had been a daughter, and she'd chosen to be a warrior. The distance between those two things was the same shape in both of them.

"Fathers can be tough," Sarah said gently. "But that doesn't mean they don't love you. They just want to keep you safe. Even if that comes across as controlling, sometimes."

"I just want him to be proud of me." Ideo's voice dropped to something small and raw. "All my life that's all I've ever wanted."

She sighed heavily. Then let out a small chuckle, the kind that comes when you're trying to keep something larger at bay.

"I'm so foolish." She wiped a single tear from her eye with the heel of her palm.

The heel of her palm. Not her fingertips, not the back of her hand. The thick, callused part that gripped hilts and crowbars and climbing ropes. She pressed it hard against her eye and held it there for a breath, then two, jaw tight, as if she could push the feeling back inside by force. The room was quiet except for the sound of her breathing, and Sarah did not fill the silence. She let it hold.

"No. No, you're not. You're one of the strongest people I know."

"That's not saying much." Ideo giggled, the sound wet and bright at the same time. "You don't know that many people."

They both laughed at that. The sound filled the small room and pushed the sadness back, not away, but back, to a distance where it could be carried.

"I guess we'll find out soon enough," Ideo said, putting the shuriken in the leather sheath on her hip.

"Your father will be proud of you. I'm sure of it!" Sarah took Ideo's hand in hers and held it.

Jayk appeared in the doorway.

"Am I interrupting anything? I've been looking all over for you, Sarah."

"Well, you've found me. What's up?"

"I just wondered if you needed any help packing, or anything like that."

"Nope. Fortunately for me I pack light. Everything I need is already attached." She patted the electro cannon with her right hand. "But I appreciate the offer."

She smiled at Ideo and nodded. Then, without looking back to Jayk, she said, "Let's go see if the others need any help."

Sarah made her way to the medical unit where she found ROGER tinkering with something at his workstation, his back to the door. Something metallic glinted in his hands before he shifted, blocking her view. The fluorescent tubes cast their flat, clinical light across the room, and the air smelled of antiseptic and soldering flux, a sharp mineral note that reminded her, distantly, of concert halls. Of rosin. Of the fine dust that collected on the bridge of a cello after hours of rehearsal.

"Rensa told me to come get you."

"Ok, I'll be there in just a moment." ROGER didn't turn around.

She stood in the doorway and watched everyone else prepare. Down the corridor, she could hear the others hauling crates, calling instructions to each other, the steady percussion of useful work. Rensa carrying supplies. Dade stacking rations. Ideo packing blades. Even Jayk, limping and half-mad, could carry a box on his shoulder and set it where it needed to go. Everyone had hands for the work. Two hands.

"You know, it's times like these I really wish I had my arm back." She leaned against the doorframe. "I feel so useless. I can't help with anything. I'm almost more of a burden."

The words came out flat, but beneath them was something vast and tangled. She had a weapon fused to her body that could level a wall, and she couldn't carry a crate of medical supplies. Couldn't fold a blanket. Couldn't button a coat, couldn't lace a boot, couldn't hold a cup of water without bracing it against her hip. The cannon made her powerful, and the power made her helpless, and the two things lived in the same limb, in the same absence. She didn't miss the arm. She missed the hand. The fingers. The ability to pick up a pen, to touch someone's face, to curl five fingers around a mug of something warm and feel the heat seep through. She missed the small, human gestures that connected people to each other, the ones nobody thought about until they were gone.

Sometimes, in the quiet hours, she felt it. A phantom ache in fingers that no longer existed, a tingling in the pads of a thumb and forefinger that would never again press down on a fret, never again draw a bow across strings. She had been a cellist. Her hands had been her instrument. She had played Ravel's Bolero with a philharmonic, had felt the low vibration of the C string travel through the wood and into her sternum, and those fingers, those precise and practiced and irreplaceable fingers, were gone. Replaced by a barrel and a conducting sphere and enough electromagnetic force to crack stone.

The grief and the power were the same thing. She could not separate them.

ROGER paused. His hands went still over whatever he held. He looked up, not at Sarah, but at the wall in front of him, and something passed through those lavender lenses. A calculation, perhaps. Or something closer to understanding.

Then he went back to what he was working on. He opened a drawer, slid something inside, and closed it. Quick. Not quite quick enough to be nothing. He turned to face her.

"I think when the time comes, you'll realize you're more valuable than you think."

He held her gaze a moment longer than necessary.

One of his trainees approached, a young woman with nervous hands and earnest eyes. "Any words of wisdom before you go?"

"Yes," ROGER replied, putting his hand on her shoulder. "Try not to kill anyone."

After Dr. Lemky's MagLev technology had been adopted across the world, all new vehicles were constructed to hover. No more wheels. No more tires. No more axles. Everything floated just inches above the ground, supported by skiffs when at rest. The roads had been ripped up. The parking lots dismantled. The world had been transformed, and then the world had ended, and the vehicles that remained sat half-buried in sand and rust, monuments to an ingenuity that hadn't been enough to save anyone.

Which meant the trailer ROGER had retrieved from the airfield boneyard had no wheels. It hovered when powered, and when it wasn't powered, it sat dead and heavy on the earth. Dragging it across the wastes to Solos would require brute, sustained, mechanical force.

A job ROGER alone was suited to perform.

Fortunately, he had spent his time in the bunker well, training a team of men and women in combat medicine, surgery, and the treatment of common illness. He would leave the bunker's inhabitants in their capable hands.

Rensa had been working on something quietly in her dorm since ROGER had left for the boneyard. Two days. She had spent two days alone in that room with a pile of scavenged leather, a length of heavy chain, salvaged buckles, and a curved needle she'd fashioned from a piece of wire. The same hands that swung a crowbar through bone, the same scarred knuckles that had cracked jaws and split lips and once, in the demimonde, bitten clean through a man's ear when he wouldn't take no for an answer... those hands had sat in her lap and threaded leather through chain links, stitch by careful stitch, pulling each one tight, testing it, pulling again.

She didn't know why she'd started it. That wasn't true. She knew. She just wouldn't have said it aloud if you'd held a blade to her throat. ROGER was going to drag a trailer across the wastes for a week. He needed something to distribute the weight across his frame, and nobody else had thought of it, and Rensa had looked at the problem and solved it the only way she knew how. Not with words. Never with words. With her hands.

She had tested every seam herself. Hooked the chain to her bunk's frame and pulled until her boots slid on the floor, checking for any stitch that might give. She checked twice. Three times. Because if she was going to make something, it would hold. It would be right.

When ROGER returned, she proudly presented it. Proudly, and also quickly, thrusting it forward before she could think too hard about what the gesture meant.

"Here. This should make your journey a little bit easier."

She held out a sleeveless harness she had fashioned from leather garments she'd found and sewn together, fitted with a length of heavy chain that could be attached to the trailer's hitch. The stitching was tight and even. The buckles sat flush. It was rough work, but it was good work, the work of someone who understood that the difference between something that holds and something that fails is the willingness to do it twice.

"Thank you, Rensa." ROGER put it on and examined the chain, turning to test the range of motion. "I will look a proper pack mule with this."

"Oh yeah, I guess you will. Ha!" She let out an awkward laugh. A flicker of something crossed her face, there and gone, the vulnerability of a person who has just handed someone proof that she cares, and now needs to get as far from that proof as possible.

He adjusted the straps and pulled at the seams. She had done skillful work. If it held, it would make the long haul to Solos considerably easier.

"Thank you, Rensa. You've really outdone yourself." He looked at her appraisingly. "We could use your skills at sewing in the medical unit."

"No thanks, Doc." Rensa grinned. It was a grin that showed teeth, sharp and devilish, green eyes bright as broken glass. The grin of someone who had learned to smile as a warning long before she'd learned to smile as a kindness. "I'm better at tearing them apart than patching them up."

They set to loading the trailer immediately.

Robot chassis. Salvaged parts. Circuit boards and servos, spare wiring, anything ROGER deemed tradeable. Packs of dried rations and water containers. Blankets. First aid supplies, what little they had. The trailer filled with the accumulated junk and treasure of a civilization reduced to barter, and they strapped it all down tight.

The rest they would carry on their backs.

Wax shook hands with Po and instructed him to take care of everyone, and of himself. They spoke quietly for a moment, two men who understood the weight of what was being asked, and what was being left behind.

"Don't worry," Po said. "I'll keep an eye on everyone."

The group assembled at the bunker's exit. The sun was low, throwing long shadows across the hardpan. ROGER stood in Rensa's harness, the chain running taut from his back to the trailer's hitch, his frame angled forward like a draft horse leaning into the yoke. The others carried packs and weapons and the particular silence of people who know they are walking toward something they cannot see.

"We'll be back before you know it!" Rensa called out. She turned to look back once more. "Hold down the fort for us!"

And with that, the group made their way across the wastes and toward the setting sun.
