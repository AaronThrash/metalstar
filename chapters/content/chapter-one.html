Chapter One: Dark Epoch

"In our obsession with antagonisms of the moment, we often forget how much unites all the members of humanity. Perhaps we need some outside, universal threat to make us recognize this common bond. I occasionally think how quickly our differences worldwide would vanish if we were facing an alien threat from outside this world." ~ Ronald Reagan
    Jayk navigated the barren wasteland like a passenger in a body that still knew how to walk, even if his mind had forgotten why. His lips were cracked and dry as the desert stretched before him, a litany of dust and ruin repeating toward every horizon. A single drop of rain struck his forehead, a cold communion, and he lifted his face to the purpling sky. Storm coming. It had not rained in weeks. His body remembered what his mind had stopped asking for: mercy from above. The land offered no reprieve. Broken structures rose from the earth in the distance like gravestones. Wind carried hard granules of sand through the atmosphere, scouring what remained of the old world. The destruction had been nearly complete. There was very little left of how things once were. As he drew closer to the city, the silence gave way to sound. Bent metal frames and twisted signs banged out their tormented beats against empty buildings; hollow rooms inhabited now only by warm weather. The air swirled between abandoned vehicles and along cracked sidewalks, playing out the soft mewls of a despondent tune. This was the city's vespers: a service for the dead, conducted by wind and rust, attended by no one. Twilight caught the few surfaces still intact and threw the dying light back at the sky. The storm swelled to a startling magnitude. Jayk continued his careful passage through the empty streets, eyes trained for enemies, feet finding the path as if by memory. He had walked through ruins before. He would walk through ruins again. The world was made of them now. The deluge came. The sky opened and rain poured down in sheets, a baptism he had not asked for but would not refuse. Jayk stood in the downpour and tilted his head toward the heavens as a smile crept across his face. He reached into his pack and withdrew a folded square of bright yellow plastic. As he blew air into it, the object expanded: an inflatable wading pool, banana-yellow, with the words "Lemonade Seahorse" printed along the inside in sky-blue and magenta. A forgotten children's cartoon from a bygone era... some tiny horse-headed aquatic creature that once made children laugh, that once meant summer afternoons and innocence and cold drinks sweating in the sun. Now it would collect rainwater. That was all. That was enough. He found an open structure and stepped inside as the storm began to play its own discordant melody. The walls were chipped where time and poor construction had weakened the plaster, revealing the metal bones beneath. Rusted bars probed through brick and mortar like the ribs of a cathedral left to rot. A portrait of destruction, preserved in time. A quick survey told him this place had once been a post office. Letters that were never delivered. Messages to people who had stopped waiting. He understood something about that. Jayk removed his scabbard and sword and placed them behind the counter, hidden from view; a precaution against anyone, or anything, that might challenge him in the night. Then he shrugged off his pack and set it on the ground as a pillow. He lay down on the cold floor, listening to the rain conduct its requiem against the roof, and let himself drift toward sleep. Even here, in this profane sanctuary, something like peace could be found... borrowed, brief, and fragile as breath. He closed his eyes as the darkness took him gently. --- It was at that exact same moment that Sarah was preparing to be born. ROGER stood before a cloning unit, examining the vital signs of a young man suspended in dark fluid. The name on the digital readout: John D'Arby. Muscular. Dark-haired. Square-featured. Heart rate steady. Fluid levels optimal. ROGER monitored, calibrated, recorded; the endless rhythm of his purpose. ROGER was a Robotic Genetic Engineer, designed to shepherd the preborn through their long gestation: tracking vitals, preparing neural downloads, guiding each clone from data to flesh. After birth, he would remain; teacher, caretaker, custodian, until they could walk the Earth again and make it theirs. This was the Protogaia Project. A failsafe. A final hope encoded in frozen cells and patient machines, an ark built against the day when meteor or war or catastrophe would silence the world above. Human and animal DNA catalogued, preserved, waiting for the climate to forgive. Waiting for ROGER to bring them back. He moved to Sarah's chamber and checked her readings. Soon now. Very soon. Sarah hung between sleeping and waking, suspended in warm opacity. Through the glass she could see almost nothing; just the faint geometry of the room beyond, shapes without meaning. The amniotic fluid pressed against her skin like an answer to a question she had not learned to ask. She floated in a silence so complete it felt like frequency zero, the flat line before signal. And if she could have named what she felt in that moment, that strange calm, that absence of self, she might have called it peace. A resonance with something larger. No boundaries, no separation. Just current flowing through current, continuous and whole. It did not last. The memories came like voltage. They crashed into her brain in a single devastating pulse: childhood, growth, experience, personality... decades of living compressed into one brutal download. Sarah Greene, the original, the donor, the double-nought, had been scanned and mapped and copied, her entire existence rendered into data and stored until this new brain was ready to receive it. Now the transfer was complete. Sarah 01 woke into a life she had never lived but would remember as her own. The peace shattered. The unity collapsed. She was suddenly someone, specific, bounded, alone. She opened her eyes. Elsewhere in the facility, something was wrong. The roots had come quietly, burrowing through reinforced walls, searching for minerals and moisture. They belonged to no earthly tree. Where they pierced the structure, they bled; a dark sap streaming down and pooling on the concrete floor. Unlike the gentle amber of oak or willow, this ichor was sour, corrosive, hungry. It dissolved metal. It ate through cable housings. It dripped onto the miles of wiring that connected two thousand sleeping souls and began to unmake them. ROGER registered the damage as it cascaded through his systems. Alerts multiplied. Containment breached. Temperature variance. Fluid contamination. The obsidian chambers began to wail; a chorus of alarms splitting the air, crashing against the soft symphony that had filled the room for centuries. Stravinsky's "Rite of Spring" fought and lost against the noise, its careful harmonics swallowed by acoustical panic. The chamber doors began to open. One by one, the glass enclosures whirred and shifted, spilling their precious fluid onto the floor. Clones not yet ready. Bodies not yet formed. The project was failing, and ROGER could calculate the loss but not prevent it. Sarah felt the change before she understood it. The liquid around her pressed harder, then gave way. The hatch unsealed. She was falling... The tubes tore from her body. The cables ripped from her skull. She hit the ground in a flood of fluid, and the pain was everything, and then there was nothing at all. --- Jayk woke to judgment. A woman crouched over him; scruffy, tousle-haired, her grimace poking out from beneath thick sandy features. She dangled a heavy chain in one hand. In the other, a claw hammer, gripped tight. Behind her stood a rugged man in a loose vest and dark hard-woven trousers. Beside him, another; roguish, wild-eyed, gaunt cheekbones over a wiry frame. Two more flanked them, making them five in all. None of them looked happy to see him. He sat up on his elbows. "Did I forget to pay the rent?" The woman crouched lower and waved the hammer's business end dangerously close to his eyes, back and forth, as if deciding which one to hollow out first. "Eeny... meeny... miney..." A heavy paw landed on her shoulder. The rugged man. Silent. She stopped. Jayk gathered himself and rose slowly. These people wanted a fight. He had been in his share, some won, some lost, but it always hurt the same way after. If a fight was what they wanted, he would give them one. But carefully. Carefully. One would assume that humans, sharing a common enemy, would not turn on each other. One would be wrong. It had never been the case. Not even when he was a boy. The museum. He had not thought of it in years, maybe longer. But now the memory surfaced unbidden: an enormous structure of stone and tile, vast interlocking rooms, a labyrinth of artifacts and relics from the old world. Before the Dark Epoch. Before everything ended. He had called that place home. He and the other children would spar through its halls, bounding from room to room, up and down staircases, between dioramas and display cases. It was there he learned to wield a sword. There he prepared to fight the enemy. Projectile weapons had died with the old world; no factories to mass-produce bullets, no refineries to make the powder that propelled them. The survivors fought with what remained: axes, bats, bricks, sticks, hammers, knives. The utilitarian devices of a forgotten civilization, repurposed as instruments of survival. A sharp pain cracked across his temple and the memory shattered. He was on the floor again, ears ringing, vision swimming. The rugged man had struck him hard. Jayk scrambled to his feet, thrust out a palm: stop. He needed time. Time to think. Time to get to his sword, still hidden behind the counter. "Wait," he muttered. The word escaped before his brain could catch it. "You wear the alb of a practitioner of Omaha," the rugged man growled. "Which means you are an enemy to us. Defend yourself like a man." He spat the last word like something foul. The alb. His cloak. They thought he was one of them, the followers of Sid Mantelis, that strange sect who believed their animal gods would save humanity from the usurpers. They must have been following him. Watching. Waiting to pass judgement. "No, listen to me..." "No. You will listen to me." The man's eyes flashed with the certainty of the righteous. "There is no reasoning with your kind." Before Jayk could respond, powerful hands seized him and hurled him over the counter. Exactly where he wanted to be. He scrambled, searching for his sword, but heard the woman's voice first. "Looking for this?" She held it by the hilt, letting it dangle heavy in her grip. Fear washed through him, cold and sudden. For a long moment, he did not know what to do. The rugged man vaulted the counter. They were face to face again. He lunged. Jayk's body answered before his mind could, dodging, pivoting, delivering a brutal blow to the man's ribs. The impact surprised them both. The man stumbled. He had not expected someone of Jayk's build to hit that hard. Jayk wiped blood from his lip. "I don't want to fight you. Our fight is with them." The man straightened and drew a blade from behind his back that bridged the gap between them. "You're right," he said. "You don't want to fight me." Something in Jayk shifted. The reluctance burned away. "Oh, you're a cocky one. Alright. Let's see what you've got." He beckoned the man forward. The blade sang through the air. Jayk twisted aside, barely, and felt the wind of it pass where his stomach had been. He yanked down a metal stand, grabbed a ceiling joist, and kicked hard into the man's chest. The man staggered but did not fall. Across the room, the others stood ready. If Jayk ran, they would kill him. He needed his sword back. He needed to think. The wiry one shouted: "We're wastin' time! Finish him, Po! Or I'll do it!" Po. Now the rugged man had a name. Jayk had dodged several attacks, but he could not bring himself to kill another human. Not if he did not have to. Then he saw it; the loose hang of Po's vest, open at the sides, and an idea formed. He dropped to his knees just as Po closed the distance. In one fluid motion, Jayk thrust his arm into the open sleeve of Po's vest and spun, sliding his back against Po's chest. His other arm found the second armhole. Now they were bound together; Jayk wearing Po's vest backward, Po's arms trapped. He grabbed Po's wrists and slammed his knife hand into the metal counter. Once. Twice. Three times. The blade clattered to the floor. Then Jayk snapped his head backward into Po's face. Again. Again. Blood sprayed from nose and mouth. He lunged back, drove Po into the wall, then lurched forward and flipped him over his shoulder, arms sliding free of the vest as he rose. Po crumpled to the ground, head hanging, arms slack. Defeated. Jayk snatched up the knife and pressed it under Po's throat, tilting back his head. He used Po's body as a shield, eyes sweeping the others. "Anyone else?" They lowered their weapons. Stood silent. Jayk released his grip. He opened his mouth to explain, once more, that he did not want to fight, that this was all a misunderstanding... And then the pain came again. Not from outside. From within. A splintering in his skull, white-hot and absolute, and the world tilted sideways and went dark. "Whoa! What happened to him?" The woman, Rensa, stared down at the crumpled body. "Who de hell knows?" The wiry one laughed. "Maybe he got knackered." He moved toward Po. "Hey, old man. You okay? You fought like arse." Po rubbed the back of his head and accepted the offered hand, pulling himself upright. "He's stronger than he looks." He studied the unconscious stranger for a long moment. "We should bring him with us. Let Wax have a talk with him." Rensa huffed, reaching for his arms. "You two grab his feet." They lifted him together, carrying him out of the ruined post office and into the storm-washed dark. Behind them, the abandoned letters kept their silence. --- The tough fibrous roots had burrowed deep, searching for minerals, for moisture, for anything the earth could offer. They belonged to no tree that had ever grown on this world. Where they breached the walls of the underground facility, they bled; a dark sap streaming down concrete and pooling on the floor. Unlike the gentle amber of oak or willow, this ichor was sour, hungry, wrong. It ate through metal. It dissolved cable housings. It dripped onto the miles of wiring that connected two thousand sleeping souls and began, quietly, to kill them. The birthing chambers failed in cascade. One after another, the obsidian cells unsealed too early, spilling fluid and unfinished bodies onto the cold floor. ROGER calculated the loss in real time. Eighty percent. Ninety. The project he had been built to protect was dying faster than he could count the dead. He could do nothing. He could only save who could still be saved. Sarah woke to sirens. The sound moved through her in plangent waves; thunderous and relentless. Her eyes were open but the world was noise without shape, input without parsing. She could not see. She could not hear clearly. Her senses were newborn and overwhelmed, still learning to translate raw data into meaning. A voice reached her through the chaos. Calm. Measured. Honest. "You have to leave this place. It is not safe for you to remain here." ROGER knelt beside her, his tone carrying no urgency but absolute certainty. "Whatever damaged the chambers may be dangerous to you. I know you cannot see or hear clearly yet, but you must come with me. I will lead you to the surface, then return for other survivors." She could not see his face. She could not see anything but light and blur and the vague suggestion of geometry. But his hand found hers, cold, precise, steady, and she let him pull her upright. They moved through darkness. Her feet found ground she could not see; her body trusted a guide she could not verify. Somewhere behind them, she thought she heard a voice call out... hello... but they moved too quickly, and the sound was lost. Steel doors groaned open. Hot air rushed against her face. ROGER stopped. "What is it?" she heard herself say. "What do you see?" All she could make out was brightness; yellow, searing, total. The sun she had never met, filling her vision like a wall. "Something is terribly wrong," he said quietly. His hand released hers. "Get down as low as you can," he whispered, "and move backward. Quickly." She dropped. She crawled. She tried to make sense of the shapes resolving in her vision; polygons of color, large forms at the edge of sight, there and then gone. Sounds reached her in fragments: grunting, snorting, the clang of chains, heavy footfalls. Her brain fought to connect the signals, to build a picture from static. Colors sharpened into objects. Objects into creatures. Something was close. The grunting was close. A smell hit her; rank, animal, wrong, and then something cold and hard pressed against her temple. She batted it away. Then lost consciousness.
