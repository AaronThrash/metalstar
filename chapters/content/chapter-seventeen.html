Po breathed in through his nose, held the air in his chest for three counts, and let it go through his teeth. The breath was a current, a warm river running through the center of him, and when it left his body it carried something with it. Fear. Doubt. Fatigue. All of it poured out with the exhale, leaving behind only the clarity he needed.

His opponent circled to the left. The man was quick, quicker than Po had anticipated, and light on his feet in a way that suggested formal training, someone who had learned to fight before the world ended, or been taught by someone who had. He had found most of Po's weaknesses early, the slight hitch in his right hip, the tendency to drop his guard after a combination, and had exploited them with the patience of a predator circling wounded prey. The crowd roared around them like something alive, a wall of sound that pressed against the ropes from all sides. Po had already won several exhibition bouts over the past days and had earned a fair amount of barter coin for his efforts. ROGER had returned to serve as his corner man, spending most of his time patching cuts and sizing up opponents. ROGER's unique capabilities gave him the ability to detect imperceptible changes in a fighter's stamina, endurance, and physical condition, the sort of advantage that might be considered unfair if it were not still up to Po to do the actual fighting.

He sat in his corner between rounds, sipping water and pressing a cold rag to his swollen lip. The crowd noise faded to a low, persistent hum. ROGER knelt beside him.

"He's got a brutal left jab," Po said.

"Concentrate on his right knee. It is vulnerable." ROGER's voice was precise and unhurried, each word placed with the care of a surgeon setting bone. "You are better on the mat than he is. Get him down, take control, and do not relent. Also, you have greater endurance than he does at this moment. His blood oxygen levels are dropping."

Po nodded. He closed his eyes and let the breath come again, in through the nose, held, released. His Kata lived in that breath, the ancient forms that had been practiced by ten thousand bodies before his own, each one adding something to the shape of the movement. He was not fighting this man. He was flowing around him. Water does not struggle against stone. It finds the path.

When the round resumed Po came out strong. A fortuitous kick caught the side of his opponent's head and sent him stumbling off balance, arms pinwheeling, and Po followed it with two sharp kicks to the body before the man could set his feet. His opponent attempted a sweep, but this time Po was ready and caught his arm, trying for a scissor throw that was blocked at the last moment. No matter. A quick left hook sent the man tumbling again, a tooth flying in a red arc, and the crowd noise surged like a wave breaking against rock. Po moved in. With one hand he gripped the back of his opponent's skull and brought his other fist down in hard hammer blows to the face, breaking the man's nose and sending blood screaming across his mouth and chin. Then he brought his knee up and in one explosive movement pulled his opponent's head down to meet it.

The man dropped backward onto the mat. He did not get up. He did not move.

The crowd erupted, but Po did not hear them. He stood very still, his chest heaving, his hands at his sides, and watched the man's ribcage for the shallow rise and fall that meant he was still breathing. He found it and felt the tension leave his shoulders.

"Is he?" Po asked ROGER quietly.

"His pulse is slow, and his breathing is labored, but he will live." ROGER studied the fallen man with those lavender eyes that saw through skin and bone and muscle to the mechanisms beneath. "What happened to the knee?"

"I had to improvise," Po said, and a small grin appeared on his face, the kind of grin that costs something to earn.

Gunther materialized at ringside as if he had been waiting in the wings for his cue, which, Po reflected, he probably had. The man had a showman's instinct for timing, and a hyena's instinct for everything else. He climbed through the ropes with surprising agility for someone of his bulk and crossed the mat to where Po stood.

"You've done well for yourself, Po. Even I'm impressed." Gunther's voice came out like a rusted gate hinge, a croak that always sounded as though his throat were lined with gravel and phlegm. He clapped Po on the shoulder with a hand that lingered too long. "I spoke with Billy Black, head of the Tommy Boys, about the weapons you're looking for. He's got what you need, and since I'll be brokering the deal I'll take a small cut. Seems only fair."

"Of course. I wouldn't have it any other way," Po responded evenly, buttoning his shirt over the bruises that were already darkening across his ribs.

"Good." Gunther was already walking away, his silk jacket catching the torchlight. He paused at the ropes and looked back. "Stay sharp, Po. The prizefight's in just two days, and as it stands right now you're favored to win. There's a lot riding on you, so don't screw it up."

Po watched him go. The smile had left his face. He understood what Gunther was, the same way he understood what a snake was, by watching where it moved and knowing what it ate. Cards could be marked. Dice could be loaded. But fighting, people would always want fighting. And Gunther wanted to be the man who provided it.

The gorge swallowed them like a throat.

They had been walking for most of a day since leaving the mountain, moving east across the open valley under a sky that offered no cover and no mercy. The sun hammered the dry ground and the heat rose in visible waves that made the horizon shimmer and bend. They had finished the last of their water before midday, and the conversation had thinned along with it, sentences growing shorter, silences growing longer, until they communicated in gestures and grunts and the shared understanding of people who were too tired to waste breath on words.

It had been John's idea to take the gorge. He had studied the old man's map the night before, tracing the topography lines with his finger while Sarah slept and Jayk kept watch, and had found the narrow canyon that cut south through the ridge like a wound that had never healed. It would add time to their journey, he explained. It would stretch their already depleted rations past the point of comfort. But the steep walls would hide them from any airship that passed overhead, and after what they had faced on the mountain, the prospect of walking exposed across open ground for another two days held no appeal for any of them.

"We could head that way," John had said, pointing toward where the land dropped away into shadow. "It would provide access to that canyon. There may even be a natural stream we could use as a source of water. But it'll definitely add some time to our walk."

"What do you think, Jayk?" Sarah had asked.

"I say let's give it a go," Jayk had proposed. "We can use the walls to avoid being seen."

So they had altered their course and descended, and now the walls of the gorge rose around them like the ribs of some enormous dead thing, red and brown and striated with layers of geological history that no one was left to read. The air at the bottom was still and furnace hot, trapped between the rock faces where no wind could reach it. Their shadows had disappeared, swallowed by the shade of the eastern wall, and the dry riverbed beneath their boots was cracked and pale as old bone.

There was no stream.

The dehydration came on gradually, the way darkness comes, so slowly that one does not notice until it has already arrived. John felt it first as a tightness behind his eyes, then as a heaviness in his limbs that made each step feel like a negotiation between his body and the ground. Sarah's lips cracked and bled, and she stopped wiping the blood away because the effort of raising her hand seemed disproportionate to the reward. Jayk simply grew quiet, which for Jayk was its own kind of alarm.

John held the map out before him, squinting at the faded lines. The images on the page swirled and shifted, the contour lines becoming rivers, the rivers becoming roads, the roads becoming a child's drawing of a house with smoke coming from the chimney. He blinked and realized he was holding it upside down. He turned it around.

"Are you sure this is the way?" Sarah asked. Her voice was thin and dry as paper.

"It's the way. I'm following the sun on this map. We just need to keep going east." He paused, stared at the corrected orientation, and amended, "I mean west."

"Oh. Well. That's good." Sarah took two more steps and her foot caught on a stone that seemed to leap up to meet her. She pitched forward and would have gone down if Jayk had not reached for her, his hand finding her waist, his arm pulling her upright against his side.

"Woah. You ok?" Jayk asked.

She looked up at him and put her hand on his cheek. Her eyes were unfocused, seeing something past him, through him, beyond the walls of the gorge to some other place entirely. "I'm fine," she said. "How are you?" She smiled at him, a dreamy, distant smile. "Pretty eyes."

Jayk felt the blood rush to his face. His heart began hammering in a rhythm that had nothing to do with dehydration and everything to do with her hand on his skin and the way she was looking at him. He walked a few beats supporting her with his arm, and with each step he was acutely aware of the warmth of her body against his side.

"Can we sit down for just a minute?" Sarah asked, wriggling free from his grasp. "I'm tired."

"John. John, a little help," Jayk called out. John was stumbling ahead of them, weaving slightly, his eyes fixed on a point in the sky that may or may not have been there.

"Let her sit down for a minute," John slurred, still staring upward at the cloudless heavens. "I need a break too." Then his legs went slack beneath him and he dropped to the ground with a thud, like a puppet whose strings had been cut. The map fluttered from his hand and landed facedown in the dust.

Jayk lowered Sarah to the ground and felt the world tilt around him. The walls of the gorge seemed to breathe, expanding and contracting in a slow, rhythmic pulse that matched the beating of his heart. The sky above the canyon rim was no longer blue but a color he did not have a name for, something between violet and amber, and the stones around him began to hum, a low drone that he could feel in his teeth and the bones of his skull. He blinked and saw figures emerging from the rock face, swaddled in cloaks and hooded jackets, their shapes wavering like heat mirages, moving in quick darting motions that his eyes could not track. They were there and then they were not and then they were there again, closer, and his mind could not decide whether they were real or whether the canyon had finally finished the job the sun had started.

He gave in. The ground rose to meet him and the sky went dark and everything stopped.

When awareness returned, it came in fragments. Coolness against his skin. The sound of dripping water, rhythmic and patient, an underground metronome. A ceiling of rough stone lit by the amber glow of small fires that cast dancing shadows on the walls. He was lying on his back on something softer than the ground, and someone was pressing a wet cloth to his forehead.

The cave was deep, impossibly deep, bored into the canyon wall by water or time or both. It smelled of mineral, of damp earth and stone and the particular coolness that exists only underground, where the sun has never reached. The trading post, for that was what it was, had been carved from this natural formation and expanded over years by human hands into a network of chambers and passages. They had stumbled upon it by accident, or perhaps the people within had allowed them to stumble upon it, which was not the same thing.

They were fed water from an underground pool, cold and clean and tasting faintly of iron, and the water tasted so good that it hurt going down, the way a song hurts when it finds the exact frequency of one's grief. Sarah drank in small sips, her hands shaking around the clay cup. John drank in great gulps and then immediately asked for more. Jayk drank slowly, deliberately, rationing himself out of habit even when rationing was no longer necessary.

After a while they had recovered enough to sit up and hold a conversation. One of the men began asking questions. He was weathered and wary, with the look of someone who had survived long enough to be suspicious of everything, which was, in this world, the look of someone who had survived.

"Spotters saw you heading toward the mountain a couple of days ago," the man said.

"What were you doing out there?" another asked, the question arriving with the bluntness of an interrogation.

"We were looking for a medicinal root to save a friend of ours," John explained. He sat with the cup between both hands, sipping slowly, his senses returning in increments.

"Well, then you're lucky you came this way. Normally we would have left you out there." The first man let the implications of that statement settle before continuing. "You can't be too careful with the raiders and pirates, and of course there's Naomie's thugs." He studied them with the practiced eye of a man who made his living reading strangers. "Did you find what you were looking for?"

"We did. We were just on our way back to Solos," John said.

"If you're heading back to Solos, you're going the wrong way." A young woman stepped forward from behind her father, her arms crossed, her expression carrying the particular exasperation of someone who had watched three people nearly die of stubbornness and dehydration. "You should be going north."

The man looked at his daughter, and something passed between them, a question asked and answered without words. She nodded.

"You can travel with my daughter," the man offered. "She's a good tracker. She'll get you back to Solos."

"We'd appreciate that, thank you," John said, and the gratitude in his voice was unadorned and real. "And thank you for saving our lives. If you hadn't come out when you did, I doubt we would have made it much longer."

"You're welcome. On both accounts." The man stood, and the matter was settled with the economy of people for whom decisions were not abstract exercises but the difference between living and dying. "Rest up a bit. Have something to eat. My daughter will get ready and she'll take you to Solos."

Jayk sat against the cave wall and watched the firelight play across the stone. The raceweed was still in his pack, bundled and secure, the white flowers wilting now but the roots intact, the medicine that was also a poison that was also a prayer. He thought of Ideo lying in the dark of her father's house, her breathing counted by a machine that did not sleep, and he willed the distance between them to be shorter than it was.

At a casino in Solos, seated at a small round table with five other players, Dade was losing at poker when Rensa found him. The room was a riot of noise, cheers and laughter and groans, the sounds of winning and losing swirling together into a palpable air of excitement that permeated every corner. With food being the most valuable commodity, entertainment was surely its kin. Panis et circenses. Bread and circuses. It had been true since Rome, and Rome had never really ended, only changed its clothes.

"Hey, who's winning?" Rensa asked, pulling up a chair and sitting down beside him.

"Right now I'm behind. But that's ok, I'll make it back up." Dade groaned, lifting his hole cards just high enough from the worn felt of the table for her to see them.

"Ewww, not with a hand like that." She said it loudly enough for the entire table to hear. The other players began to smile. Dade threw down his cards and turned his full attention to Rensa, his expression a masterwork of theatrical betrayal.

"What'd ya do that for?!" He glared at her.

"Oh good, you're done!" Rensa smiled brightly, taking him by the arm and hauling him from the table like a mother extracting a child from a candy shop. "He'll only be a minute, boys." When they had gotten far enough from the tables to avoid being overheard, she dropped the smile. "I've found someone who's willing to barter for medicine, but I don't have anything of value. And, apparently, neither do you."

"I might've. I had those guys on the hook, they were about to dump," Dade protested.

"No they weren't."

"How would you know?"

"Because I've watched you play cards, Dade. You have the worst poker face I've ever seen. You might as well hold your hand up to a mirror." She let that land and then moved on. "Either way, what can we use to trade with? Po is working the prizefight, but that's barter coin, and the guy won't take that. I asked. We need something that's marketable." She looked at him with the expression of someone who had already exhausted her own ideas and was now, regrettably, relying on his. "Between the two of us we should be able to figure something out."

Several minutes of quiet contemplation followed. Dade scratched his chin. He stared at the ceiling. He picked at a scab on his knuckle. Finally, with the air of a man who had discovered fire, he said, "What about that stuff Ideo invented?"

"The explosive? That's more of a weapon," Rensa argued.

"Yeah, but maybe one of the gangs could use it? And maybe they'd be willing to trade something useful for it? Something besides barter coin?" He grinned the grin that always preceded his worst ideas. "And I think I know just the gang. Come on."

They made their way south through the Byzantine corridors and cramped passageways of the city, past junctions that had been named by people who were no longer alive and renamed by people who did not care what the originals had been. The steel district announced itself before they reached it, the air growing hotter and carrying the taste of metal and smoke. Foundries lined the tunnels, their exhaust vents channeling black haze along the low ceilings, and the sound of hammers on anvils provided a relentless cadence that rattled in the chest. Dade asked around until somebody pointed them in the direction of a kid wearing the Po' Boys' characteristic purple and green plaid.

They approached the young man. Dade did the talking. "We're looking for Randall. Know where we could find him?"

"Who's asking?" The kid fired back, all of fourteen years old and already wearing the armour of belligerence that passed for maturity in Solos.

Rensa pulled a stiletto from within the folds of her leather patchwork jacket and held it loosely, the blade catching the foundry light. "Listen here, you little rat. Unless you wanna be known as One-eyed Willy, you'll take us to see him."

Dade turned to Rensa and whispered, "One-eyed Willy?"

The kid led them through the foundry, past vats of molten metal that glowed like the open mouths of furnaces, through corridors thick with heat and the acrid smell of slag, and into a back room where several men sat around playing cards. "Wait here. I'll get him for you."

When they were alone, Rensa leaned close. "I hope you know what you're doing."

"Relax. These guys wanna make a name for themselves, and they smuggle in goods from the Spice Road. I'm sure we can get just about whatever we want for something like those explosives."

He wore a purple and green plaid silk vest over a pinstriped suit, and he wore it the way some men wear a crown, as if the garment existed to serve him and not the other way around. Be back here in an hour. I've got something you can use to barter with. Bring me this weapon of yours and if it's worth it we'll make an exchange."

"Right. Yeah. Good point."

Po found Hong in his study. It was a tiny room at the end of the hall, barely large enough for the two worn chairs and the shelves that lined every wall, and those shelves were packed with books, real books, printed on paper that had yellowed and softened with age. The room had the particular smell that all libraries share, that of paper and fabric and wood pulp, accompanied by just the slightest hint of mildew, the smell of knowledge slowly returning to the earth from which it came. A small lamp burned on the table between the chairs, casting a warm circle of light that made the room feel like a sanctuary against the noise and filth of the city beyond its walls.

Hong sat in one of the chairs with a book open across his lap and a pair of delicately crafted reading glasses perched on his nose. He did not look up immediately. Po stood in the doorway, not wanting to disturb the older man, and for a moment the two of them existed in a silence that was not uncomfortable but rather the particular quiet of two people who had learned that most words were unnecessary.

"I used to read to my daughter when she was little," Hong said at last, looking up from his book and removing the glasses, which he set on the small table alongside the open volume. His voice was soft, the voice of a man visiting a memory he both cherished and could not touch. Po entered the room and took the chair beside him. The leather creaked under his weight.

"Do you have any children, Mr. Po?"

Po was quiet for a moment. The lamplight caught the cut over his lip and the bruise along his jaw, and in that light he looked older than he was, or perhaps merely as old as his experiences had made him. "Once. "Then you can understand how important it is that my daughter is back. Many nights I went to sleep with the fear that she had died. Sometimes I still feel that way, even with her here." The next two days passed quickly.

The championship prizefight was held in Gunther's underground auditorium, a cavernous space that had been, in some former life, the basement of something important, a warehouse perhaps, or a parking structure, the bones of which had been repurposed with the ingenuity of people who built their world from the corpse of the one that came before. Torches lined the walls. The ring occupied the center, a raised platform of rough hewn planks surrounded by ropes strung between iron posts driven into the concrete floor. A raised dais overlooked the arena from the north side, furnished with actual chairs, the luxury of sitting down reserved for those who could pay for the privilege.

It was standing room only. Everyone who was anyone had come, and many who were no one had come as well, because spectacle is a hunger that does not care about status. All of the rival gangs were represented, their colors visible in the crowd like patches of disease. Their lieutenants stood in knots of influence, talking and watching and being seen, because that was the other purpose of an event like this, to see and be seen, to remind everyone of one's continued existence.

Gunther found Po in the narrow room behind the walkway that led to the ring. The noise of the crowd penetrated the walls like something trying to get in. Po sat on a low bench with his eyes closed, his breathing slow and measured, the metronomic discipline of a man who was conserving every reserve of energy and calm for what was coming. ROGER stood beside him like a sentinel.

"There's a lot riding on this tonight, Po. I don't have to tell you that, but I feel I need to impress upon you the importance of a victory." Gunther's voice croaked through the small room, filling it with the sound of rust and gravel. "Once the fight is over, I've arranged a meeting with you and Billy Black to pick up the crates of weapons. You'll have what you want, and I'll have what I want. It's a win-win."

"I understand what's at stake. Trust me," Po said without opening his eyes.

"Good. I'm glad we could reach an agreement on this matter." Gunther left the room, and the air felt cleaner for his absence. Po could hear his footsteps retreating up the walkway, heading for his box seat above the crowd, heading for the best view of other people's pain.

Rensa and Dade stood in the thick of the audience, pressed between bodies that smelled of sweat and smoke and anticipation. The fights before the main event had been brutal, men beating each other senseless, sending blood and teeth spilling onto the mat. With every man who had to be dragged from the planks the crowd had applauded louder. One man had his leg broken, and the crowd cheered. Another, his arm, and the crowd groaned, not from sympathy but from the loss of a bet. There had been cracked ribs, a dislocated shoulder, and a shattered clavicle that produced a sound Rensa could hear from twenty feet away.

Rensa was watching the crowd rather than the ring when she noticed them. Two children, working the audience with the coordinated precision of a machine, one bumping, the other dipping. She had spotted the pair earlier that week at one of Po's exhibition bouts, but had let them go, partly out of professional courtesy and partly out of curiosity. Tonight, curiosity had become something else. She recognized the technique. She had used it herself, a lifetime ago.

"Stay here. I'll be right back," she said to Dade, and slipped into the crowd.

She positioned herself in the path of the younger one, the girl, and pretended to be absorbed by the action in the ring. The girl came up smooth, bumping into her with the casualness of someone simply navigating a packed room, and Rensa felt the feathered touch of small fingers reaching for her jacket pocket.

Rensa's hand closed around the girl's wrist like a trap. "Gotcha."

"Hey! Lemme go! Kidnapper!" The child howled, thrashing against Rensa's grip with the ferocity of a caught animal.

"Keep your voice down, or I'll break your skinny little arm," Rensa hissed, dragging her back to where Dade stood. She did not mean it. But the girl did not know that, and in this world the distance between a threat and a promise was measured in the eyes of the person making it.

"What have you got there?" Dade asked, eyeballing the girl with theatrical suspicion.

"A petty thief. Half of a duo." Rensa turned to the girl. "Where's your partner?"

"Why should I tell you?" The girl spat at Rensa's feet with an accuracy that was almost admirable.

"Oh, you're a vicious one. I like her." Dade grinned at the girl with the wide, untrustworthy smile of a man who ate children for breakfast. "Let's cut her open and eat her heart."

The girl's eyes went enormous. "Kumar! Help!" Her brother came barreling through the crowd at the sound of his sister's call, shoving adults aside with an indifference to size that bordered on suicidal.

"Let my sister go!" Kumar demanded, producing a thin blade and brandishing it at Rensa with the steady hand of someone who had used it before. He was twelve years old and looked as though he had already lived enough for thirty.

"What? They're twins!?" Dade looked between the two of them with genuine delight. "Can we keep 'em?"

"I'm Dosha, and you better let me go, or my brother will cut you!" The girl kicked Rensa in the shin with a boot that was far harder than it looked and tried to wriggle free.

"Calm down. We're not here to hurt you." Rensa let the girl go and held up her hands, showing them empty. Kumar did not lower his blade. "I saw you earlier this week. Both of you." She looked at the boy and then at the girl, and something in her face changed, the hardness softening into something that was not quite tenderness but was in the same territory. "Where are your parents?"

"We ain't got no parents," Kumar stated with a defiance that was older than he was. "We make our own way."

Rensa nodded slowly. She knew that sentence. She had said it herself, years ago, in a different city, with the same blade edge voice and the same refusal to let anyone see the wound beneath. Dade knew it too. They had both grown up in circumstances that rhymed with what these children were living, and the recognition was instant and wordless.

"Well, a little friendly advice?" Rensa knelt so she was at their eye level. "I spotted you a mile away. You should find a way to hide your arm. Don't reach so far. And you," she looked at Dosha, "need to drop your shoulder. It's painfully obvious."

"Who are you?" Dosha cocked her head and studied Rensa with one narrowed eye, the look of a creature deciding whether something was predator or potential ally.

Rensa explained to them, simply and without condescension, that they had a choice. They could continue living in Solos, plying their trade as pickpockets, surviving the way they had always survived. Or they could come with her people and learn something different, become part of something larger than themselves. When they chose to stay, Rensa did not get upset. She drew them a map on a scrap of paper, marking the route with quick, sure strokes, and told them to use it if they changed their minds.

Kumar folded the map carefully and tucked it inside his shirt, close to his skin, the way one carries something valuable. Dosha watched Rensa with that cocked head and narrow eye, and then, without a word, took her brother's hand and pulled him back into the crowd. They disappeared between the bodies like fish returning to water, and within seconds they were invisible.

"I give 'em a week," Dade said.

Rensa said nothing. She was watching the place where they had been, and her expression was one that Dade had the good sense not to comment on.

Po stepped into the ring for the first of five bouts. He was the favorite, but he was an unknown, a newcomer whose reputation had been built in days rather than years, and the crowd's allegiance was still split. His first opponent had a weak defense and the unfortunate habit of telegraphing his attacks with a slight drop of the shoulder that Po read like a page. Po came out strong, uncoiling combinations with the fluid precision of a man who had practiced each movement ten thousand times until it lived in his muscles rather than his mind. He knocked the man off balance with his first three punches and then exploited the opening with the patience of a river finding a crack in stone, working the man's guard apart blow by blow, driving him backward until there was nowhere left to go. He brought him to the mat and locked his arm, sliding his leg across the man's throat and wrenching until consciousness departed like a bird leaving a branch.

The crowd cheered. Po stood and raised his hands.

ROGER met him at the ropes with water and a sponge. He dabbed at the blood on Po's face, working with the gentle efficiency of someone who had performed this task a thousand times. "This is mostly his blood, I think," Po said.

"It is," ROGER confirmed. "How do you feel?"

"Fine. He wasn't much of a fighter. Sad to say." There was no boast in it. Only a statement of fact delivered with something that sounded almost like regret, because Po took no pleasure in easy victories, finding in them only the reminder that the world had lost so much that even its fighters were diminished.

They left the ring and walked the narrow passage back to the room behind the arena. Po sat on the bench and closed his eyes and the breathing began again, in through the nose, held, released, each cycle drawing the adrenaline from his blood and replacing it with the calm that was his truest weapon. "The next bout should be with a more difficult opponent," he said to ROGER, taking a long drink of water.

Out in the arena, the announcer was calling for attention, his voice climbing above the crowd noise with the practiced projection of a man who had made his living with his lungs. He was building toward something, and the crowd could feel it, the way an audience can always feel the crescendo before it arrives.

"A last minute entrant from right here in Solos! Introducing the angel of death, the maestro of mayhem, the captain of carnage, let's give it up for IVAAAAN!" The announcer drew the name out in long, rising syllables, and the crowd erupted.

Po opened his eyes. "Gunther didn't say anything about this." His voice was flat, but ROGER, who could read the electrical impulses of a man's nervous system, detected the spike of alertness beneath the calm. "He knows Ivan will fight to the end. He's looking to rid himself of his competition, and he thinks I'm going to do his dirty work." The head of the Hackmen. Trained assassins. Fixers. The people one called when a problem needed solving and the solution was a body in a ditch. They were psychopaths and cold blooded killers who did not flinch at the thought of death, their own or anyone else's.

Po was not the type to back down from a fight. But he was also not a murderer. He had never taken a life unless it was absolutely necessary, and even then the taking left a mark on him that did not heal. Life, he believed, was precious enough, and ending one only further reduced the population of a species that could not afford the loss. "I won't do it," he said aloud. "I will not kill this man."

"And returning champion, in the opposite corner, let's welcome back POOOOO!" The announcer stretched the syllable until it became a sound rather than a name, and the crowd answered with a cacophony of cheers and jeers, a discordant chorus that shook the walls. Bets were placed frantically, coin and goods exchanging hands in a frenzy of speculation.

Po and ROGER walked up the ramp from the back entrance toward the ring. The torchlight painted everything in shades of amber and shadow, and the faces of the crowd blurred into a single organism, a beast with a thousand eyes and one hunger. When Po reached the ring he looked across the mat and saw his opponent for the first time.

Ivan was a tall, lean, muscular edifice of a man with a bald head and an auburn beard that he had allowed to grow wild across his face. His chest was bare, and what Po saw there made his breath catch. Scars. Dozens of them. Hundreds. Lines and curves and geometric patterns carved into the skin with deliberate precision, each one a mark the man had given himself, a diagram of self inflicted wounds that spoke to a condition darker and deeper than anything a fist could reach. He stood in his corner rolling his neck slowly, the vertebrae popping in a sequence that sounded like knuckles cracking one by one, and his eyes, pale and flat, swept the crowd with the detached amusement of a predator surveying a field of prey.

His coach stood beside him, a rangy woman with long flaxen hair that fell over her shoulders like a curtain of pale gold. She leaned close to Ivan and said something, and the two of them looked at Po and began to laugh. It was a calculated thing, a blade disguised as humor, designed to cut before the first punch was thrown.

Po did not allow it to reach him. He had been the subject of psychological manipulation before, by men and women far more skilled than these, and he had learned that the only power such tactics held was the power one gave them.

"He is just a man," ROGER said quietly beside him. "In fact, I believe I detect a small heart murmur."

"He's just a man," Po agreed. He climbed through the ropes and stood in his corner. He closed his eyes one final time, and in the darkness behind his lids he found the breath, the current, the warm river running through his center. He ran through his mental catalogue of defensive positions and attack sequences with the speed and precision of a combat tactician surveying the field before a battle, though he knew nothing about his enemy except what his eyes had shown him, which was that this man was a force of nature wearing a human body like a borrowed suit.

The referee called them together. A few words were spoken that neither fighter heard. The referee raised his hand and brought it down.

Ivan charged.

He came across the ring like something unleashed, not running but surging, a wave of bone and muscle and intent that closed the distance between them in a heartbeat. Po sidestepped, letting the momentum carry Ivan past him, and drove a jab into the man's body as he went by. Ivan barely flinched. It was like striking a wall of packed earth. He turned and came again, his hands reaching, grasping, and Po ducked under them and delivered a right hook to the jaw that would have dropped most men to the mat.

Ivan smiled.

Po felt something cold move through his stomach. Not fear. He had trained the fear out of himself years ago. But something adjacent to it, the recognition that the rules he had learned did not apply here, that this man operated on a different frequency entirely, one where pain was not a deterrent but a hymn.

Ivan got his hands on Po, gripping his shoulders with fingers that felt like iron clamps, and delivered a series of bone crushing knee strikes to Po's midsection that sent the air exploding from his lungs and his body sputtering backward. What Ivan lacked in speed and versatility he compensated for in sheer, annihilating power. Each blow landed with the force of something mechanical, something that did not tire and did not hesitate and did not care.

Po retreated, circling, keeping distance, jabbing to maintain space the way a man holds a torch against a wild animal. He threw combinations that landed clean, snapping Ivan's head back, opening cuts above his eye and along his cheek, but the man kept coming, absorbing punishment with an indifference that was not courage but something else, something that lived in those self inflicted scars, a relationship with pain so intimate that additional doses of it simply did not register.

Ivan delivered a front kick that caught Po in the sternum and sent him stumbling backward, and in that moment of imbalance Ivan was on him, grabbing him by the waist and throwing him across the ring with an ease that was almost contemptuous. Po hit the mat hard, rolled, and was on his feet again before Ivan could follow up, but the message had been delivered. Ivan was toying with him. A man of his size and strength could have ended this fight at any point, even against an opponent of Po's skill and conditioning. He was prolonging it because he enjoyed it, because the crowd's screaming fed something inside him that the self inflicted scars could not reach.

Po understood then what he was dealing with, and he adjusted. Ivan was underestimating him. Enjoying himself too much to finish the job. And that enjoyment, that arrogance, was a door that could be forced open. If Po accelerated his attacks, if he pushed the tempo past the point where Ivan could savor each exchange, he could throw the bigger man off his rhythm and find the opening he needed.

Po moved in. He closed the distance deliberately, offering himself as bait, and when Ivan reached for him, Po was already inside the grab, too close for the long arms to find leverage. He fired uppercuts into Ivan's body, short vicious blows that targeted the liver and the floating ribs, and for the first time he saw something flicker across Ivan's face, not pain exactly, but the acknowledgment of it, the recognition that this small man could hurt him. Ivan grabbed Po in a full body hold and lifted him off the mat like a man picking up a child. Po brought both fists down in hammer blows to Ivan's face and then, with his palms open, smashed both of Ivan's ears simultaneously. The concussive force sent Ivan's equilibrium into chaos, his inner ear screaming with damage, and he dropped Po instantly, staggering sideways with his hands pressed to the sides of his head.

Po did not hesitate. He swept Ivan's ankle and brought the big man crashing to the mat, the impact reverberating through the platform like a drumbeat. Before Ivan could recover, Po scrambled around behind him and locked the man's leg, stepping across his body and wrenching the joint, forcing Ivan to flip onto his stomach. Ivan thrashed and clawed at the mat but had no counter for the hold. For the first time in the fight, Po had the upper hand.

The crowd was deafening. The sound poured over the ring in waves, a tide of human noise that drowned thought and language and left only the primal call and response of violence witnessed. Po tightened his grip. Ivan growled, a sound that came from somewhere deeper than his throat, somewhere animal and ancient.

Then it happened.

The first explosion punched through the auditorium like a fist through paper. The concussion hit before the sound did, a wave of force that lifted bodies from the ground and sent the torches guttering wild. A section of the wall to the south disintegrated in a bloom of fire and dust and spinning debris. The crowd screamed, a collective shriek that cut through the ringing in everyone's ears, and then the second explosion came, this one closer, ripping through the platform itself and sending the two fighters toppling one over the other in a tangle of limbs and splintered wood.

Dust filled the air. The torchlight dimmed to a hellish amber glow filtered through suspended particles. People were running, climbing over each other, trampling the fallen in their panic to reach the exits. The ringing in Po's ears was so loud it had become a kind of silence, a wall of sound so complete that individual noises could not penetrate it.

Rensa and Dade were at his side before the dust had settled. They were not running. They were not panicked. They moved through the chaos with the calm of people who had known what was coming, because they had, though what they had provided was the weapon and not the plan, a distinction that mattered less with every passing second.

"Guess the fight's over," Rensa said, helping Po drag Ivan from the ruins of the platform. Ivan was conscious, bleeding from a cut on his scalp, his eyes wild with a confusion that looked foreign on his face, as though the concept of being caught off guard was one he had not encountered before.

"Let me have your attention, please!" The voice came from above. Randall stood on the dais, the box seats cleared of their wealthy patrons, and he looked down on the chaos below with the serene composure of a man who had been waiting for this moment his entire life. He wore the purple and green plaid vest. His shoes were still polished. Behind him, his men stood in a line, armed and silent, their faces hidden behind scarves and bandanas, an honour guard for a new king crowning himself on the ruins of the old order.

"The Po' Boys are taking over. And anyone who has a problem with that can come see me!" His voice carried through the auditorium with the projection of a man who had rehearsed this speech, who had practiced it in the mirror, who had waited for the moment when the stage was set and the audience was captive and the only remaining question was whether they would applaud or be buried. "We're expanding our operation! If you place a bet, if you hire a whore, if you buy a weapon, it comes through us! Understood?"

The crowd, what remained of it, stared up at him in the stunned silence of people who have just witnessed the rules of their world rewritten in fire.

"You've seen what we can do, and that was just a small taste of our firepower. Don't force me to show you what we're truly capable of!" Randall surveyed the damage with the pride of an artist regarding his finished canvas. The smoke curled around him. The dust settled on his shoulders like a mantle.

Po stood in the wreckage of the ring, his body bruised and bleeding, his ears still ringing, and looked at Rensa. She met his gaze without flinching. There would be a conversation about this, and it would not be pleasant, but it would not happen here, not now, not in the smoke and the screaming and the new silence of a city that had just changed hands.

Dade stood beside her, and for once in his life he was not smiling.

The circus had come to Solos. And the price of admission had just gone up.
