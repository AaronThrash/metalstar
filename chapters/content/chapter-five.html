The canyon walls echoed with wailing.

Great ululations spewed from jagged mouths as Jayk's sword found its way through bellies and hearts. Two of the beasts already lay dead at his feet. Three more had fired their weapons, crackling bolts of electrostatic fury that he'd barely evaded, the air still humming where the charges had passed.

Their blood pooled in the mud, thick and dark.

Then Po arrived, his squad flooding into the chaos, and Jayk heard his name called out like a warning. He turned just in time to see one of the monsters behind him, its cannon already glowing, the charge building toward his skull.

He dropped to his knees.

The swing was instinct, muscle memory forged in years of practice. His blade carved diagonally across the creature's torso, slicing through the harness straps that held the shoulder-mounted cannons in place. The weapons clattered to the ground as the creature's guts spilled onto the wasteland floor, thick and reeking. The Sky Reaper screamed, a sound like metal tearing.

For one moment, Jayk stood still and beheld the spectacle before him.

Grotesque. Violent. Holy in its horror.

The Sky Reapers were fierce, towering nine to ten feet, their shoulder-mounted electrostatic cannons firing with savage precision. A heavy fog began to manifest over the battlefield, the cloud produced by charged particles ionizing the moisture in the air. More beasts emerged from the slave facility, pouring out like a plague.

And then, from the flanks, Ideo and Rensa brought their troops rushing in to join the carnage.

The battle swelled into a symphony of screams.

Ideo broke from her squad, moving low and fast toward one of the nearby airships. Her impact gel would cripple it if she could get close enough. Jayk saw her go, a shadow slipping through chaos, and trusted her to do what she did best.

He bent down and retrieved one of the fallen electrostatic cannons from where it had clattered to the ground. Turned it over in his hands. Pushed. Pulled. Nothing. The weapon refused to yield its secrets.

A discharge rocketed past his side, close enough that the branching arcs of electricity danced across his skin before dissipating. A narrow miss. He'd seen what their weapons did to human flesh, the way it blackened and split. He had no intention of learning firsthand.

He tossed the cannon aside and made for the facility.

The slaves were inside. That was the mission: cripple the alien forces, burn the gardens, free the people.

—

The planetary reengineering had already transformed the Earth into something unrecognizable.

It had begun with a garden.

Now, generations later, the world had become a baroque fantasia of alien foliage, twisting and winding across every landscape, painting bizarre images of blossoming flora over the bones of the old world. Tangling puce vines. Twisting cerise limbs. Wild flourishing blossoms of gilded lobelia and aster-like herbs stretching for miles. Enormous violet trunks erupted from the land, forming masses of unearthly efflorescence in all directions. Slender blue limbs emerged from thickset trunks, moving with their own volition, searching, grasping.

The variegations filled every horizon. It was impossible to imagine the planet had ever been anything but this.

Between the dense farrago of distorted trees and vining shrubs, a jewel box of marvelously colored plant life, there existed only sparse patches of ruddy heath and uncultivated soil. The last vestiges of a dead and dying world.

It was diabolical in its beauty.

And the humans who tended it were little more than cattle. Property. Tools with which to cultivate the land.

—

Jayk turned from the carnage and made for the encampment, and the nearer he came the less it resembled anything built by hands.

It rose from the earth like something that had pushed its way up from beneath. A vast swollen mass of organic matter, ribbed and glistening, the color of old bruises and dried blood. Its flanks wept thin amber rivulets that hissed where they met the ground.

There were no walls in any honest sense. No corners. No architecture. Only the heaving contours of a thing that had grown rather than been raised.

At its base yawned the entrance: a gaping maw torn low in its side, lipped with pale folded tissue, dark and wet, breathing out a warm fetid air. Less a doorway than a wound in the world that had never been allowed to close.

It looked, for all the world, like the opening of some immense beast that had laid itself down over the land and gone to sleep with hundreds of souls still living in its belly.

He paused at the threshold. Then he stepped inside, and was swallowed up by its immensity.

The din of the battle fell away behind him, muffled to a distant heartbeat by the walls of meat. Within, there was no path. No corridor, no sign, no door to mark one chamber from the next, only a writhing warren of organic passages that branched and folded and doubled back upon themselves, slick underfoot.

The ceiling pressed low in places, then opened without warning into cavities vast and vaulted as the inside of a ribcage. Faint amber light bled from veins in the walls and pulsed, slow and arrhythmic, like something digesting. The air was thick, warm, wet with rot, and it clung to the back of his throat.

He moved through the entrails of the thing the only way he could, by instinct, by the cries that echoed from somewhere deeper in, choosing each turning blind and trusting it would carry him toward the living and not deeper into the dark. The walls shivered as he passed. Now and then a passage narrowed around him like a swallowing throat, and he pressed through it, and tried not to think about what it was he was walking inside of.

It was here, at the dead end of one such passage, that he came upon a long chamber lit by a single sickly glow.

Through a reinforced window, he saw them: a horde of people, young and old, dressed in rags or nothing at all. Depression and hopelessness leapt from their faces like something physical, something that could infect you if you looked too long.

And there, against the far wall, sat a girl.

Mid-twenties. Grungy red hair. A sadness in her eyes that seemed deeper than the others, more complex. She alone didn't bear the markings of hard labor, no calluses, no scars, no sunburn from endless hours in the gardens.

She was different.

There were no doors here. No locks. No hinges. Only a wall of living bars, floor-to-ceiling columns of pale ribbed tissue grown close as the teeth of a comb, wet and faintly pulsing, like the inside of some great throat sealed against the light.

They glistened. Now and then they shivered, as though the wall itself were breathing.

The prison was alive, and it would not open for any key. It would take a different kind of force.

The irony was not lost on him.

He heard movement behind him, soft steps, too light to belong to any beast. He turned, blade ready, and found Ideo glaring back at him.

"You didn't really think you could do this alone, did you?" Her sarcasm cut through the tension. "Stand back."

She opened her hand. Two slender vials lay across her palm, one a weeping amber, the other clear as water. The same two she'd shown him on the hill before the charge.

He understood at once.

He rushed to the bars and shouted to the prisoners. "Get back against the far wall! Cover your ears!"

The prisoners stirred, confused, terrified. Some moved. Others froze.

Behind him, Ideo mixed her chemicals with practiced calm. Within moments she'd formed a tiny ball, no bigger than a marble, and flung it at the wall.

The explosion jarred his teeth.

Thunder filled the chamber. Smoke and a sharp, acrid haze billowed outward. When it cleared, a ragged hole had been torn through the living bars, their severed ends blackened and hissing where the venom had eaten them through.

"Hurry," Ideo said. "There's not much time."

Along the ruined edges the pale tissue had already begun to stir. To swell. To reach. Severed columns groped blindly toward one another across the gap, threads of new growth spinning out like roots seeking soil, the hole narrowing even as they watched.

"Move." Her voice cracked like a whip. "MOVE -- it's closing!"

Jayk followed, speaking sharply, urgently. "You have to come with us now." He scanned the room, counting faces. Hundreds. Hundreds of souls held prisoner by these creatures. "We're here to help you, but you have to move!"

Most began shuffling toward the exit, disoriented, blinking at the smoke. Some lingered, too terrified to leave the safety of the cell they knew.

Jayk ran to the red-haired girl and took her arm. "Come on. We have to go."

Her dull eyes gave no sign she understood.

He softened his voice, leaned closer. "Don't worry. I'll protect you."

Something flickered in her gaze. She nodded, silent, and rose to follow.

He took her hand. She grasped it tightly.

They ran toward Ideo, who stood at the shrinking gap waving the freed prisoners through, counting heads, keeping order in chaos, the living bars knitting closed at her shoulders.

Jayk could see the fear in their eyes, all of them, the rescued and the rescuers alike.

"How many more rooms like this?" he asked.

"Not sure. This place is a maze." Ideo scanned the corridor, already moving. "I'll check for more cells. You get these people out."

Jayk nodded and turned back toward the battle.

Outside, chaos.

Many of the rescued stood frozen, paralyzed by captivity and the sudden violence of freedom. Others grabbed tools from the dead and threw themselves into the fight. Still others scattered in all directions, running for anywhere that wasn't here.

The red-haired girl slumped against him, and he nearly dropped her.

Then they were surrounded.

The creatures had seen their cargo escaping. They redoubled their assault, two of the beasts bearing down on Jayk and the girl. Their shoulder-mounted cannons hummed with charging energy.

Rensa's squad appeared from the flank, buying him seconds.

Jayk set the girl down. "Stay here."

He raised his sword and ran toward the nearest creature, hoping to draw its fire, to pull them away from her.

They ignored him.

Both beasts turned toward the girl.

*No.*

He reversed course, sprinting back, and leapt onto the nearest creature's back. His sword plunged deep into its shoulder, just above the cannon harness. "You were supposed to follow me!" he shouted.

The Sky Reaper let out a horrible shriek. It reached back, caught Jayk's arm, and flung him like a rag doll.

He hit the ground hard. Stunned. Dizzy. The impact rang in his skull.

The creature yanked the sword from its shoulder and tossed it aside. Injured but undefeated. It turned, mouth filled with blood, and howled at him. Lifeless eyes. Serrated teeth. A nightmare given flesh.

It charged.

Jayk watched it come, weaponless, helpless…

And then its chest exploded.

A crackling bolt of electrostatic fire tore through the creature from behind, spilling its contents across the battlefield. Another blast fired just past Jayk's feet, scorching the earth. One of its own had shot it in the back, then fired again and missed him by inches.

He scrambled for his sword, shouting. "A little help!"

The creature that had fired, the one that had saved him by accident, was already taking aim again. He watched the charged particles accumulate in its shoulder-mounted cannon, condensing into a bolt of pure electricity.

"Now!" He was exposed. No cover. No time.

"NO!"

The creature's head exploded.

Jayk flinched as gore rained down. The beast toppled backward, its cannon discharging uselessly into its own corpse, electricity crackling across dead flesh.

Another millisecond and he'd have been a memory in someone else's thoughts.

He turned.

The girl stood there.

One of their weapons lay at her feet, smoke still rising from the barrel. She had picked it up. She had fired it.

That was impossible.

But she had fired it. Twice. And saved his life.

She looked at him with a determined sort of fear in her eyes.

He walked toward her, something strange welling up in his chest. The battle still raged around them, but the sounds grew distant, muffled, like the faraway rumbling of a storm on the horizon. The screaming and clashing of metal faded to a dull roar.

It was surreal. As if he wasn't actually there.

Her eyes locked with his as he moved closer, yet it seemed they were miles apart. Behind her, one of Po's men was shouting, waving his arms, his face obscured by thin fog. Jayk couldn't hear his voice, couldn't make out the words. His ears ringing like church bells.

Every step felt heavy. Battle weariness was setting in, draining the life from his limbs.

He moved closer.

And then he heard it, faint but familiar. The crackling of electrostatic particles charging behind him.

Before he could move, before he could shout a warning, the bolts streaked past him.

She spun like a top and fell.

It happened so quickly.

One moment she was standing. The next she was a heap on the ground, and the sounds of battle came crashing back against his mind louder than before.

He ran to her without a thought for his own safety.

She lay in her own blood, red mixing with soil, a confused jumble of mud and crimson. Her arm lay beside her… beside her, no longer attached; nerves still quivering, fingers twitching as if pulled by marionette strings.

Jayk stood frozen. A macabre portrait of a moment suspended in time.

She didn't scream. Didn't wail or convulse. Tears of agony streamed down her face, but she made no sound. Her body was dumping beta-endorphins through her brain, flooding her with shock, protecting her from the full weight of what had happened.

She wasn't bleeding as badly as she should have been. The electrostatic bolt had cauterized the wound even as it tore her apart.

He had promised to protect her.

He had failed.

He knelt beside her, and for a long moment they were alone on the battlefield. The chaos continued around them… screams, explosions, the crack of energy weapons, but none of it touched this small circle of silence.

Her eyes abandoned all desire to focus. She didn't speak a word. But when he lifted her from the foul earth, she folded into him, a gentle capitulation, trusting him even now.

Her delicate frame hung weightless in his arms.

For a moment, they must have appeared as one.

The field was strewn with the bodies of the dead and dying.

Jayk maneuvered through the detritus, carrying her as gently as a kitten. Blood soaked into his clothes. Her blood. He tried not to think about how much of it there was.

Then he saw him.

A man emerged from the carnage, walking toward them with purpose. He was dressed spotlessly in white, impossibly clean amid the mud and gore, with a small pin on his lapel bearing the image of Earth. His hair was cut neatly, parted along the side. His tyrian eyes implored Jayk, yet he made no sound.

He extended his arms.

Looked down at her ruined body.

Jayk gave her to him freely. There was something in the stranger's manner, a fatherly protectiveness, an urgency beneath the calm, that made refusal impossible.

The man carried her, and they walked together across the battlefield.

Jayk had no idea who he was or why he had appeared. But he let them go ahead while he turned to tend to the others.

Po limped toward him, body weak, voice hoarse. "We should head back. We've done what we came to do." He gestured toward the garden, where flames were beginning to rise. "Rensa's team is burning it down. Let's get the survivors and wounded to safety."

Jayk nodded, breathlessly.

Ideo and Dade rounded up their squads, protecting the rear as they made their exit. They ushered the freed prisoners toward the rock faces, out into the wastes, northeast toward the city.

They moved at a hurried pace for as long as they could. But the wounded couldn't sustain it. After several minutes, they slowed. The bunker was still a two-day hike through the forgotten city and the airfield beyond.

Po called a halt. They set up camp along a thin stretch of bushes and did what they could for the wounded.

The man in white worked through the night.

He moved from person to person, dressing wounds, setting bones, wrapping and bandaging. He helped to heal the broken. But his attention never wavered from the girl. He kept a vigilant eye on her condition even as he tended to others.

Po and the rest slept. The only sounds were the wretched moans of the suffering.

Jayk watched him work.

Something was wrong.

The man's movements were peculiar, precise in a way that felt unnatural, too smooth, too efficient. Jayk couldn't place it at first.

Then it hit him.

The man wasn't breathing.

Jayk rose slowly and moved toward him. If he could engage him in conversation, get close enough to be sure... perhaps his eyes were playing tricks.

The man turned as Jayk approached.

Before Jayk could speak, the stranger's voice emerged: gentle, measured. "I want to thank you for saving her."

The words disarmed him.

The man reached out and touched Jayk's arm, then took his right hand in both of his own. He examined Jayk's arm as if inspecting it, cupping his hand carefully.

"You have a hairline fracture in the upper extremity of your humerus." A pause. "Do you feel that?"

A sharp sting lanced through Jayk's arm. He flinched, drawing in a quick breath.

"I can set it for you if you'd like." The man's eyes moved to the other wounded. "For the others, we'll need to get somewhere safe to operate. Soon."

Jayk stared at him.

Cold hands. Lavender eyes. No breath. No fear.

The truth settled into place.

This man, this *thing*, was one of them. The robots from the compound. But far more advanced than anything Jayk had seen. A relic of the lost world, walking among them as if he had never left.

Jayk wasted no time.

"Who are you?"

Jayk found Po in the dark. "We need to leave."

Po nodded. No argument. They both knew.

They entered the city ahead of sunrise.

Light pierced through broken glass and fell along twisted metal fragments, casting shadows that crawled across the ground like drunken silhouettes. The walk was slow. Everyone was tired. Hungry. There was barely enough water for them all.

They moved between the ruins of skyscrapers and shattered storefronts, the bones of a world that had forgotten them.

Then Jayk saw it. A yellow wall on the horizon, rising like a wave.

"RUN!"

The sandstorm came bellowing toward them.

They scattered between buildings, scrambling for cover as howling winds filled the streets with debris. Chunks of concrete and steel broke loose from the tops of structures and pelted the ground below. A crooked length of metal fell from above and landed inches from Po.

They were trapped. Directionless. Taking shelter when and where they could, scurrying through the city like mice fleeing a flood.

John had been alone for too long.

Two days since he'd found any cans of food. The mice and snakes were nearly hunted to extinction. It hadn't rained in weeks, and the stale water at the bottom of old cans was all that kept him alive.

He'd woken from nightmares three times that day, wrestling invisible enemies, drenched in sweat.

It was becoming painfully clear: he could no longer afford distrust. This isolation had to end.

He lay on his back, staring at the cracks lining the ceiling, conserving energy. Beside him sat a stack of books he'd read several times over. Manuals. Business machines. Useless material for a useless world.

Then the light shifted.

A dull roar, like a train whistle, pierced the silence. The building shook. Wind and sand charged in through every gap, every crack. Terrible shrieks and moans issued from outside.

John leapt up and ran for the crawlspace he'd dug near the entrance, but funnels of dirt spun through the lobby, forcing him back inside.

And then he saw them.

People. Dressed in modest rags and torn clothes. Charging into the adjacent buildings, seeking refuge from the storm.

This was his moment.

He'd been a Gunnery Sergeant. He knew how to move, how to blend, how to become invisible in plain sight.

He stripped off his cleaner clothes, the ones that marked him as an outsider, and wriggled out into the chaos. He inserted himself among the refugees, hunching his shoulders, adopting their exhausted shuffle. He acted disoriented. Wandered.

One of the men in gear grabbed his arm, said something about getting over by the others, then disappeared into the dust.

John let himself be herded.

He looked like them now. Tired. Weather-beaten. Just another survivor.

No one questioned him. No one even looked twice.

The storm raged.

They ran through doorways and scuttled behind walls. Clouds of concrete and dust rained down. One of the women screamed, then suddenly stopped. No one could get a bearing on where anyone else was.

Shards of ancient glass fell in huge broken sheets, crashing to the ground like frozen lightning.

They were powerless.

Then, as quickly as it had come, the storm vanished, leaving a tidal wave of anguish in its wake.

"Jayk! JAYK!"

Po's voice cut through the settling dust.

"I'm here!" Jayk called from an adjacent building, stumbling through a fog of debris. "What about the others?"

"We got separated."

Jayk emerged and met him in the street. Others began to appear, a few at a time, coughing, bleeding, dazed.

"Rensa! Dade!" Po shouted, while Jayk ran from building to building checking for survivors.

He found the man in white moving slowly, listing to one side. A short length of metal protruded from his leg.

Jayk ran to him and grabbed his arm. "Hey, you've got metal in your leg. You shouldn't be walking."

The man looked down at the wound. Without expression, he reached down, pulled the metal free, and tossed it casually aside. Then he looked back up at Jayk, lavender eyes calm.

"I'll be fine. Please check on the others. We need to assess the damage."

Jayk stared at him for a long moment.

Then nodded.

His suspicions confirmed.

"Jayk, over here!"

Rensa called from behind a thick wall. She'd found another injured person, a woman with a deep gash in her thigh.

Po rushed over. "We need to gather everyone we can find, injured and healthy. Carry them if we have to. Whatever it takes." He knelt beside the woman and pressed her hand firmly against the wound. "Keep pressure here to stem the bleeding, understand?"

She nodded, teeth clenched.

"I'll make a tourniquet."

"Po, get everybody together!" Jayk shouted across the ruins. "Has anyone seen Dade?"

"Over here!" Dade's voice came from deeper in the wreckage. He emerged dragging another survivor from a collapsed doorway.

Jayk ran to help.

—

It took hours.

They pulled the wounded from the rubble, gathered everyone into the open, passed out what remained of their food. The provisions were spartan, barely enough to feed the group, but it would sustain them for the journey home.

They would be weak. But they would make it back.

They triaged the wounded. Some went on makeshift stretchers. Others into slings dragged along the ground. Still more received bandages and were told to walk.

They moved like that for many hours, a slow procession through the ruins of the forgotten city. As the sun began to set, they reached the airfield that marked the halfway point.

They stopped beside a row of empty cargo jets, their skeletal frames frozen in time. The massive husks would serve as shelter for the night.

Po found Jayk near one of the jet engines, staring out at nothing.

"Had any more headaches?"

Jayk shook his head. "Not since I came to the bunker."

Po was quiet for a moment. Then: "We lost a lot of lives saving them." He looked back at the horde of injured and dying spread across the tarmac. "I hope we're doing the right thing here."

Jayk had already given it thought. He'd hoped to avoid the conversation, but Po deserved honesty.

"We rescue as many as we can. If even one person is lost to these creatures when we could have saved them, then we've failed."

It was quixotic. He knew that. But it was also who he was.

He just hoped his idealism wouldn't be the death of them all.

"Besides," he added, "their numbers strengthen our army."

Po looked around at the ragtag group. Elderly. Injured. Dying.

"We don't have an army, Jayk. And I'm not sure what we'd do with one if we did." His voice was gentle but firm. "We're completely out of our element. You're the only one with any military knowledge, and everything you know came from books." A pause. "I'm not saying I don't admire your enthusiasm. I just want to make sure we're doing this for the right reasons."

Po still didn't understand. None of them did.

Jayk turned to face him.

"I'm sure that when the time comes, we'll know what to do with it." His voice hardened. "Po, war is coming. After what we just did, you can be sure of it."

He turned away and went to find somewhere to sleep.

Behind him, Po stood alone in the shadow of the dead aircraft, watching the stars emerge one by one.

—

"Jayk, wake up! JAYK!"

Rensa's voice tore through his sleep. He sat up abruptly and cracked his head against the steel frame of the helicopter he'd been sleeping in.

Disoriented. Heart pounding. He scrambled toward her voice.

The girl lay on a sling near the jet's wheel well, pale and lethargic. Sweat and dried blood matted her hair. Her breathing was shallow, barely there.

The man in white knelt beside her.

"What's wrong?" Jayk asked, already knowing.

"She's dying."

The words hung in the air like a funeral bell.

"We still have a day's hike through the desert," Jayk said. "Even longer with the wounded." His eyes found Rensa, searching for an answer she couldn't give.

But she gave him one anyway.

"Take her alone." Rensa pressed her supplies into his hands. "You can travel faster without us. Take the water. Use these clothes for bandages."

He tried to refuse, but she cut him off.

"No. Go. If you hurry, you can be there before nightfall."

The man in white rose to his feet, smooth and certain. "I'll come with you. You could use my assistance."

Jayk looked him over. The man's leg was in horrible shape, some sort of milky fluid staining his white pants where the metal had pierced him. He'd be a burden. A liability.

But something in those lavender eyes said otherwise.

"Alright," Jayk said. "Just keep up."

The man regarded him with the faintest trace of amusement.

"I'll keep up with you." A pause. "Can you keep up with me?"

He glanced down at his ruined leg, then back up. "Hand me a bandage, if you would."

Jayk tore a shirt lengthwise and passed it over. The man wrapped his leg with quick, precise movements, like he'd done it a thousand times before.

Then he bent down, lifted the girl into his arms as if she weighed nothing, and started walking.

He didn't wait for Jayk.

He didn't look back.

Jayk grabbed the pack and hurried after him, already beginning to doubt who was going to have to keep up with whom.

They moved northeast across the sand and rocky terrain, the man in white carrying Sarah like she was made of glass. He moved fast. Too fast for a man with a wounded leg. Too smoothly.

After a long while, Jayk had to stop.

"We need to rest," he huffed, hands on his knees. "Just for a minute."

The man turned and walked back to him without a hint of exertion.

"I don't need to rest, Jayk." The lavender eyes watched him steadily. "I thought you could keep up."

Jayk dropped his pack and perched on the edge of a flat rock, pulling out the canteen.

"Well, this is a little unfair." He took a long drink. "You're a robot. I'm a human. I need to rest."

The man looked toward the distant hills, then back.

"Then we stop."

He lowered Sarah gently to the ground, cradling her head as he set her down. Then he straightened and stood still, watching Jayk drink.

After a moment: "What gave me away?"

Jayk wiped his mouth. "You mean apart from the fact that you have milk for blood?"

A slight nod. A sideways glance.

"You knew before that. What gave me away?"

Jayk considered the question.

"Men have fear," he said finally. "But you didn't."

The silence stretched between them.

Then Jayk handed him the canteen. "For her."

The man knelt beside Sarah and carefully tipped water to her lips.

"How far is it to the bunker?" he asked after a time.

Jayk pointed toward the horizon. "Past that mountain ridge. The one shaped like a camel's hump."

The man looked, nodded.

Then he turned back to Jayk, and something shifted in his expression. Something serious.

"I have to ask you something, Jayk. And I hope you take me in the most serious manner."

It wasn't a request. It was an appeal.

"What are those creatures?"

Jayk wasn't sure where to begin.

"All my life, they've been here," he said slowly. "I can't imagine a time when they weren't."

He stared out at the desert, at the dead world stretching in every direction.

"I was raised in a museum. With other families. We huddled together. Protected each other." His voice dropped. "We avoided them. Hid from them. Survived."

A pause.

"Then one day, they found us."

He could still hear the screaming. Still smell the smoke.

"They killed almost everyone I knew. My parents. My friends. Everyone."

He looked at his hands.

"I escaped somehow. Been on my own ever since."

The man in white listened without interruption. Without judgment.

"I have no idea what they are," Jayk continued. "Or where they came from. I just know they're not from around here."

The silence settled between them like dust.

Then the man knelt, lifted Sarah again, and looked toward the mountain ridge.

"We should be going now."

Jayk grabbed his pack and followed.

They walked in silence for a long time.

The sun moved across the sky. The ridge grew closer. Sarah's breathing stayed shallow but steady.

Finally, Jayk asked the question he'd been holding since the battlefield.

"What's your name? Or do you have one?"

The man didn't break stride.

"I am a ROGER," he said. "A Robotic Genetic Engineer."

He glanced back, and for the first time, something like warmth crossed his features.

"Build number 5954."

Jayk heaved his pack higher on his shoulder, keeping pace.

"How did you get here?"

ROGER looked down at the girl in his arms. At the pale face and the ruined arm and the life that hung by a thread.

"Sarah brought me."
