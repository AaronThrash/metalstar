The dogmatic city of Sid Mantelis was considered the last remaining vestige of true human civilization.

To enter into the sacred protective society of clergy and holy men, one must virtually sell one's soul, promising everlasting allegiance to the city and unwavering devotion to its faith. In return, those who lived under the control of the clerics were offered full immunity from enslavement by the creatures. Protection. Purpose. The illusion of order in a world that had forgotten the meaning of the word.

A shimmering translucent dome enclosed the city like a bell jar over a specimen, a cordon sanitaire of electromagnetic nanoparticles that prohibited outside air, radiation, and liquid from piercing its membrane. Inside, the atmosphere was clean. Controlled. The weather obeyed. And if you stood at the center of the main courtyard and looked up, you could almost believe the sky was real.

 

Cathedral spires pierced the silvery wisps of an oxygen-rich sky, their stone faces carved with the likenesses of animals long since vanished from the earth. Below them, vast interlocking pathways wound through parks where elaborately shaped topiaries depicted the gods: bears, wolves, elephants, lions, their leafy forms trimmed with obsessive precision, each hedge an altar, each garden a shrine.

The images could be found everywhere. Embroidered on the garments of the people. Sewn into intricate patchworks. Fashioned out of colorful cloth that hung from windows and archways like banners in a kingdom that had forgotten what kingdoms were for.

Verdant farmland stretched across the interior, rows of fruits and vegetables cultivated with care, their abundance a quiet rebuke to the starving world beyond the dome. An extensive network of sewage and drainage systems provided the necessary water reclamation and waste disposal. And just outside the dome's edge, a literal cottage industry had sprouted up around these sewers; humans surviving off the garbage disposed of by the city's inhabitants. The faithful ate. The faithless picked through what remained.

When night fell, the dome bathed the sacred city in sepia-toned hues and soft shadows that pooled among the dense foliage like amber poured from heaven. The pastoral landscape took on the quality of a painting: warm, golden, impossibly serene.

It was a monument to human achievement in times of great difficulty.

It was also a lie.

A small group of dominant men could be seen walking devoutly about the grounds of the immense city, dressed in cowls and albs, their faces hidden behind carved wooden masks in the shapes of animals.

These were the personas.

The persona was a part of the ceremonial garb that the clergy wore with the rest of their vestments: the alb, the cincture, the sash. The albs themselves were ornate, decorated with fine patterns of crewelwork representing the animal gods. Each image, known as an "aspect," was embroidered in silver and gold thread in rich detail and featured prominently on the front of the robe and along the sash when one was worn.

The persona disguised the clergy from the laity with a shroud of anonymity, preserving, in theory, the relationship of equality between them. No member of the clergy was to be elevated above any member of the congregation. They masqueraded as representations of the animal deities, keeping their faces covered at all times.

In theory, the masks made all men equal before the gods.

In practice, the masks made all men invisible. And invisible men, as history has proven time and again, are capable of anything.

Worship occurred regularly at scheduled intervals throughout the day. The faithful gathered. The clergy presided. The hymns rose through the dome like smoke, and the people believed they were heard.

Beauty belied the evil that stood hidden in plain view.

The magnum opus that was Sid Mantelis had not been built by visionaries. It had not been born from compassion or civic duty or the noble desire to preserve humankind.

It grew out of necessity. And necessity, when exploited by the ruthless, becomes tyranny.

There was no single architect to blame. No lone villain whose removal might topple the edifice. It was a confluence of actions, a constant redirection of power, a system designed to perpetuate itself. The people who gave birth to Sid Mantelis were a group of greedy, lying, back-stabbing, power-hungry madmen whose ability to dominate the will of the people was their grand prestige.

They had built a paradise.

And they had built it on a foundation of rot.

In the sanctum, Cygnus knelt on the prie-dieu.

The prayer bench was old, older than anyone living, its wood worn smooth by centuries of supplicant hands. The room was dim, lit only by the amber glow of candles that flickered in their sconces, casting long shadows across the stone walls. It smelled of incense and dust and the particular stillness of rooms where men go to speak to gods who do not answer.

Before him lay an open book.

The pages were thick, yellowed, fragile with age. Nearly four hundred years old. The Mutual of Omaha's Wild Kingdom a relic from an era considered virtually prehistoric. Its pages were filled with richly colored illustrations, photographs, and beautiful descriptions of animals that had once roamed the Earth in numbers too vast to count.

They were all gone now. Every last one.

The book was the cornerstone of the faith. The sacred text from which all worship flowed. And Cygnus handled its pages with the reverence of a man who understood exactly what it was worth, not as scripture, but as a tool.

He turned a page. A full-color photograph of a male lion stared back at him, its mane golden, its eyes fierce with a sovereignty that needed no validation.

Hiassem. The most high. The king of kings.

The name was sacred. The congregation whispered it in prayer, shouted it in worship, etched it onto their children's hearts before they could walk. They did not know, would never know, that the word was a mockery. A joke played on the faithful by the founders of their own religion.

*Hiassem.*

*Messiah, spelled backwards.*

Cygnus smiled.

"Sir."

A young man appeared in the doorway, bowing slightly. He held a lavishly detailed alb across his outstretched arms; the fabric heavy with crewelwork, the sash and cincture embroidered with the golden image of a lion in full roar.

Lagos. Aspect of the Rabbit. Young, eager, impressionable. Useful.

"I brought these for your annunciation service," Lagos said, his voice low and respectful. "The persona will be given at your ascension."

The vestments of the Chief Cleric. Reserved for only one man in all of Sid Mantelis. After tonight, that man would be Cygnus.

He did not turn from the relic.

"What is the role of religion, Lagos?"

The young clergyman answered without hesitation. "To provide humankind with a law and a system of morals, sir."

"No."

Cygnus let the word hang. Let it fill the sanctum like the final note of a hymn.

"Its chief responsibility, and this is paramount, even to that of morality, is to provide man with a method by which to retain his satisfaction with a world in which he finds it difficult enough to live." He turned a page of the book. Did not look up. "Much of the world is a horror from which humankind may never be released. Suffering from life is like suffering from a disease."

Now he looked up. His eyes found Lagos across the dim room.

"And we are its cure."

He paused.

"We are the true echo of the gods."

Lagos stood very still. "Yes, sir. I understand."

"Good." Cygnus closed the book with care. Rose to his feet. "Because the last Chief Cleric did not. Which is why Hiassem took him from us, to give us better guidance in these most troubling of times."

"Omaha," Lagos replied softly.

Cygnus walked toward the young man and placed his left hand on his shoulder. The gesture was deliberate. Paternal. The kind of touch that binds a student to a mentor, a soldier to a general, a believer to a cause.

"Omaha."

After a moment of pious reflection, he withdrew his hand.

"You've brought my ascension vestments. Would you be so kind as to place them in my chamber?" He turned away, his voice shifting, lighter now, conversational, but with an edge that cut beneath the pleasantries. "Will everyone be at the event? Have we received a full list of attendance?"

The question was not about attendance. Lagos understood this. He was being groomed, and the groomed learn quickly what questions really mean.

"Yes, sir. All the clergy will be present, as you have directed."

"And Trinidad?"

"The witch will not be there, sir."

"Good." Cygnus allowed himself a thin smile. "Seeing as how she is the primary suspect in the Chief Cleric's murder, I don't believe it would be proper etiquette for her to attend my ascension."

He knew, of course, that denying her an invitation would ensure her appearance. A woman like Trinidad did not tolerate exclusion. She would come. She would be seen. And she would be dealt with.

Lagos bowed slightly. "Nor wise on her part, sir. I don't understand why she hasn't been arrested."

"She is a high-ranking member of her order, Lagos. It would not look good to arrest her without evidence." He turned to face the prayer bench. "Even though we know she was responsible, there is still protocol to adhere to. Soon enough, she will be brought to task."

He placed his hand on the relic.

"Have patience. Hiassem works in his own time, and in his own way."

"Omaha."

"Omaha."

With that, Lagos left the sanctum and brought the ascension vestments to the cleric's chamber.

Tonight, Cygnus would dress in the Chief Cleric's alb and cincture. He would take on the aspect of Hiassem and become known as Leo, a name he would carry until his death.

He intended to carry it for a very long time.

Across the city, Trinidad awoke unsteadily from a difficult dream.

She had been moaning, which drew the attention of Riley, who was just outside her bedchamber, sending his thoughts to her mind.

*Are you okay in there?*

"It's alright, Riley. You can come in."

Trinidad sat up in her bed. Her thick mane of silvery blonde hair fell in curly ringlets, framing a beautiful face that might have been perfect if not for the milky translucence that clouded her pale green eyes. Eyes that remained unfocused to the world. Eyes that had never seen it.

Riley came into the room slowly.

He was a small, thin boy with a mop of mousy brown hair and two big brown eyes that peered at everything with quiet intensity. A child of the wastes, arrived one morning as if summoned. Which, in a way, he had been. Trinidad had dreamed of him the night before, had seen him in her vision: a silent boy standing at the edge of a road that led nowhere, waiting for someone who hadn't come yet.

The next day, he appeared.

She was blind. He was mute. Together they had forged a bond that neither of them fully understood, but both were grateful for. She was his voice. He was her eyes. When she needed to see, she looked through his sight. When he needed to be heard, she spoke his meaning aloud.

It was not perfect. Images are imprecise things, filtered through a child's understanding of the world. But it was theirs.

*You were tossing. Bad dreams again.*

"Yes. My dreams were uncomfortable. I'm sure you felt that, too." She slipped on her robe de chambre and padded toward the kitchen, navigating the familiar route with practiced ease. Riley walked beside her, and through his eyes she saw the morning light falling across the worn tiles, the kettle on the stovetop, the two cups waiting on the counter where she had left them the night before.

She filled the kettle and set it to boil. Prepared the cups for tea.

"Now," she said. "One at a time. The most important first."

*The lion.*

Trinidad nodded slowly. "You're concerned about the new Chief Cleric. I am as well."

*You couldn't sleep.*

"No. I couldn't." She reached for the tea leaves, her fingers finding the tin by touch, by memory. "And I know you're hungry."

Through his eyes she saw his gaze drift toward the fruit basket hanging over the sink. She reached up, selected an apple by weight and texture, and began washing it for him.

*The old Chief Cleric. The silver box they put him in.*

"I know. I'm concerned about what happened to him, also. And I'm aware that Cygnus has already made attempts to place the blame on me." She handed Riley the apple. He took it in both hands. "We are moving into desperate times, Riley. We must be cautious. But we cannot allow ourselves to be afraid."

*We have to be brave. Like lions.*

"Yes." She smiled. "We must be very brave. You're a good boy, you know that? No matter what happens to me, you will always be taken care of. I would never let anything happen to you."

*Like a mama duck. Her babies are safe.*

"Yes, Riley. Just like that. You are safe with me." She patted him on the head, tousling his hair with gentle fingers. He leaned into her hand the way children do when they trust completely.

*They call you a witch.*

"I know they do." Her voice was calm. Steady. "But you know that isn't true. They're afraid of what they don't understand. That's all. Don't worry about it."

The tea kettle began to whistle, a high, thin sound that filled the small kitchen. Trinidad poured the hot water into both cups with the careful precision of someone who had measured this distance a thousand times.

"Now," she said. "We need to prepare for the annunciation service tonight."

She had not been invited. She had no intention of staying away.

Trinidad was known among the clergy as the blind prophet.

She was the head of her order, a small group of women who initiated believers into what the clerics of Sid Mantelis officially referred to as a cult. Known commonly as the Coterie Ule, Trinidad's sisterhood was peaceful, contemplative, and had been of great use to the Church in the past. Trinidad's dreams had a persistent habit of coming true. Visions arrived unbidden in her sleep, vivid and terrible and exact, and more often than not they unfolded in the waking world within days.

For this reason alone the order was tolerated. In return, Trinidad provided counsel for the Chief Cleric.

But the old Chief Cleric was dead. And the new one did not want her counsel.

He wanted her silence.

All over the city, people were preparing for the service.

The annunciation of a new Chief Cleric was more than a transfer of power. It was a spectacle, a holy event that drew every soul in Sid Mantelis to the Central Cathedral. But this particular ceremony carried additional significance: the new Chief Cleric had exercised his special right to open the Cathedral to any young votaries who wished to ascend on the same day.

In Sid Mantelis, when a child reached the age of nine, they underwent the rite of ascension. They would give up the aspect of their youth, the animal that had represented them since infancy, and take on the aspect that would carry them into adulthood. Normally this occurred during the Month of Preferment, a season of ceremony and celebration. But whenever a new Chief Cleric was named, he could extend the privilege to any child ready to ascend.

It was considered an extraordinary honor. An auspicious beginning.

For the parents, it meant one thing above all else: getting their children dressed.

"Quit squirming, Mona."

Tamias was trying to wrangle her daughter into an ascension robe that had once belonged to her older brother. The robe was too long, too wide, and, according to Mona, far too itchy.

"But it's itchy, Mama."

"This is the same robe your brother ascended in on his Preferment Day, and he didn't complain nearly as much as you."

"It must have gotten itchier in the closet for all those years." Mona grumbled, pulling at the fabric with undisguised contempt.

From the other room, her son's voice rose in familiar distress.

"Mom, I can't find my bull pendant."

"Did you look in the drawer of your nightstand?"

"Of course I did, Mom. I looked there first."

Tamias waited.

A pause. The sound of a drawer quietly sliding open.

"...Nevermind, I found it."

"It was in the nightstand, wasn't it?"

"Yes, Mom." Bo replied quietly.

"You're welcome."

"Thank you, Mom."

Tamias's children were good kids, if a little stubborn. Both of them had illustrated a headstrong independence from an early age, which suited their mother fine in most situations. In this particular situation, it meant that Mona was still fighting the robe, and Bo had almost certainly put the pendant somewhere else before finding it exactly where his mother said it would be.

Despite her bellyaching, Mona finally shrugged into the garment so her mother could fit it properly. She just needed to hem the sleeves and take off some of the length. A few minutes of work. An hour of complaining.

The annunciation of the new Chief Cleric would be the first time Mona was allowed into the Central Cathedral. Unlike other children her age, she was not excited. She had seen the ornate fixtures of the smaller cathedrals scattered throughout the city, the gilded altars and carved pews, and had already decided that the Central Cathedral would simply be more of the same. Church was a place of restriction and pious contemplation, neither of which appealed to Mona's independent spirit.

She would rather have been outside. Running. Climbing. Investigating things that had nothing to do with animal gods and clergy in masks.

"Quit squirming, you've got a rat's nest growing up here!" Tamias had moved on to her daughter's hair, pulling a brush through tangles that had apparently been accumulating for days.

"But it hurts! Owwwwie! You're ripping out my skull, Mama!"

"Well, if you brushed it more often it wouldn't get this way!"

"I wish I were a bird, so I could fly away from here and never have to brush my hair, or get dressed up, or anything!"

Tamias stopped brushing. Looked at her daughter. At the wild hair. At the defiant eyes. At the girl who would rather be a bird than sit still for five minutes.

"I swear, you and your brother are complete opposites. But you're both stubborn in the same way." She resumed brushing, gentler now. "You're too free-spirited. I hope one day you start taking things seriously."

Mona said nothing. She endured the brush. Stared out the window at the cathedral spires rising above the rooftops, their stone faces carved with animals she was supposed to worship but couldn't bring herself to care about.

Tonight she would ascend into her new aspect.

She didn't know yet that it would be the last ordinary night of her life.

In truth, Bo and his sister could not have been more different.

Bo was a pious and obedient child who planned to follow his father into the Sentinella. He was consumed by the pomp and circumstance of everything ecclesial, and reveled in its glory and magnificence. The ceremonies, the vestments, the hymns that echoed through the cathedral halls; all of it filled him with a devotion that was genuine and unquestioning.

Mona, for her part, was a dreamer. She fascinated herself with thoughts of the outside world and considered Sid Mantelis to be a tomb for the mind. Where Bo saw grandeur, she saw a cage. Where he heard hymns, she heard chains.

That night, Mona would take on the aspect of the bull and become known to all of Sid Mantelis as Tauri from then on.

She had not chosen the name. But it would become hers.

An ornate mahogany staircase led to a long hall that opened into an enormous room.

The ceiling was covered with hand-beaten copper that gave the appearance of sunlight, even in the twilight hours. It was early afternoon now, and between the luminous marble floors and polished canopy above, it was difficult to discern where the room ended and the heavens began. The light seemed to pour from everywhere and nowhere at once, as if the building itself were radiating grace.

Tamias's husband Urso, a member of the Sentinella, was standing guard just outside the Chief Cleric's salon. Through the heavy doors, he could hear Cygnus complaining to the other clerics about the youth ascending on his day of annunciation.

"I don't see why we allow these urchins to steal my limelight! This is my day!" Cygnus called out to the clerics as he dressed. He was a handsome, middle-aged man wearing a garish silken raiment, admiring his own reflection with the self-satisfied gaze of someone who had never doubted his own importance.

"Cygnus, this is an opportunity for you to show favor and extend a small amount of Hiassem's glory onto the young initiates of the faith." Tike, one of the elder clerics, spoke with the patience of a man who had been managing difficult egos for decades. "It's a simple gesture, really. One that bestows deference and illustrates your loyalty to the people."

"It's also a gesture of humility," Lupus added. "And reminds the people that no man is above another."

"I've been thinking about that." Cygnus paused. Turned from the mirror. "Perhaps we should change that."

He walked out of the washroom wearing his persona, the carved wooden mask of the swan obscuring his features. His voice, muffled now behind the mask, carried a calm that was more unsettling than any shout.

"The people crave leadership. They want to believe someone is in charge. What better way to provide that than to reveal ourselves to them?"

Silence.

"Bite your tongue, Cygnus!" Tike's voice broke with genuine alarm. "What you're proposing is heresy. It goes against the very foundation of the Church and the promise of the faith!"

"Perhaps what I am proposing is heresy." Cygnus conceded. He adjusted his vestments with slow, deliberate care. "But when I am Leo, it will be the word of Hiassem himself. No one would dare defy their Lord." He turned to face them. "Now, would they?"

The question hung in the air like a blade.

"Now, if you will excuse me, gentlemen. I must prepare for my annunciation ceremony." He crossed the room and swung open the doors, holding them wide. An invitation to leave. "Your counsel has been most... informative."

The word dripped with disdain.

Tike and Lupus departed in haste. Fear surrounded the appointment of the new Chief Cleric, but they knew there was nothing they could do to prevent it. The rites would be said in a few hours. Once Cygnus donned the persona of Leo, his word would become law, his desires scripture.

They needed help.

They needed Trinidad.

Trinidad's home was a modest little abode hidden behind a wall of lush greenery on the outskirts of the city proper. The windows were open, as they always were, allowing fresh air to circulate within the space. It smelled of dried herbs and old books and the faint sweetness of tea leaves.

Riley saw them first.

Through his eyes, Trinidad perceived two familiar personas waiting patiently on her doorstep: the dog and the wolf. Tike and Lupus. She called to them from within the austere dwelling, inviting them inside.

She and Riley had just finished lunch and were about to begin selecting their garments for the evening when the knock came.

"Tike, Lupus. To what do we owe the pleasure?" Trinidad said in a lively and polite manner, rising from her chair with the fluid ease of someone who had memorized every corner of her home.

The two clerics entered and greeted her with the reverence her position deserved. "Dame Trinidad, thank you for receiving us," Lupus said, extending his hand and grasping hers. Riley looked on from the corner, his brown eyes tracking the visitors with quiet watchfulness.

"The pleasure is truly mine. Rarely do we get such esteemed visitors, and so far from the citadel." Trinidad turned toward the kitchen, her hand trailing the wall with practiced familiarity. "Tea?"

"Oh, don't trouble yourself. We won't be but a minute," Tike said.

"Then sit." She directed them to the sofa with a gesture that was somehow both gracious and commanding. "Tell me."

Lupus began. "It's about Cygnus. Already his language is fringing on heresy, and he hasn't even ascended yet. We fear the decision to appoint him was made in haste."

"We were wondering," Tike continued, leaning forward, "if there was anything you could do, or say, that might caution him against being too rash. Perhaps a testimony. A prediction that might bode... unfavorably." He chose his words carefully. "Something to buy us some time."

"At least until we can talk some sense into him," Lupus finished. "His predecessor trusted you. And Cygnus will, too."

Trinidad sat very still. Riley, from his corner, sent her an image.

*Two men with their hands out. Begging.*

"That all sounds very reasonable, gentlemen," she said. "But I am the chief suspect in his predecessor's murder. I seriously doubt he will listen to me." A pause. "He didn't even invite me to his annunciation ceremony."

"An error," Tike said quickly. "Someone simply overlooked it."

"Besides, there's no evidence to prove you had any involvement with our former Chief Cleric's disappearance," Lupus added.

"Yes. He's only presumed dead."

"You'd be doing us a tremendous favor. Please, attend the ceremony and deliver an omen of sorts. Something he must take seriously."

"Please." Tike's voice dropped. "For all our sakes. For the sake of Sid Mantelis."

Trinidad was unmoved by their pleading. She had already planned to attend, but she didn't fabricate prophecies. She only spoke when the spirit moved her. She could not conjure omens to suit the agendas of frightened men.

But she knew she had to do something. Not only for their sake, but for her own.

"I will do what I can, gentlemen."

"Thank you. Thank you," Tike said, the relief evident in his voice.

They rose to leave. Trinidad remained seated.

"One more thing," she said, as they reached the door.

They turned.

"What does Cygnus plan to do to me?"

The two men looked at each other. Then back to her.

"He plans on arresting you," Tike said. "But we will protect you."

"Yes, we will." Lupus stepped closer, his voice low and earnest. "It'll be a formality. To keep up appearances, to show the laity that he's strong. A deterrent to further criminality."

Trinidad paused. Her clouded eyes stared past them both, past the walls of her little home, past the dome itself, into something they could not see.

"Ok. Well, I trust you gentlemen have my best interests at heart." She smiled wanly. "Omaha."

"Omaha," both men replied in unison.

And with that, they left Trinidad's abode and headed back to their own chambers.

Riley, from the corner, watched them go.

*They're afraid.*

"Yes," Trinidad said softly, after the door had closed. "They should be."

The annunciation ceremony was held in the Central Cathedral.

It was as opulent as Mona had feared.

The nave was a forest of candlelight, hundreds of flames trembling in their sconces, casting the vaulted ceiling into a shifting tapestry of gold and shadow. The great pipe organ, silent now but looming at the far end of the transept like a monument to sound itself, waited with the patience of something that knew its moment would come. The air was thick with incense and the hushed shuffle of bodies finding their seats, the rustling of vestments, the soft percussion of shoes on marble.

Tamias brought her children in through the main entrance and ushered them toward the front pews where the young votaries would sit awaiting their ascension. Mona fidgeted in her brother's old robe, which Tamias had hemmed but could not make less itchy. Bo sat straight-backed beside her, his bull pendant polished and gleaming against his chest, his expression one of reverent anticipation.

Their father, Urso, being a member of the Sentinella, would join them later for the celebration. He was on duty now, stationed somewhere in the cathedral's depths, his bear aspect hidden beneath the black and silver regalia of the Holy Guard. He would watch over the ceremony from the periphery, as he always did: present, protective, unseen.

The ceremony began with the reading of the initiation rites.

Hugo, the officiant, stepped forward in his elephant persona, the carved wooden mask lending his already deep voice a cavernous resonance that filled every corner of the cathedral.

"Blessed Hiassem, we are gathered here today to usher in the ascension of our new Chief Cleric, Cygnus." Hugo's words rose through the nave like a hymn without melody. "He has been chosen by a majority of elders to accept the persona of Leo and take his rightful place as the living representation of your voice for the people."

The congregation held its breath. Even Mona stopped fidgeting.

"Bring him into your wild kingdom and bless his ascension within the tribe of Omaha."

And every soul in the cathedral spoke as one.

"Omaha."

The word filled the space the way water fills a vessel, rising from every direction at once, echoing off the stone walls and copper ceiling until the building itself seemed to vibrate with it. For a moment, just a moment, it was beautiful. It was what faith was supposed to sound like.

"Cygnus, please step forward and receive the persona of Leo, and take your new position as the ordained aspect of our Lord Hiassem."

Cygnus rose from his seat.

He moved through the nave toward the apse in smooth, graceful movements, unhurried, deliberate, as though the aisle had been built for this single walk. Every eye followed him. Every candle seemed to lean toward him as he passed, their flames bending in the current of his passage. He presented himself to Hugo with the quiet certainty of a man who had been rehearsing this moment for far longer than anyone knew.

With his back to the congregation, Cygnus bowed his head. Hugo's hands, steady despite the gravity of the act, lifted the swan persona from his face and set it aside. For a bare moment, the back of Cygnus's head was all that was visible to the laity: dark hair, the pale curve of his neck, a man stripped of his mask and therefore, briefly, human.

Then Hugo raised the golden persona of Leo, the lion, and placed it over his features.

"With the persona of Leo now given to you, and the aspect of the lion represented on your vesture, you are now, and will be until your passing, the voice of Hiassem." Hugo's voice was solemn, measured, each word placed with the care of a mason setting stone. "May you judge fairly and manage honestly."

Cygnus turned.

No. Leo turned.

He faced the congregation wearing the golden mask of the lion, its mane fanning out around the edges in gilt filigree, its expression frozen in a sovereignty that needed no validation. He raised his hands.

"Omaha."

"Omaha!" The congregation responded with joy, with relief, with the desperate gladness of people who wanted to believe that new leadership meant better days. Their voices crashed against the nave like a wave against a seawall, joyful, thunderous, blind.

Hugo led them in a hymn. The pipe organ breathed to life, its first notes low and tentative, then building, swelling, filling the cathedral until the music was indistinguishable from the architecture, until the sound was the stone and the stone was the sound and the people inside were simply vessels through which both passed.

Then Hugo asked the young votaries to come to the transept and have their new aspects bestowed upon them.

Tamias beamed. She squeezed Mona's hand and whispered, "Go on. This is your moment."

Mona rose. She walked toward the transept with the other children, a dozen of them, small bodies in oversized robes, some solemn, some terrified, one picking his nose. She did not look back at her mother. She did not look up at the golden mask of the new Chief Cleric. She looked straight ahead, chin level, as though daring the ceremony to impress her.

One by one, the children received their new aspects. Bo, who had ascended during an earlier Preferment, watched from the pew with the quiet pride of an older brother who had already been through the fire.

When Mona's turn came, Hugo placed his hand on her head and spoke the words that would rename her.

"From this day forward, you will carry the aspect of the bull and be known as Tauri."

She had not chosen the name.

But it would become hers.

After the children had all ascended, Leo rose to close the ceremony.

He stood at the top of the apse steps, the golden lion mask catching the candlelight, and began by thanking everyone for attending, congratulating the youth on their ascension. His voice was warm, generous, paternal. The voice of a shepherd surveying his flock.

Then the warmth left it.

"I am told," Leo said, "that the killer of our beloved former Chief Cleric is among us at this very moment."

Silence.

Not the silence of a pause between words. The silence of a blade drawn in a room full of people. The kind that presses against the eardrums, that makes the heart stutter, that turns a cathedral full of worshipers into a congregation of held breath.

Then the murmurs came. From the back pews first, then rolling forward like a wave through the congregation, hushed and frantic, faces turning to faces, hands finding hands.

"Trinidad." Leo's voice cut through the murmurs like a bell through fog. "Come forth and confess your crime to the people of the community, and let Hiassem proclaim his justice."

He stood unmoved.

Trinidad and Riley rose from their pew. They had come, as she said they would. Uninvited. Unwelcome. Seen.

They crossed the nave together, Trinidad's hand resting on Riley's shoulder, her clouded eyes fixed on nothing and everything, her silver-blonde hair catching the candlelight like something from a painting of a saint. Riley walked beside her with the quiet seriousness of a boy who understood more than he could say.

At the foot of the apse steps, they stopped.

Leo looked down at them.

"Trinidad, Hiassem spoke to me this morning while I read the holy book." His voice carried the careful cadence of a man delivering a verdict he had written before the trial began. "He asked for mercy on your soul, but revealed to me that you were the sole actor in the murder of the former Chief Cleric, and demanded that you be punished for your actions."

Trinidad said nothing. Her face was calm. Still as stone.

"It gives me no pleasure to do this, but I must ask that the Sentinella take you into custody at this time."

At that moment, the Chief Cleric's private guard materialized from the shadows at the edges of the chancel, four large men in black and silver regalia, each bearing the ceremonial halberd of the Sentinella: tall, spiked staff weapons with decorated blades featuring the aspect of the lion in gold gilt and hooked flukes. They surrounded Trinidad and Riley, taking them by the arms.

Trinidad did not resist. She was wordless as they ushered her away, moving through the cathedral with the eerie composure of someone who had already seen this moment in a dream.

The congregation watched. Some wept. Some looked away. Most simply stared, unable to reconcile the blind woman they had known for years with the word that had been spoken over her: murderer.

The cathedral doors closed behind them with a sound like the final note of a requiem.

Riley was consumed with fear.

His small body trembled as the Sentinella led them through corridors of stone, down staircases that grew darker and colder with each turning. He looked to Trinidad for support, his brown eyes wide, searching her face for the reassurance he needed.

She could not see his face. But she could feel him through their bond, the images coming in sharp and frightened: dark walls, torchlight, the gleam of halberds, the heavy boots of the guards on the steps ahead.

She spoke to him through the place where words were unnecessary.

*Don't worry, Riley. We'll be safe.*

An image came back. The guards. Their weapons. The darkness below.

*Urso is a friend of ours. He will make sure nothing happens to us.*

A question, formed as a picture: a large man with kind eyes.

*The bear aspect.*

Recognition. A memory of a face.

*Yes, he's the one. He's Mona's father. Do you remember Mona?*

An image: a girl with wild hair, fidgeting in a pew. And then, layered beneath it, the animal she had just been given.

*The bull.*

*Yes, that's right.* Trinidad's inner voice softened, the way a mother's does when tucking in a child. *Now, come along. We'll be fine. We're just going on a little adventure.*

The cells were dark and damp from disuse.

It was uncommon to have prisoners in Sid Mantelis. The jail rarely saw any detainees, and certainly not for any length of time. Occasionally, the odd overindulgent drunk might end up gracing them with his presence, the distilleries not being strictly forbidden, but it was not the usual occurrence. Theft was rarer still. And murder rarer than that.

So when Trinidad and Riley made their appearance in the dungeon cells beneath the citadel, the jailers were completely unprepared.

To have such a high-profile guest. For such a vicious crime. It was unconscionable. But most of all it was unbelievable. And because it was unbelievable, she was still treated with the same regard and dignity befitting her station. The jailers brought her clean blankets. A candle. Water for Riley. Small courtesies extended to a woman most of them believed was innocent, in a cell most of them believed she did not deserve.

And, of course, she was afforded the protection promised to her by Tike and Lupus.

"Dame Trinidad." Urso's voice was low, barely above a breath, as he leaned close to the bars. "My wife sends her regards, and knows you are innocent. This charge is ridiculous."

"Thank you, Urso." Trinidad placed her hand against the cold iron, as close to contact as the bars would allow. "Tell Tamias I expect to see her and your daughter, Tauri, both soon."

She used the name of Mona's new aspect, the one bestowed only hours ago. A small thing. A way of saying: I was there. I saw. Even blind, I saw.

Urso nodded, though she could not see it. The cell door was locked and he turned to leave, pausing at the foot of the stairs to murmur something to the jailer. A quiet word. An instruction. The jailer nodded once.

Then Urso climbed the steps and was gone.

The morning sun found itself blanketed in red clouds.

Inside the citadel, the new Chief Cleric prepared for his first duties since his ascension. After a hearty breakfast, he commanded Lagos to have the Sentinella bring the prisoner to him for adjudication. The quicker he passed judgment on the witch, the quicker he could begin acting unopposed, reshaping the customs and practices of the religion of Omaha to suit his vision.

Trinidad and Riley were led to the solar by four members of the Sentinella, each man a monument of black and silver, their halberds held upright like the bars of a moving cage. As they climbed the marble staircase and crossed the promenade, Urso kept close. His protective gaze tracked the pair with the intensity of a man who understood that protection, in Sid Mantelis, was something you provided at the cost of your own safety.

He hoped it would be enough.

Once inside the solar, Leo instructed the guards to wait outside the doors.

"You can remove the shackles. I'll be fine." His voice was casual, almost pleasant. "Raven doesn't mean me any harm, do you, my dear? Thank you, gentlemen."

The doors closed. The Sentinella took their positions on the opposite side.

Trinidad stood very still in the center of the room. Riley pressed close to her side. Through his eyes she perceived the solar: a grand chamber flooded with morning light from tall windows, tapestries on the walls depicting animal gods in postures of benediction, a heavy desk covered with scrolls and the golden persona of Leo set upon it like a crown awaiting its king.

Leo himself was seated behind the desk. Unmasked. Smiling.

"My name is Trinidad," she said. "You know that."

"Oh, but that name is not the name of your aspect, chosen by Hiassem." Leo's voice was silk over steel. "He would have me speak to you properly in order to pass judgment."

He rose. Walked around the desk. Stood before her.

"Tell me, Raven. Did you really think you could oppose me openly without any retribution?"

"I don't know what you mean."

"That little prophecy of yours."

The room was silent. The light from the windows made long rectangles across the marble floor. Riley, through their bond, sent Trinidad an image: a man with no mask standing too close, his eyes cold, his mouth smiling.

*Dangerous.*

"My allegiance is to the truth," Trinidad said.

"We'll see about that."

Leo leaned in. Close enough that she could feel the warmth of his breath, close enough that even without sight she knew exactly how near he stood.

"You will die a traitor's death. I'll see to it." His voice dropped to a register that was no longer casual, no longer pleasant, no longer the voice of a cleric or a politician or a man of faith. It was something older than all of those things. Something patient. Almost inhuman.

"You," he said. "And the boy."
